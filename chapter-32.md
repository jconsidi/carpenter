# Chapter 32 — The Coin

The coin arrived with a clean man.

Clean men were not always dangerous, but dangerous men often understood the uses of cleanliness.
Dust told stories.
Sweat confessed labor.
Torn cloth admitted urgency.
Clean hands could imply innocence, leisure, or the ability to make other hands carry blood.

The man’s name was Barak.

He came with a coin, no object he admitted was broken, and two men who stood far enough behind him to pretend they were not a delegation.

A woman named Naama saw him first at the door.

She had become useful around routes, thresholds, and the refusal of vague purpose.

“Purpose?”

Barak smiled.

“To ask a question.”

Mara, inside, said, “Laundry.”

Naama ignored her, mostly.

“Object?”

Barak lifted the coin between finger and thumb.

Yosef looked up from the table leg he was repairing.

Maryam’s face changed.

Daniel did not move.

Coins were small doors.
They opened into taxes, debt, empire, hunger, sacrifice, wages, soldiers, roads, bribes, temple exchange, widow’s jars, and the strange human habit of treating stamped metal as if it were less fictional than mercy.

Barak stepped inside only after Naama permitted it.

He placed the coin on the table.

“Is it lawful,” he asked, “to give tribute to Caesar?”

Natan, who happened to be present with a broken tablet frame and bad timing, inhaled sharply.

Mara muttered, “That is not a question.
That is a pit wearing sandals.”

Daniel looked at the coin.

A face stamped into metal.
Foreign authority made portable.
The emperor’s image traveling through hands that had never seen him and bodies he could still command.

He did not answer.

Good.

Maryam asked, “Who pays when men speak bravely about coins?”

Barak’s smile tightened.

“I ask about law.”

Maryam said, “I ask about cost.”

Yosef picked up the coin only after looking at Daniel, then turned it in his palm.

“Stamped face,” he said.

Barak nodded.

“Caesar’s.”

“Whose grain bought it?”

Barak blinked.

“Pardon?”

Naama said, “Whose labor earned it?”

Hanina, who had arrived because coins attracted hunger like flies, said, “Whose onions did it remove before becoming shiny?”

Mara said, “Whose body does it threaten if someone answers wrong?”

The two men behind Barak shifted.

Good.

Daniel finally spoke.

“If I say do not pay, who is punished first?”

Barak looked at him.

“If I say pay, who uses my words to bless the taking?”

No answer.

Daniel touched the coin without lifting it.

“The face on the coin belongs to the man who claims it.
The hands that earned it do not.
If Rome demands stamped metal, stamped metal may go.
Do not give Rome your neighbor and call it obedience.”

Barak watched him.

“And what belongs to God?”

Daniel hated that he had expected the follow-up.

He looked around the room: Maryam, Yosef, Mara, Naama, Hanina, Natan, Erel in the doorway, a child outside listening badly, Barak’s men pretending not to learn.

“The hungry bear God’s image before coins bear Caesar’s,” Daniel said.
“If your question makes you see the coin and not the hungry, the question has already lied.”

Natan did not write.

Good.

Barak did not like the answer.

This was encouraging.

He tried again.

“So pay?”

Daniel said, “Do not use me to avoid deciding what your household does with the hungry after Rome takes coin.”

“So resist?”

“Do not use me to make courage for which poorer people pay.”

Mara whispered, “Excellent non-answer.”

Maryam said, “Incomplete answer.”

Also true.

The room worked on it.

By evening, the coin had become a teaching object.

What does the coin claim?

Metal.

Tax.

Image.

Fear.

What does it not own?

Bodies.

Worship.

Neighbor.

Labor’s dignity.

Food already owed.

Truth.

The answer that traveled was predictably worse.

Yeshua said pay Rome.

Yeshua said refuse Rome.

Yeshua said coins have faces.

Yeshua said Caesar owns metal but not people.

Yeshua said hungry faces matter more than Caesar’s face.

The last version was closest and most dangerous.

Dalia sent a message from the lake: If Rome wants fish tax, send Caesar nets and see if he can row.

Mara wanted to copy that publicly.

Maryam forbade it.

Barak returned the next day, displeased, with a broken scale.

One pan hung lower even when empty.

Erel saw it first.

“It lies before grain.”

Everyone turned.

Good boy.

Barak set the scale down.

“If measures lie,” he said, “who decides the true one?”

Daniel looked at the coin beside the scale.

“First, everyone measured by it gets to know it lied.”

Yosef nodded.

Maryam asked, “Who has been measured?”

Barak did not answer.

There it was.

A question about coins had become a question about false measures because all clean traps hid dirt somewhere.

By the end of the week, market disputes reopened.
Men who preferred abstract law regretted bringing objects.
Women arrived with accounts.
Workers arrived with rates.
A widow brought three coins and a story no coin could hold.

Rome did not vanish.

Taxes did not end.

The scale was repaired.

The false records were not.

That would take longer.

Daniel added to the list:

Stamped metal may go to stamped men.

Bodies do not.

Mara added:

If Caesar wants onions, he must peel them himself.

Maryam crossed out must and wrote should not receive them.

Less satisfying.

More useful.

The coin went into the chest of things not to worship.

On its label, Yosef wrote:

Ask what the question wants to make invisible.
