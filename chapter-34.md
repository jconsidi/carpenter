# Chapter 34 — Friends Who Misunderstand

The feather did not survive the road home.

This was expected and still sad.

It had come from Dinah’s bird, the one that had left the cage badly but away, and Mara had carried it with unusual care for almost half a day before wind, sweat, road dust, and the general hostility of reality reduced it from feather to suggestion.

By the time they reached familiar paths, it had lost half its barbs and acquired a bend that made it look accusatory.

Mara held it up.

“This bird failed structurally.”

Daniel looked at it.

“It flew.”

“Briefly and poorly.”

“Still.”

She frowned.

“Do we keep it?”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

“Open is not the same as free.”

“I know the line.
I was there.”

“Then because it reminds us badly.”

Mara considered.

“I like badly.”

So the damaged feather went into the box of suspicious usefulness, after Sima declared it insufficiently bird-like and therefore safe from worship.

The fair weight from Azriel went beside it.

The table wedge from Naama’s repaired table went beside the weight.

The broken lamp strap went near the storm rope.

The copied rate board did not fit in any box and therefore leaned against the wall, where Ruth immediately complained that even objects from Jerusalem arrived with posture.

Home received them without ceremony.

This helped.

The beam still held.
The channels needed clearing.
Hanina had kept the bread routes alive and had several criticisms ready.
Erel’s mother had sent word that he was becoming useful and therefore more irritating.
Yair had two bad nights and one good morning.
Asa’s brake had slipped again.
Dalia sent fish with the message: Jerusalem did not eat you.
Suspicious.

Life had not waited for Jerusalem to finish.

Good.

Daniel slept one night as if his body had become clay.

The next morning, Andrai arrived.

Not alone.

This was the first warning.

He came with Shimon, Yonah, Berek on a crutch, Mattai, and three younger lake men carrying rope, a small broken oarlock, dried fish, and the air of people who had discussed something on the road and arrived already partly convinced.

Dalia did not come.

This was the second warning.

She sent a note.

If they become dramatic, feed them first and remove sharp objects.

Maryam read it and immediately ordered food.

Shimon objected.

“We ate.”

Maryam looked at him.

He sat.

Good.

Andrai watched Daniel while pretending not to.
He looked thinner than before the storm, not physically perhaps, but in the way people looked after almost dying and discovering the world had not become clear afterward.

Berek leaned on his crutch and looked embarrassed by needing it.

Yonah looked at the rope.

Mattai looked at everyone as if they were knots he might later tighten.

They ate.

Not enough to solve everything.

Enough to prevent some stupidity.

Then Maryam said, “Work?”

Andrai placed the oarlock on the table.

“Broken.”

Yosef inspected it.

Erel leaned close.

Daniel waited.

Andrai said, “Also we have a question.”

“Of course,” Mara said.

Shimon said, “We have a proposal.”

Dalia’s note had been accurate.

Daniel looked at Andrai, not Shimon.

“What broke?”

Andrai touched the oarlock.

“This.
Also the storm rack needs another section.
Also we think the lake should have a proper set of practices.
Ropes, lamps, shore teams, watchers, weather calls, boys trained, women at landing, Dalia shouting officially.”

“All good,” Daniel said carefully.

Andrai nodded.

“And we want you to help establish it.”

“That seems reasonable.”

Shimon leaned forward.

“And bless it.”

There it was.

Mara closed her eyes.

Maryam’s face did not change.

Yosef continued inspecting the oarlock as if men did not periodically attempt to overload language.

Daniel said, “What do you mean by bless?”

Shimon looked impatient.

“You know.
Speak over it.
Say it is good.
Give it weight so men obey.”

Dalia had sent them without herself because she knew.

Daniel looked at Andrai.

Andrai did not meet his eyes quickly enough.

So he was not innocent.

Berek said, “After the storm, some men only obey when they think the rule comes from more than Dalia.”

Mara said, “Then those men should be tied to the dock until wisdom grows.”

Shimon looked interested.

Maryam said, “Mara.”

“What? It is lake-safe.”

Andrai said, “We know Dalia is right.
But men who resist her may listen if the practices are named by you.”

Daniel felt the old net close softly.

Not malicious.

Practical.

That made it worse.

“You want to use me to make men obey what Dalia already knows.”

Shimon said, “Yes.”

At least he was honest.

Andrai said, “Not use.
Ask.”

“Ask to use.”

He winced.

Good.

Yonah spoke for the first time.

“If they obey, fewer die.”

Silence.

There.

The hard part.

Not vanity only.
Lives.

Daniel rubbed his face.

Maryam asked, “Can they refuse?”

Andrai said, “Who?”

“The men you want to bind with his blessing.”

Shimon frowned.

“It is safety.”

Maryam waited.

Berek said, “Safety can still become a rope.”

Mattai grunted approval.

Good.

Yosef set the oarlock down.

“It failed because load came at a bad angle repeatedly.
Binding tighter would have cracked the frame.”

Shimon sighed.

“I hate wood lessons.”

“Then stop needing them,” Mara said.

Daniel looked at the oarlock.

“If Dalia’s practice is right, it should be established by those who carry the lake.
If I speak over it, some will obey the sound and not understand the load.
Then when I am absent, they wait.”

Andrai said, “Some already wait.”

“Yes.
That is the danger.”

Shimon said, “So you refuse to help.”

“No.”

“Then bless it.”

“No.”

Shimon threw up his hands.

“Your no and yes are worse than fish.”

Maryam said, “Perhaps ask what help he will give.”

Shimon looked unconvinced but turned to Daniel.

Daniel said, “I will come to the lake if needed.
I will help repair the storm rack.
I will help ask what broke.
I will say publicly that Dalia’s weather calls are to be obeyed because she knows the lake and because the practices are sound.
I will not make obedience depend on me.”

Andrai looked up.

“That may be enough.”

“It will anger some.”

“Good,” Mara said.

Berek looked at his leg.

“Say also that men who ignore Dalia owe work to those who wait.”

Dalia would like that.

Daniel smiled faintly.

“Yes.”

Shimon muttered, “Not a blessing.”

Mattai said, “Better.
Blessings wash off.
Work blisters.”

Everyone looked at him.

Mattai shrugged.

“Hands remember.”

Daniel pointed at him.

“That goes.”

Mara said, “With his name.”

Mattai looked horrified.

“No.”

Too late.

---

The lake men misunderstood help because they needed survival.

Natan misunderstood it because he needed language.

He arrived the next day with two students, a repaired shelf support, a writing case, and the expression of a man who had prepared an honest question so carefully that it had become dangerous anyway.

Maryam made them eat.

Natan said they had.

Maryam looked at the younger student, who immediately confessed they had eaten badly and too early.

Maryam fed them.

Then Natan placed the writing case down.

It was not broken.

Mara pointed.

“Fraud.”

Natan said, “The object is a copy.”

He opened it.

Inside were several tablets and scraps.
Lines from the hills.
Bread rules.
Storm sayings.
Name conditions.
Jerusalem map warnings.
Table notes.
Corrections.
Some in Natan’s hand, some copied by students, some with names attached, some not.

Daniel felt dread.

Natan said quickly, “This is not for public distribution.”

“Yet,” Mara said.

He winced.

“Yes.
That is the question.”

Maryam’s eyes sharpened.

Natan continued, “The words are already traveling badly.
If we do not preserve conditions, others will preserve only the shine.
I propose a collection for rooms.
Not for crowds.
For teachers, household heads, women carrying tables, lake crews, those who make marks.
A way to carry the limp.”

Daniel stared at the scraps.

The story limping in writing.

Useful.

Terrifying.

Yosef asked, “Who controls it?”

Good.

Natan looked down.

“That is why I brought it.”

“Who controls it now?”

“I do.”

Mara said, “Bad.”

“Yes.”

Progress.

Natan said, “I know.
That is why it cannot remain mine.”

Daniel looked at Maryam.

She did not answer for him.

Natan continued, “If written conditions are not kept, Barak and others will write cleaner versions.
If I keep it, I become gate.
If we do not write, memory becomes gate.
I do not know how to do this without harm.”

Good.

Damn.

A useful person becoming more dangerous by knowing danger.

Maryam asked, “Who is erased in your copies?”

Natan flinched.

He had expected this and still flinched.

He opened one tablet.

“I began adding names.”

“Began,” Mara said.

“Yes.”

Maryam read.

“Maryam for rooms and shame.
Hanina for hunger.
Dalia for shore work.
Ruth for spectators.
Mara for marks and turning around.
Yosef for load.
Erel for learning before pride hardens.
Asa for fit.
Tobiah for small repairs.
Yair for names before wounds.
Naama for routes.
Tirzah for grain.
Dinah for open is not free.
Avital for death is grief before meaning.”

Her voice changed at Avital.

Good.

Natan added, “And others.
Not enough.”

“No,” Maryam said.

Natan nodded.

Daniel said, “What do you want from me?”

Natan looked at him.

“Permission.”

“No.”

Natan’s face fell.

Daniel continued, “Not from me alone.”

Natan breathed.

Good.

“You need permission from those named where possible.
Or their households.
And from those whose words you carry.
And a way to correct.
And a way to destroy a copy that becomes weapon.”

Natan nodded quickly.

“Too quickly,” Mara said.

He stopped nodding.

Yosef said, “Write the repair instructions on top, not after.”

Natan looked at him.

“Meaning?”

“Conditions first.
Sayings after.
If someone copies only the saying, it breaks.”

Maryam said, “And food before reading in rooms where hunger is present.”

Hanina, arriving exactly on cue with onions, said, “And no reading my hunger words without actual food nearby.”

Natan wrote.

Mara said, “And no copying me solemnly.”

“That may be difficult.”

“Then fail better.”

Daniel looked at the case.

“A collection can become scripture.”

The room stilled.

Natan swallowed.

“Yes.”

“Scripture can become table.”

“Yes.”

“Table can become machine.”

“Yes.”

Maryam said, “And not writing can become erasure.”

Also yes.

There were no clean exits.

Daniel said, “Then begin with a warning.”

Natan took a fresh scrap.

Daniel spoke slowly.

“These words are tools from rooms.
If you remove the room, inspect the tool before use.
If a saying makes you stop seeing people, put it down.
If a name becomes larger than the hungry, sick, grieving, indebted, frightened, or tired, you have copied wrongly.
If the words make you powerful, ask who became smaller.”

Mara whispered, “Shiny.”

Daniel nodded.

“If the words shine, rub dirt on them.”

Natan looked up.

“Literally?”

Mara said, “Sometimes.”

They left it ambiguous.

Natan’s collection began that day.

Not because Daniel approved.

Because the alternative had already begun without them.

---

Erel misunderstood differently.

He misunderstood by wanting to be ready.

This was harder to refuse.

After the lake men and Natan left, Erel stayed late, sanding pegs that had no need of sanding.
Daniel recognized this because Erel had done it before asking whether he was a follower.

“What is broken?”

Erel looked up.

“Maybe me.”

Daniel set down his tool immediately.

“No.
Begin again.”

Erel smiled faintly.

“I know.
But perhaps.”

Daniel waited.

Erel said, “Jerusalem sounded large.”

“It was.”

“And dangerous.”

“Yes.”

“And you will go again.”

Probably.

“Yes.”

“And there may be worse.”

Daniel said nothing.

Erel continued, “I want to come.”

“No.”

The answer came too fast.

Erel’s face closed.

Daniel cursed inwardly.

Maryam, passing by the doorway, paused but did not enter.

Good.

Daniel tried again.

“No as first answer.
Not final structure.”

Erel nodded stiffly.

Good.

“Why do you want to come?” Daniel asked.

“To help.”

“And?”

Erel looked away.

“To not be left behind.”

There.

Honest.

Hard.

“And?”

“To be where it matters.”

Daniel closed his eyes.

That one was dangerous.

“To matter,” Daniel said.

Erel’s jaw tightened.

“Yes.”

Daniel remembered his own desire to be necessary and felt old shame stir.

He said, “Jerusalem is not more real than here.”

Erel looked unconvinced.

“People act like it is.”

“Yes.”

“And the story gathers there.”

“Yes.”

“And if danger comes there—”

“You think being near danger proves you belong to the work.”

Erel looked down.

Not denial.

Good.

Daniel said, “Some danger needs hands.
Some danger needs fewer bodies in the room.”

“I can learn.”

“Yes.”

“Then teach me.”

Daniel almost said he was.

But Erel was asking for something specific: preparation for public conflict, not just repair.

Daniel called Maryam in.

Erel looked embarrassed.

Maryam sat.

Good.

Yosef came too, because tool conversations had gravity.

Mara arrived because privacy was a rumor.

Erel said, “I want to be ready.”

Yosef asked, “For what work?”

Erel struggled.

“Carrying messages.
Holding ropes.
Watching exits.
Feeding people.
Helping injured.
Taking objects away if crowds grow.
Not being stupid.”

Mara said, “Too broad.
Especially last.”

Maryam said, “Which of those can be practiced here?”

Erel blinked.

“All?”

Good.

So they made Erel’s preparation local.

Messages through bread routes.
Crowd exit practice at room gatherings.
Injury carrying with Asa’s board and Berek’s crutch.
Rope handling with lake men when they came.
Food before questions.
Listening for who is being ignored.
Knowing when not to enter.
Knowing when to leave.
Knowing how to say, “I am not needed here,” without shame.

That last one hurt him.

Good.

Daniel said, “If you learn only how to enter important rooms, you become danger.
Learn how to leave them.”

Erel nodded slowly.

Mara added, “And how to be bored usefully.”

Erel looked at her.

“That is a skill?”

“The rarest.”

Daniel smiled.

Erel would not come to Jerusalem next time by desire alone.

He accepted it.

Not happily.

Acceptance without happiness counted.

The misunderstanding that hurt most came from Maryam.

Not because she was wrong.

Because she was almost right.

It happened three evenings after Natan’s collection began.
The house was quieter than usual.
Mara had gone to help Sima ruin three marks by improving them.
Yosef was with Erel.
Hanina had left onions and warnings.
Ruth had insulted the weather and gone home.
Daniel was sorting Jerusalem objects for the box.

Maryam watched him handle the fair weight.

“You will go back.”

He did not ask where.

“Yes.”

“Soon?”

“I don’t know.”

“But yes.”

“Yes.”

She nodded.

He placed the fair weight beside the table wedge.

Maryam said, “Part of me wants to hide the sandals.”

He smiled faintly.

“That would delay me badly.”

“I know.”

Silence.

Then she said, “I also want to tell you to go before fear makes the house smaller.”

He looked at her.

That was the misunderstanding.

Or maybe not.

She saw him.
She saw fear.
She saw avoidance before he named it.
She also saw the work calling.
But she might be sending him because she feared holding him too tightly.
Because mothers, too, could overcorrect.
Because love could become theft, and fear of theft could become abandonment.

He said carefully, “Do you want me to go?”

Maryam closed her eyes.

“No.”

Good.

Truth first.

“Do you think I should?”

“I think if the work is there and you refuse only because I will grieve, you make my grief master.”

Daniel sat back.

“And if I go because I fear making your grief master?”

She opened her eyes.

“Then my grief is still master.”

Yes.

There.

Fear was endlessly creative.

Maryam’s voice softened.

“I do not know.”

Daniel felt relieved and frightened.

She said it again.

“I do not know.”

He nodded.

“Good.”

She smiled weakly.

“Good?”

“Better than if you knew for both of us.”

She touched the fair weight.

“I misunderstood something.”

“What?”

“I thought come home could be enough structure.”

“It is.”

“No.
It is one structure.
Not enough.”

Ah.

Yes.

Come home.
Return.
Powerful.
True.
But not sufficient if home became gravitational excuse or if return was impossible or if work required going where home could not protect him.

Maryam said, “I wanted home to be a rope.”

“It is.”

“It can also be a net.”

He swallowed.

Yes.

She looked at him with pain.

“If I say come home, you must hear love.
Not command.
Not trap.
Not proof that leaving is betrayal.”

He could not speak.

She continued, “And if you say you must go, I must hear work.
Not rejection.
Not desire for death.
Not the story stealing you before you choose.”

They sat with that.

A correction inside love.

Hardest kind.

Daniel picked up the writing tool and added to the list:

Home is rope, not net.

Maryam read it.

Then added:

Return can mean coming back or carrying love forward.

He looked at her.

“Too beautiful.”

“Then make it limp.”

He added:

Ask who uses return to control whom.

Maryam nodded.

Better.

Worse.

True.

Yosef misunderstood by trusting tools.

This was rare enough to be notable.

He had begun making traveling kits.

Quietly.

Too quietly.

Small rolls: cord, knife, cloth, pegs, awl, wedge, marked measuring cord, charcoal, spare strap, folded warning scraps, tiny salt packet because Hanina insisted food and metaphor both needed seasoning.
Kits for Daniel, himself, Erel if allowed, Mara because she stole tools anyway, Maryam because she used tools socially even when not physically.

Daniel watched him lay out three rolls.

“You are preparing for departure.”

Yosef did not look up.

“Yes.”

“Soon?”

“When needed.”

Daniel sat.

“Do you think tools are enough?”

Yosef paused.

There.

Not a mistake exactly.

A reliance.

“No.”

“But?”

Yosef tied a cord around one roll.

“If I can prepare tools, I can do something.”

Daniel understood.

Preparation as prayer for men who distrusted decorative prayer.

Yosef said, “I cannot prepare Jerusalem.
I cannot prepare Rome.
I cannot prepare men like Barak.
I can prepare cord.”

Daniel looked at the rolls.

Good.

Not enough.

Maybe enough for today.

He said, “Then write what each tool cannot do.”

Yosef looked at him.

Daniel continued, “On the roll.
Cord cannot make wisdom.
Knife cannot decide justice.
Cloth cannot heal alone.
Peg cannot hold rotten wood.
Measuring cord cannot measure grief.”

Yosef stared.

Then smiled faintly.

“That is annoying.”

“Yes.”

“Good.”

They wrote the limits on scraps tucked into each roll.

Cord: holds if tied to something sound.
Can also bind.

Knife: cuts rope, food, cloth.
Does not decide when to cut.

Cloth: covers, warms, cleans.
Can also hide.

Peg: holds pieces only if wood can bear.

Charcoal: marks.
Marks fade and can be misread.

Salt: preserves, flavors.
Does not make spoiled food good.

Mara later added to her roll:

Insults: useful for puncturing shine.
Not substitute for plan.

Excellent.

Yosef accepted that tools needed limits written near them.

Daniel accepted that preparing cord was not denial.

They both improved slightly.

Mara misunderstood by thinking she did not.

This was perhaps her most Mara-like failure.

She had become very good at spotting shiny story, public appetite, goat-shaped foolishness, and Daniel’s face when old memory leaned too close.
This made her overconfident in her ability to stand outside the story.

She discovered otherwise when Sima called her teacher.

Sima did it casually, while holding a copied mark board and asking whether the bread eye should look outward or sideways when used near ovens versus routes.

“Teacher, should the eye—”

Mara dropped the board.

Everyone looked.

Sima looked stricken.

“I didn’t mean—”

“No,” Mara said.

Too fast.

Daniel turned.

Maryam, near the oven, watched.

Sima’s lip trembled.

Mara saw and softened badly.

“I mean, no.
I am not— That is not— Do not call me that.”

Sima nodded too many times.

“Sorry.”

Mara fled.

This was the technical term.

Daniel found her near the lower channel, stabbing mud with a stick.

“Go away.”

“No.”

“I can leave.”

“Yes.
I can follow.”

“That violates many rules.”

“I brought no title.”

She stabbed the mud again.

Daniel sat on a stone.

“Sima did not mean harm.”

“I know.”

“She sees you teaching.”

“I know.”

“Then?”

Mara turned on him.

“I told you witnesses stand around while other people become important.”

“Yes.”

“Teacher is worse.”

“Why?”

“Because then they stay.”

Ah.

Daniel waited.

She looked toward the village.

“If Sima calls me teacher, then when I leave, it matters.”

“Yes.”

“If I stay, it matters.”

“Yes.”

“If she copies me badly, it matters.”

“Yes.”

“If she becomes sharp in ways that hurt people, it is partly me.”

“Yes.”

Mara glared.

“You are supposed to argue.”

“No.
You are correct.”

She looked betrayed.

Daniel continued, “That is authority.
Weight other people feel when you move.”

“I hate Maryam’s sentences.”

“Everyone does eventually.”

Mara sat hard on the ground.

“I wanted to be someone who can leave.”

“You are.”

“Teachers cannot.”

“Bad teachers cannot.
Good ones teach leaving too.”

She threw the stick at him.

It missed.

Good.

He said, “You can tell Sima what the word means if she uses it.”

“I do not know what it means.”

“Then begin there.”

She groaned.

Later, she returned to Sima and said, “If you call me teacher, it means you may also tell me when my mark is bad, you may leave, you may ask Maryam instead, you may refuse goats as theology, and you must not copy my insults until you know who they protect.”

Sima considered.

“Can I still call you teacher?”

Mara closed her eyes.

“Only when working.”

Sima nodded.

“Teacher, the bread eye is wrong.”

Mara looked at Daniel across the courtyard as if blaming him for civilization.

Daniel smiled.

Mara had misunderstood herself.

Good.

It made her more dangerous and more honest.

---

The friends around Daniel began to fracture not by leaving, but by meaning different things when they stayed.

Andrai wanted practices that saved lives, and sometimes he wanted Daniel’s name to force men to obey what was already true.

Natan wanted words that preserved conditions, and sometimes he wanted writing to make uncertainty manageable.

Erel wanted readiness, and sometimes he wanted danger to prove belonging.

Maryam wanted Daniel home, and sometimes she feared home could become theft.

Yosef wanted tools prepared, and sometimes tools were prayers he did not want to name.

Mara wanted to remain free, and sometimes freedom was hiding from the weight she already had.

Hanina wanted hunger fed, and sometimes feeding became governance she resented but did well.

Ruth wanted stupidity named, and sometimes naming became a pleasure she had to watch.

Dalia wanted men alive, and sometimes authority had to wear another person’s sound to reach fools.

Shifra wanted strategy, and sometimes strategy approached control with clean hands.

Elior wanted to help without praise, and sometimes still checked whether anyone saw his humility.

Natan’s students wanted sayings, and sometimes conditions bored them.

Children wanted marks, and sometimes marks became games before they became safeguards.

No one understood fully.

This was not failure.

It was what happened when work became shared.

Daniel wrote that down.

Shared work creates shared misunderstanding.

Then:

Correction is not interruption of the work.
It is part of the work.

Then, after thought:

If everyone agrees too quickly, inspect the wedge.

That one came from Naama’s table.

Good.

---

The summons from Jerusalem came on the fifth morning after the friends began correcting themselves.

Not official.

Worse.

Polite.

A letter from Shifra, carried by Joazar, Barak’s quieter companion, along with a small object wrapped in cloth.

Joazar arrived dusty, tired, and without performance.
He greeted Maryam first, then Yosef, then Daniel, then Mara, which proved either intelligence or coaching.

Mara said, “You may enter.”

Daniel raised an eyebrow.

She whispered, “He learned the door.”

Good.

The object was a broken seal.

Not a seal animal, which Mara clarified before anyone could enjoy confusion.
A clay seal from a storage document, cracked so the mark could no longer be read fully.
The impression showed part of an official sign, part of a private mark, and a break through the section that mattered.

Yosef inspected the clay.

“Cannot repair.”

Joazar nodded.

“No.
But it can show tampering.”

Maryam read Shifra’s letter.

Her face changed.

She handed it to Daniel.

Barak and several others had begun circulating a cleaner statement in Jerusalem:

The Galilean refuses holy order.
The Galilean disrupts offerings.
The Galilean teaches women and children to judge the temple.
The Galilean denies the promised one while gathering followers.
The Galilean hides his claim.

Below, Shifra had written:

He makes your story run.
Come if you intend to make it limp.
If not, stay away and we will do what we can.

Daniel read it twice.

Mara read over his shoulder and became very quiet.

Yosef took the broken seal.

“Tampered story,” he said.

“Yes,” Joazar answered.

Daniel looked at him.

“Why bring this?”

Joazar hesitated.

“Because I helped Barak speak once.”

Honest.

Good.

“And?”

“And I do not want my silence to seal this.”

Maryam nodded.

“You bring witness.”

“Yes.”

Daniel sat.

Jerusalem again.

Tables again.

Barak again.

Story running clean.

Maryam did not say go.

Yosef did not prepare aloud.

Mara looked at him with the expression of someone ready to consult before martyrdom and after breakfast.

Erel appeared at the doorway, having heard enough to know danger had entered.

Daniel looked at the broken seal.

A mark that once authenticated now unreadable because someone broke it.

What is broken?

The story's seal.

What can be done?

Go? Send answer? Send Natan? Send Shifra more support? Public correction? Private room? Refuse summons? Delay? Prepare.

What can it bear?

Not panic.
Not solitary heroics.
Not avoidance.

Who carries result?

All.

Maryam said, “Food first.”

Of course.

They fed Joazar.

They fed themselves.

Then they gathered the room.

---

The argument lasted all day.

This was good.

Fast agreement would have been suspicious.

Natan wanted to go immediately and answer in writing.

Mara said writing was how literate men made cages out of air.

Natan conceded partially and still wanted documents.

Yosef wanted to know who would go, where they would stay, what objects they would carry, and which routes were safest.

Maryam wanted to know whether going would help those harmed by Barak’s statement or only draw more attention.

Hanina wanted to know who would maintain bread routes if Maryam went.

Ruth wanted to go because she distrusted Jerusalem unsupervised.

Everyone agreed and refused.

Dalia’s message arrived late through a lake trader, because apparently rumor had outrun weather:

If going to city, bring people who can shout, count, feed, and carry.
Do not bring Shimon unless you want a second problem.

Helpful.

Shifra’s letter had said come if you intend to make it limp.

Mara liked that too much.

Daniel did not decide until evening.

He stood in the courtyard with the broken seal in hand.

Everyone watched.

Too many.

He sat down.

Better.

“I think we go,” he said.

Maryam closed her eyes.

Yosef nodded once.

Mara looked at the ground.

Daniel continued, “Not to answer Barak’s story with a cleaner one.
Not to claim title.
Not to overturn tables for clarity.
