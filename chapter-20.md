# Chapter 20 — Forty Days

Daniel did not go to the wilderness because the story said wilderness came next.

This was important.

He repeated it often enough that everyone stopped believing the repetition had no purpose.

He went because the house had become too full of answers.

Not bad answers.
That would have been easier.
Bad answers could be refused.
The dangerous ones were good enough to use.
Fear needs a channel.
Water can mark a turn but cannot walk it.
Truth needs carriers.
Bring work.
Return.
A true metaphor still needs clearing.
Every answer, once spoken, gathered people.
Every phrase became a tool someone wanted to borrow, sharpen, misuse, praise, sell, or hang above a doorway until it stopped cutting.

Daniel had thought the river would be the dangerous threshold.

It had been.

He had not understood that thresholds also let things in.

After the river, people came with questions that had survived first telling.
They were better questions than before.
More careful.
Less hungry for spectacle.
This was worse, because good questions deserved work.

A man asked how to repair an apology when the person wronged refused to hear it.

A woman asked whether returning stolen grain mattered if the person who stole it had children.

A boy asked if fear needed a channel, what anger needed.

Mara said, "A fence."

Maryam said, "A witness."

Yosef said, "Work before speech."

Daniel had said, "Space."

That answer had stayed with him.

Space.

He had too little of it.

The house held Maryam, Yosef, tools, cloth, goat boards, repair requests, memory, names, rumor, thresholds, and the old story sitting in the corner like an uninvited elder no one could insult directly.
Outside the house, the village held need.
Beyond the village, the river held questions.
Beyond the river, the wilderness held absence.

Absence sounded useful.

This worried him.

One should distrust anything that sounded useful while also allowing escape.

When he told Maryam, she did not answer immediately.

They were standing near the small channel by the wall, clearing silt after another light rain.
The channel had performed well.
Not perfectly.
Enough.
Everything was enough lately and never enough.

"You want to leave," she said.

"Yes."

"To avoid people."

"Some."

"To think."

"Yes."

"To stop being asked."

"Yes."

"To ask without being watched."

He looked at her.

"Yes."

She nodded.

"Both."

He sighed.

"Yes."

Yosef heard later and said nothing until he had inspected three tool handles, a sandal strap, and Daniel's face.

Then he said, "How far?"

"Not far enough to be foolish."

"That is not a measure."

"No."

"How long?"

Daniel hesitated.

The story answered before he could.

Forty days.

He hated the number immediately.

Forty was a story number.
Rain, wandering, testing, waiting, old tales heavy with pattern.
A person could not say forty without inviting meaning to sit.

"Not forty," he said.

Yosef looked at him.

Daniel looked away.

"I mean, not because forty."

Yosef placed the tool handle down.

"How long can the household bear?"

That was the right question.

Not how long does story require?

Not how long does fear demand?

How long can the household bear?

Maryam answered before Daniel could.

"Some days.
Not many.
More if we prepare.
Less if people know."

Daniel said, "I can go to the old watch slope."

The old watch slope was not truly wilderness.
It was a rough place beyond regular paths, above scrub and stone, where shepherds sometimes passed and children sometimes dared one another to climb.
There was a shallow cave not good enough for shelter in hard rain but enough for shade.
A spring lower down, unreliable but known.
Distance enough to be alone.
Near enough that a search would not become tragedy unless Daniel was stupid.

"That is not wilderness," Mara said when told.

Daniel closed his eyes.

No one had invited her into the conversation.
She had arrived with a piece of cord, a fig, and the usual collapse of privacy.

"It is wilderness enough."

"No.
It has a name."

"Most places have names."

"The real wilderness should be nameless and full of dramatic rocks."

"I am not seeking dramatic rocks."

"Then why go?"

He opened his eyes.

She was not mocking now.

Not only.

"Because I need to find which voices get louder when people stop speaking."

Mara looked at him.

Then, unexpectedly, nodded.

"That is a good reason."

"It may also be a bad reason."

"Most of yours are both."

Maryam said, "He does not go hungry."

Daniel looked at her.

"I can fast."

"No."

"It is in the story."

"Then no twice."

Yosef said, "A hungry man thinks hunger is truth."

Mara added, "And becomes unpleasant."

Daniel said, "I am already unpleasant."

"Yes," Mara said.
"Why feed it?"

The argument ended there.
Not because Daniel agreed, but because everyone else did.

He would take food.
Simple food.
Not enough to be comfortable.
Enough not to confuse bodily collapse with revelation.
Water.
Cord.
Knife.
Cloak.
A small tool roll because Yosef refused to let anyone enter rocks without means to repair sandal, strap, or dignity.
No heavy tools.
No large bundle.
No sign.

Mara insisted on adding a small piece of charcoal.

"For writing?"

"For marking where you got lost so we can mock you efficiently."

Maryam accepted the charcoal.

Daniel did not object.

---

Leaving was awkward.

Prophetic departures should have been clean, perhaps: a figure walking into morning light while loved ones stood in sorrowful stillness.

Daniel's departure involved two failed attempts to tie the food bundle, a debate over whether dried figs counted as necessary or indulgent, Erel arriving late with a water skin he had patched, Hanina sending onions because she believed every journey needed onions, Lavi offering advice about knees despite Daniel's knees being blameless, and Mara discovering at the last moment that the charcoal had broken and therefore required ceremonial replacement.

Daniel stood near the threshold holding a bundle that smelled faintly of onions and defeat.

"I am going to think," he said.
"Not cross a desert empire."

"Then stop making that face," Mara said.

Maryam adjusted the cloak at his shoulder.

"You return if weather turns."

"Yes."

"If you become ill."

"Yes."

"If you find men there."

"Depending on the men."

Maryam looked at him.

"Yes."

"If you begin making answers only because no one can interrupt."

He paused.

That was a better warning.

"Yes."

Yosef handed him the small tool roll.

"Bring it back."

Daniel looked at him.

"The tools?"

"Also yourself."

Subtlety, Yosef had decided, was unnecessary.

Daniel nodded.

Erel said, "Do you want someone to come partway?"

No.

Yes.

"No."

Erel accepted this.

Mara stepped forward last.

She held out a small carved piece of wood.

Daniel recognized it: a rough bird.
Not the repaired bird her brother had made.
A new one, ugly, unfinished, with one wing too large and a beak like a wedge.

"What is this?"

"A bird."

"I see that."

"Then why ask?"

He accepted it.

"It is not a sign," she said.

"Good."

"It is a bird."

"Good."

"If a bird crosses the sky and you become strange, look at this one.
It is too ugly for prophecy."

Daniel closed his hand around it.

The beak poked his palm.

"Thank you."

She shrugged.

"Return both."

"Both names?"

"Both you.
And the bird."

He nodded.

"When I leave."

"When you leave."

He stepped across the threshold.

No music.
No voice.
No opened sky.

Only Maryam watching, Yosef standing with toolmaker stillness, Erel pretending dust was in his eye, Hanina arranging onions in the basket she had brought back, Lavi advising from the road about loose stones, and Mara looking angry enough to keep from looking afraid.

Daniel walked.

---

The old watch slope was less impressive than dread had promised.

This was a relief and an insult.

The path rose through scrub, stone, dry grass, thorn, and the kind of loose rock designed to make ankles reflect on mortality.
The air thinned not in height but in company.
Human sound fell away by degrees: first voices, then hammering, then animals, then the deep household hum that one did not notice until it vanished.

By midday, Daniel reached the shallow cave and judged it adequate.

Adequate was one of the most important words in the world.

It was not comfortable.
The floor sloped slightly.
One side showed signs of old animal use.
The roof had a crack that did not threaten immediate collapse but deserved watching.
Wind entered from the left.
Rain, if hard from the wrong direction, would make the back corner damp.
There was a place to sit, a place to store food, and a view of the lower paths if he stood near the edge.

Good enough.

He cleaned the floor.
Removed old debris.
Checked for snakes.
Found one small lizard, which seemed offended but not dangerous.
Set stones to keep the food bundle from sliding.
Marked the path to the spring with Mara's charcoal on three rocks, because refusing useful precautions to avoid mockery would be another form of pride.

Then he sat.

Silence arrived.

Not all at once.

At first there were practical sounds.
Wind.
Insects.
A bird.
His own breathing.
A stone shifting after he nudged it.
Cloth moving against rock.
A distant goat, because apparently goats extended into theological solitude.

Then the sounds separated.

Then his thoughts filled the spaces.

This, he realized, was why people avoided silence.

It did not empty the mind.

It evicted the tenants and revealed the vermin.

By late afternoon, he had thought of every mistake he had made in the past month, several from childhood, several from the old world, and one from when he was six involving a borrowed chisel that Yosef had forgiven too quickly.
He had replayed the river, the bird, Natan's question, Erel's face, Maryam saying fear also tells where love is, Yosef saying water is not that strong, Mara saying return both.

He ate bread, onion, and dried fig.

The fig was necessary.

He admitted this to no one.

At sunset, the rocks changed color in a way that painters would have ruined by making meaningful.
Daniel tried to simply look.

He failed.

The sky became old story.
The stones became wilderness.
The wind became testing.
Hunger became symbol.
The ugly wooden bird became anti-symbol, which was unfortunately also a kind of symbol.

He held it up.

"You are a bird," he told it.

The bird looked poorly carved.

Good.

Night came cold.

Daniel wrapped himself in the cloak and did not sleep for a long time.

---

On the first full day, Daniel made rules.

This was predictable and therefore suspicious.

Rules:

Eat enough not to mistake hunger for wisdom.

Drink before thirst makes decisions.

Walk only where return is visible.

Do not make vows at sunrise or sunset.

Do not answer questions asked only by loneliness.

If a thought becomes grand, make it carry water.

Repair something each day if possible.

Return if weather turns.

Return if sickness comes.

Return before solitude becomes performance.

He wrote none of these down.

Writing gave thoughts posture.

Instead, he repeated them while gathering stones to improve the cave floor.
The stones needed sorting.
Flat.
Too sharp.
Useful edge.
Support.
Wedge.
Bad idea.
Possible lizard home.
Mara would have made categories worse and better.

He missed her.

This annoyed him.

He had come to be alone.

Missing people was not failure, he told himself.
It was evidence of attachment.
Attachment made return possible.
Total self-sufficiency was not holiness; it was bad architecture.

He carried water from the spring and discovered that water was heavier alone.
This was obvious and therefore instructive.
At home, water traveled in shared rhythms: someone filled, someone carried, someone used, someone noticed when a jar ran low.
Alone, every use returned to his own legs.

By noon he had stopped wanting mystical insight and wanted a better water container.

This seemed spiritually healthy.

He spent the afternoon repairing the strap on the water skin Erel had patched.
The patch was good.
Daniel improved the tie.
Not because Erel had failed, but because movement revealed strain.
He would tell Erel that, carefully.

The first temptation came near evening.

It did not look like a devil.

It looked like bread.

Specifically, like the last good piece of bread.

He had rationed poorly, perhaps because part of him expected solitude to dignify hunger.
The remaining bread sat in the bundle beside onions and figs.
He looked at it and thought, absurdly: if you are who the story says, hunger should not matter.

He froze.

There.

Not a voice outside him.

Worse.

A sentence inside using the old story's furniture.

If you are.

He knew a version of the old line: if you are the son, command stones to become bread.
Or something close.
Memory refused precision and retained danger.

Daniel looked at the stones near his hand.

Flat limestone.
Hard.
Not bread.
Never going to be bread.
He could not command them to become anything except poorly stacked.

Good.

He waited for the thought to pass.

It did not.

If you are special, your needs should be solved specially.

If the story is true, the world owes you confirmation.

If you are not special, why fear?

If you are special, why hunger?

Daniel picked up the stone.

It fit his palm.
Warm from sun.

He set it on the ground.

Then he picked up the bread.

And ate it.

Slowly.

Not as miracle.

As food.

He also ate a fig because self-punishment was not discernment.

Then he took the stone and used it to weigh the edge of his cloak against wind.

"Stone," he said.

Then, after a moment, "Bread."

He looked from one to the other.

"Different."

This was not profound.

It was necessary.

That night, he added a rule:

Do not ask stones to become bread.
Also do not pretend bread is weakness.

---

On the second day, he became useful to no one.

This was harder than expected.

At home, usefulness arrived constantly.
A peg needed sorting.
A child needed redirecting.
A roof needed watching.
Maryam needed a jar moved.
Yosef needed a hand.
Mara needed stopping.
Erel needed correction and sometimes the dignity of correcting Daniel.
Hanina needed witness.
Natan needed boundaries.
The world came with handles.

On the slope, no one asked.

This meant Daniel had to exist without being immediately required.

He disliked it.

He cleaned the cave.
Improved the water path.
Moved two stones from the path that might turn an ankle.
Cut a thorn branch away from the spring approach.
Repaired his sandal though it was not yet broken.
Then realized he was turning solitude into household work because usefulness felt safer than being.

He sat down.

Doing nothing lasted eleven breaths.

Then fifteen.

Then perhaps twenty.

The second temptation arrived as a plan.

Not a bad plan.

A brilliant one.

He could leave the village quietly.
Not now, perhaps, but soon.
Become a teacher of practical ethics and repair in another place under another name.
Avoid the river man's path.
Avoid the old story's geography.
Take what he had learned and build something useful far from narrative pressure.
No miracles.
No crowds seeking signs.
No Jerusalem.
No execution wood.
Teach foundations, water, apology, debt, dignity.
Heal nothing supernaturally, but help many materially.
Make less fire.
Save more people by leaving.

It was a beautiful plan.

This was how he knew it was dangerous.

Beautiful plans hid their costs in distant rooms.

He followed the load path.

Who would carry the leaving?

Maryam.

Yosef.

Mara.

Erel.

Hanina.

The people who trusted his return.

The old promise: when I leave.

Return when you leave.

The plan used mercy as a cloak for escape.
It was not wholly false.
He might help elsewhere.
He might avoid harm.
He might live.
But the first load landed on those he loved, and the plan had tried not to show him that.

He picked up Mara's ugly bird.

"Come back as you," she had said.

Which one?

Both.

He set the bird in front of him.

"I cannot save people by making return optional."

The bird remained ugly.

Good.

He did not discard the plan entirely.
Maryam would scold him for that.
Some future road might be necessary.
Fear could lie; so could staying.
But this plan, today, was not calling him outward.
It was calling him away.

He named it.

Escape wearing usefulness.

Naming did not defeat it.

It made it easier to watch.

---

On the third day, men came.

This was unreasonable.

Daniel had chosen a place specifically because people rarely came there.
Naturally, three shepherds appeared with a small flock, two arguments, one injured animal, and no interest in Daniel's solitude.

The injured animal had a thorn lodged near the hoof.

Daniel saw it because he was cursed by competence.

He considered hiding in the cave.

This lasted one heartbeat.

Then the smallest shepherd, a boy perhaps twelve, tried to hold the animal badly and nearly got kicked.

Daniel stood.

"Not like that."

All three turned.

The oldest shepherd narrowed his eyes.

"Who are you?"

"Someone who does not want him kicked."

This was acceptable identification.

The animal objected to everyone.
Daniel showed the boy where to stand, how to hold without placing his face near the hoof, how to shift weight.
The oldest shepherd removed the thorn with a small knife.
Daniel cleaned what could be cleaned and suggested watching for swelling.
The shepherds accepted this with the grudging respect people gave useful strangers they had not planned to need.

They offered him a little curd.

He accepted.

This ruined his solitude and improved his day.

The third temptation came after they left.

If you help without being known, perhaps that is pure.

This sounded good.

Very good.

Too good.

He examined it.

Hidden help protected dignity sometimes.
Maryam used it.
Yosef allowed work to carry credit without praise.
Daniel had learned to make others visible when true and himself less central when useful.

But hidden help could also avoid accountability.
It could let him enter people's lives without being answerable to them.
It could make him feel clean because no one praised him, while still letting him choose the work, define the repair, and leave before consequences appeared.

If the shepherd boy misused the hold later and was hurt, who would know to correct him?

If the animal worsened, who would return?

If advice traveled badly, who would repair it?

Hiddenness was not automatically humility.

Sometimes it was another form of control.

Daniel hated this.

Every path, inspected long enough, contained rot.

No.

Not rot.

Load.

Every path carried load.

The question was not how to find a path without load.
The question was who knew the load existed, who could bear it, and who would be left beneath it if it failed.

He wrote that one in dirt, then immediately erased it.

Too useful.

Too dangerous.

Mara would have stolen it by smell.

---

Days four through seven became small.

This was good.

He woke.
Drank.
Ate.
Checked the sky.
Cleared the cave entrance.
Walked to the spring.
Carried water.
Sat.
Failed to think cleanly.
Watched insects.
Repaired sandal.
Slept badly.
Woke cold.
Repeated.

Without people, time became both larger and less interesting.

He began noticing his body differently.
Hunger changed mood.
Thirst sharpened irritation.
Cold made theology stupid.
A stone under the hip could ruin contemplation more effectively than doubt.
Flies were not morally significant but could still dominate attention.
The body was not an obstacle to spiritual clarity.
It was the structure through which clarity had to pass, and it creaked.

He had spent years learning that bodies were materials but not only materials.

Here, he learned the opposite danger: minds were not above materials.

A thought made in thirst was a warped board.

A vow made while cold might shrink near fire.

A vision after too little food could be only the body's complaint wearing light.

He ate steadily after that.

Not comfortably.

Steadily.

On the eighth day, he found a small crack in the cave roof that deserved attention if rain came hard.
He marked it with charcoal.
The mark looked like a goat by accident.

He stared.

Then laughed for the first time alone.

The sound startled a lizard.

"Sorry," he told it.

The lizard left.

This seemed fair.

---

On the ninth day, Natan's question returned.

Not the parchment.

The shape.

If water does not make a man clean, why did you go down?

Daniel answered differently now.

Because I needed to learn that uncleanness was not the only issue.

He frowned.

That sounded like a teacher.

He tried again.

Because water showed me I wanted water to do my work.

Better.

Again.

Because a body can tell the truth before the mind stops arguing.

That one he liked.

Dangerous.

He imagined Natan hearing it, eyes sharpening.
He imagined Mara mocking it into safety.
He imagined Maryam asking what the sentence would cost if carried by the wrong person.
He imagined Yosef saying nothing and handing him a broken object.

He picked up the ugly bird.

"What do you think?"

The bird's large wing made it look permanently skeptical.

"Correct."

He set it down.

The fourth temptation came at midday while looking over the lower land.

From the slope, he could see paths joining.
Small figures moving.
Smoke columns.
Fields.
Distant roofs.
The village looked like a model, a structure of structures.
Problems became visible as patterns: water paths, travel paths, work rhythms, lines of poverty, places where repair knowledge had spread, places untouched.

He saw, with dangerous clarity, how much could be improved.

Teach water management systematically.
Marks standardized.
Questions carried through households.
Apprentices trained not just in tools but in inspection, dignity, rumor, debt, smoke, ventilation, thresholds.
Create local repair circles.
Teach women and children because they saw what men missed.
Make practical sayings.
Send trained people outward.
Build a movement that refused miracle and made maintenance sacred.

It was a beautiful vision.

More beautiful than leaving.

More dangerous.

He could do it.

Maybe.

That was the hook.

He could build something large enough that the old story would have to bend around it.
Replace public ministry with public maintenance.
Replace crowds seeking healing with communities learning prevention.
Replace parables with inspection protocols.
Replace signs with systems.

The modern part of him almost wept from desire.

Finally.
A plan worthy of knowledge.

Then he saw the hidden load.

Movement.

Followers.

Sayings.

Travel.

Authority.

People using his name.

Disputes over proper marks.

Men like Natan turning practice into doctrine.

Others claiming legitimacy by proximity.

Women erased again unless protected.

Children repeating without understanding.

Power gathering because power always followed useful structures and asked to sit at the head.

He would create the very thing he feared, only with better drainage.

Daniel sat down hard.

The old story did not need miracles to trap him.

Competence could do it.

Mercy with logistics could become institution.

Institution could become machine.

Machine could crush what it began to protect.

He put his head in his hands.

No wonder people preferred devils with horns.

At least one could tell where they stood.

The temptation was not to do evil.

It was to do good at a scale that made him stop seeing who carried the cost.

He named it.

Kingdom by maintenance.

Then crossed it out in dirt.

Then wrote beneath it:

Teach only what can be corrected by those who use it.

He stared at that.

Maybe.

Not answer.

A guardrail.

He kept it until wind erased part.

---

On the twelfth day, he almost went home.

Not because he was finished.

Because he was lonely.

He missed Maryam's infuriating clarity.
Yosef's pauses.
Mara's sabotage of grandeur.
Erel's earnestness.
Hanina's practical anger.
Even Natan's careful hooks.

He missed being interrupted.

This surprised him.

Silence had seemed like freedom.
Now it showed itself as a place where Daniel could become too coherent.
No one corrected the shape of his thoughts.
No one laughed at the dangerous ones.
No one asked who carried the result.
Alone, he could build elegant answers that fit no room.

He packed half the bundle.

Then stopped.

Returning because loneliness had spoken alone would be the same error in reverse.

He unpacked.

Sat.

Listened.

Loneliness said: go home.

Pride said: stay longer.

Fear said: decide now.

The body said: drink water.

The body was often the wisest available voice.

He drank.

Then slept.

In the morning, he decided to stay one more day and decide again.

This became his method.

Not forty.

One more day honestly.

The count began by accident.

One more day, thirteen.

One more day, fourteen.

One more day, fifteen.

He did not like how the number grew.

He kept counting anyway because not counting would be theatrical.

---

On the seventeenth day, weather turned.

Not dangerously.

Enough.

Clouds gathered from the west.
Wind changed.
The cave's weak crack deserved attention.
Daniel reinforced the dry side with stones and a small deflection channel, inadequate for a true storm but enough for a passing rain.
He moved the food bundle, checked the path down, marked two slick stones.

Rain came late.

The cave held mostly.

One drip formed near the back.

Of course.

He placed the empty food bowl beneath it.

Drip.

Pause.

Drip.

He laughed so hard he nearly knocked the bowl over.

Water with a channel.

Even here.

Especially here.

The rain kept him in place through the next day.
He ate less because moving to the spring was unpleasant and because the food bundle had become visibly finite.
He did not fast.
He rationed.
This distinction mattered.

In the damp quiet, the fifth temptation came as a voice that sounded like responsibility.

You should go back with answers.

Not questions.
Not cautions.
Answers.

People need them.
Maryam is tired.
Yosef is aging.
Mara cannot mock everything into safety.
Erel needs structure.
Hanina needs justice.
Natan needs direction.
The river people need language.
The village needs teaching.
The world is full of roofs and smoke and debt and shame.
Stop hesitating.
Speak.

This temptation was harder because part of it was true.

People did need help.

Hesitation could become cowardice.

Questions could become hiding places.

Daniel had used uncertainty as shelter before.

But answers formed in isolation had not been tested under load.
A design that looked perfect in a cave might fail in a kitchen, market, courtyard, sickroom, wedding, or debt dispute.
He could return with tools.
Not tablets.

He picked up a small flat stone and imagined carving laws into it.

Do not.

He set it down.

The answer came, not as voice, but as memory of Yosef:

A good solution must fit the place where it works.
And the people near it.

Daniel said aloud, "No answers without rooms."

The cave accepted this.

The drip continued.

---

On the twentieth day, Mara appeared.

Daniel thought at first she was a hallucination, which said unflattering things about both his mental state and hers.

She stood on the path below the cave with a bundle on one shoulder and the expression of someone prepared to deny wrongdoing before hearing the charge.

Daniel stared.

She climbed the last stretch, breathing hard but refusing to show it.

"No," he said.

"Good morning to you too."

"You should not be here."

"Many things are true."

"Maryam said—"

"Maryam sent me."

That stopped him.

Mara looked pleased.

Then less pleased.

"Mostly."

Daniel closed his eyes.

"Mostly?"

"She sent food to the lower spring.
I carried it farther."

"That is not mostly."

"It began as obedience."

"It stopped."

"Many journeys do."

He looked past her.

"Are you alone?"

"Erel came partway."

"Partway?"

"He is below, pretending not to wait."

Daniel exhaled.

Good.

Still not good.

"Why?"

Mara set the bundle down.

"Because you are bad at knowing when solitude becomes performance."

He stared.

That sounded like Maryam.

"And," Mara added, "because I wanted to see whether you had become unbearable."

"Have I?"

"You are talking to lizards?"

"One lizard."

"Borderline."

He almost smiled.

She looked around the cave, inspected the drip bowl, the marked crack, the water skin, the stones, the ugly bird placed near the wall.

"You kept it."

"Yes."

"It remains ugly."

"Yes."

"Good."

Daniel sat on a stone.

Mara crouched across from him.

For a while neither spoke.

The silence with Mara in it was different.
Still quiet, but interruptible.
Safer.

She opened the bundle: bread, figs, a little dried fish, fresh cord, and a note from Maryam consisting of no words, only a piece of cloth Daniel recognized as one she used for wrapping food when she wanted someone to know they were being cared for without discussing it.

He touched it.

Mara looked away.

"Everyone asks about you."

"I know."

"No, you imagine.
I know."

"Fine.
You know."

"Some think you are praying."

"I am mostly failing."

"Close enough."

"Some?"

"Some think you are avoiding work."

"Also true."

"Some think the river made you strange."

"I was strange before."

"I told them."

"Thank you?"

"Some think you will come back with teachings."

Daniel groaned.

Mara nodded.

"I told them if you came back with teachings, I would inspect them for goats."

"That may be the only thing protecting me."

"I know."

She handed him bread.

He ate.

It tasted shockingly good.

"Do not make a revelation from bread," she said.

"I am trying not to."

"Try harder."

They sat.

After a while, she said, "Why are you still here?"

He looked toward the view.

"Because I keep finding voices that sound wise."

"And?"

"And I need to hear which ones get foolish when left alone."

Mara considered.

"Which ones?"

He counted.

"Specialness.
Escape.
Hidden help.
Building something large enough to beat the story.
Returning with answers.
Staying because pride likes endurance.
Leaving because loneliness asks.
Hunger pretending to be clarity.
Mercy ignoring cost.
Accuracy ignoring people."

Mara was quiet.

Then she said, "That is too many."

"Yes."

"No wonder you are tired."

He laughed softly.

She picked up a stone and threw it badly at another stone.

"What have you found that is not foolish?"

He thought.

"Return."

She waited.

"Work after water."

She nodded.

"People correcting each other."

She smiled faintly.

"Food."

"Obviously."

"Not deciding alone."

Mara looked at him then.

"That one."

"Yes."

"Then why are you alone?"

He had no answer.

Good question.

Door.
Hook.
Foundation.

Mara did not press.

She stood.

"I will go before Erel decides I have been eaten by holy rocks."

"Thank you."

"For food?"

"For coming too far."

She shrugged.

"Return before I have to do it again."

"I will."

"When?"

He looked at the sky.

"Not today."

She made a face.

"But soon?"

"One more day honestly."

She hated this answer.

Accepted it.

At the edge of the path, she turned.

"If a voice tells you to jump from a height because someone will catch you, do not."

Daniel stared.

She looked smug.

"What?"

"That is—"

"What?"

"Too close."

Her face changed.

"I was joking."

"I know."

The old fragment flared: pinnacle, temple, angels, throw yourself down.
Or perhaps he supplied it from memory because she had opened the door.
Either way, the air shifted.

Mara climbed back up two steps.

"What is it?"

He told her.

Not fully.
Enough.

She listened, went pale with anger, and looked at every high rock nearby as if planning to prosecute them.

"No jumping," she said.

"No jumping."

"No proving."

"No proving."

"No making danger just to test rescue."

The sentence landed.

Not just old story.

Every day.

No making danger to test rescue.

Daniel sat with it.

Mara said, "Add that."

"I will."

"And if you forget, I will push you from a very small rock and not catch you."

He laughed.

She left.

The cave felt lonelier afterward.

Also less false.

---

The sixth temptation came that evening, naturally.

He stood near the edge, not dangerously close, looking over the slope where Mara and Erel had disappeared.
The drop was not a temple height.
Not dramatic.
Enough to break an ankle, maybe worse if unlucky.
The memory Mara had triggered hovered.

Throw yourself down.

Prove protection.

Force the sky to answer.

Daniel stepped back.

Not because he wanted to jump.

He did not.

But because the temptation was larger than jumping.

Make danger in order to clarify love.

Refuse ordinary uncertainty.
Create a crisis where others must reveal whether they will save you, whether God will speak, whether story will catch, whether fear was right.
Turn ambiguity into emergency.
Force a verdict.

He knew that temptation.

Not from cliffs.

From questions asked too sharply.

From plans that put Maryam and Yosef in positions where they had to prove trust.

From making his own fear so visible others had to arrange themselves around it.

From silence that became a test.

From leaving and watching who followed.

No making danger to test rescue.

Mara had named it by accident.

Or by being Mara, which was often the same.

Daniel picked up a stone and moved it back from the edge.
Then another.
He created a small line not to cross in darkness.

A boundary.

Not because the cliff was calling.

Because bodies in darkness deserved help from decisions made in light.

He slept poorly but safely.

---

On the twenty-fifth day, he stopped expecting revelation.

This felt like progress.

The days became work.
Wake, inspect, drink, eat, sit, walk, return, repair, think, refuse overthinking, sleep.
He spoke aloud sometimes, not to hear wisdom but to notice when sentences became too polished.
Polished sentences were not always false.
But in solitude, polish came too easily.
People scuffed truth into usefulness.

He began imagining how each thought would survive at home.

Maryam would ask what it cost.

Yosef would ask where it worked.

Mara would ask whether goats were involved.

Erel would ask what to do with anger.

Hanina would ask how it helped a damp room.

Natan would ask what kind of question it was.

The river man would ask what work came up.

These imagined witnesses formed a better council than his private mind.

He realized, uneasily, that even alone he was not alone.

This was either comfort or failure, depending on one's theology.

Daniel accepted it as structure.

The seventh temptation came as relief.

You do not need the old story at all.

Just stop checking.

Stop comparing.
Stop listing.
Stop fearing fit.
Stop caring whether events rhyme.
Live.
Work.
Love.
If the story comes, let it.
If it does not, ignore it.
The future is not here.
Only today.
Today is a floor.

This temptation sounded like his own best conclusions.

That made it dangerous.

He had learned today mattered.
Work mattered.
The floor before him mattered.
But using today to refuse tomorrow was another evasion.
Ignoring a visible crack because it had not yet failed was not wisdom.
The old story could not be allowed to rule every act.
It also could not be dismissed when patterns placed weight on people.

He needed neither obsession nor denial.

He needed watchfulness without worship.

He needed to let future possibility inform care without becoming command.

He hated this balance because it could not be finished.

He said aloud, "The story is not a master.
It is not nothing."

The lizard did not comment.

Good.

---

On the thirtieth day, food ran low enough that the next decision became practical.

He had planned for fewer days.

Everyone had.

He could stretch what remained, but then hunger would begin speaking louder.
The spring still ran.
Weather looked stable.
His body was tired but sound.
The cave had become almost orderly, which was its own danger.
Solitude had begun growing habits.

He should return.

Pride objected.

The number objected.

Thirty was not forty.

He laughed when he recognized the thought.

There it was.

Not story demanding reenactment.

Pride wanting completion.

If he stayed to reach forty because forty sounded meaningful, he would be obeying the story while pretending discipline.

If he left at thirty because he feared forty, he would also be obeying the story.

So he asked better questions.

What is broken?

Food nearly done.
Loneliness bending thought.
Household carrying absence.
Enough voices heard.

What can be done?

Return.

What can it bear?

A return now, not a performance of endurance.

Who is left with the result?

Everyone.

He packed.

Then unpacked.

Then packed again more carefully, because the first packing had been emotional and poor.

He cleaned the cave.
Removed marks that did not need to remain.
Left the stone line near the edge because someone else might benefit.
Cleared the spring approach.
Buried waste.
Took the ugly bird.
Took the charcoal stub.
Took the bowl.
Checked for forgotten food.
Thanked the lizard though it was not visible.

Then he sat one last time.

No voice.

No sign.

No devil defeated.

No angels arriving with food.

Just a young man in a shallow cave deciding thirty was enough because enough was not the same as complete.

This, he thought, might be the hardest lesson so far.

He started down.

---

Mara met him halfway.

Of course.

This time she had Erel with her, and both carried expressions of innocence so poorly constructed they collapsed under light.

Daniel stopped on the path.

"No."

Mara said, "You do not know what I am doing."

"Meeting me halfway."

"Then you do know."

Erel said, "We were checking the lower spring."

"With no container?"

Erel looked at his empty hands.

Mara said, "Spiritually."

Daniel laughed.

It came out rougher than expected.

Mara's face changed.

Erel stepped forward, then stopped.

Daniel realized he was crying.

Not much.

Enough.

This was embarrassing.

Mara looked alarmed and pretended not to be.

"Did the rocks hurt you?"

"No."

"The lizard?"

"No."

"Holy insects?"

"No."

Erel said, quietly, "You came back."

Daniel wiped his face.

"Yes."

Mara pointed at him.

"Both?"

"Both."

"The bird?"

He held it up.

She inspected it.

"Still ugly."

"Yes."

"Good."

They walked down together.

Not solemnly, because Mara tripped once and blamed a stone for poor placement.
Erel offered to carry Daniel's bundle.
Daniel refused, then accepted after a while because refusal had become more about pride than strength.
Erel carried it as if entrusted with something important and breakable.

At the lower road, Hanina's older girl saw them and ran ahead.

By the time Daniel reached the house, people had arranged themselves badly while trying to appear not to have gathered.

Maryam stood at the threshold.

Yosef beside her.

Hanina with onions again, because apparently onions had become the household language for survival.

Lavi with knee advice.

Two children with goat boards.

Natan at a respectful distance holding what appeared to be a broken hinge, because he had learned.

Daniel stopped.

The house looked smaller than memory and larger than safety.

Maryam did not run to him.

He was grateful.

He was not small.

She stepped forward and placed one hand on his cheek.

"You are thin."

"Yes."

"Dirty."

"Yes."

"Early."

He laughed.

"Yes."

Her eyes filled.

"Good."

Yosef gripped his shoulder.

Hard.

"Tools?"

Daniel handed him the roll.

Yosef checked it.

Everyone waited.

Yosef said, "All returned."

Mara whispered, "He checked the tools first."

Daniel said, "He said also myself."

Yosef looked at him.

"Yes."

Maryam said, "Come inside."

Daniel crossed the threshold.

No sky opened.

The beam held.

The room smelled of bread, wood, smoke, cloth, onions, and people.

Too many people.

Not too many.

He sat.

Maryam put food in his hands.

He ate.

No one asked at first.

This was mercy.

Then Mara said, "So, teachings?"

Maryam said, "Mara."

Daniel swallowed bread.

"Some."

Everyone looked at him.

He held up a hand.

"Not now."

They relaxed, or pretended to.

He looked around the room and understood that returning was not the end of wilderness.
It was the test of it.

Solitude had produced thoughts.

Now people would decide whether they held.

---

That evening, after food, after washing, after the first wave of practical inspection, Daniel sat with Maryam, Yosef, Mara, Erel, and, unexpectedly, Hanina, who had stayed to help and then stayed because leaving before the story came would be absurd.

Natan remained outside until Maryam noticed and said, "If you brought a hinge, bring it in."

He entered with the broken hinge.

Good.

Lavi did not enter because his knees had carried enough revelation for one day, but his voice drifted from outside offering commentary anyway.

Daniel told them some.

Not all.

He told them about bread and stones.
About eating instead of making hunger holy.
About the beautiful plan to leave and help elsewhere.
About hidden help not always being humility.
About the dream of building a movement of maintenance and how even good systems could gather power.
About almost staying for the number rather than the need.
About no making danger to test rescue, and he credited Mara.

Mara looked startled.

Then pleased.

Then worried that pleasure was visible.

He told them about returning at thirty because food was low and enough had been enough.

Natan listened with painful attention.

Maryam listened with her whole face.

Yosef listened with his hands still.

Hanina said, when Daniel finished the part about systems becoming machines, "So you learned what women already know about households."

Everyone looked at her.

She shrugged.

"If a system feeds people, someone will try to sit at the head and call himself the table."

Mara pointed at her.

"She teaches now."

Maryam smiled.

Daniel said, "Yes."

Hanina looked embarrassed but not sorry.

Good.

Natan said, "What will you do with what you learned?"

Daniel looked at the broken hinge beside him.

"Repair that."

Natan blinked.

Mara laughed.

Daniel continued, "Then sleep.
Then tomorrow's work."

"After that?"

"More work."

Natan accepted this, perhaps because the hinge was real and the answer annoying enough to be true.

Maryam said, "And the teachings?"

Daniel looked at her.

"Only with rooms."

She nodded.

Yosef said, "And people near."

"Yes."

Mara said, "And goat inspection."

"No."

"Yes."

"No."

Hanina said, "If there are teachings, women hear them first."

The room went quiet.

Not because the statement was shocking.

Because it was structurally sound.

Daniel looked at Maryam.

Then at Hanina.

Then Mara.

"Yes," he said.

Natan opened his mouth, then closed it.

Good.

Yosef smiled faintly.

Maryam's eyes shone.

Hanina nodded once, satisfied.

Mara said, "And goats?"

Daniel threw a shaving at her.

She caught it.

The hinge was repaired before sleep.

This mattered.

---

Later, Daniel lay awake under the beam.

The wilderness had not ended the story.

The river had not decided.

The sky had not spoken.

Stones had not become bread.

No one had carried him from a height.

No kingdom had been offered except the ones he imagined building himself.

No devil had stood outside him because the dangerous voices had known the house well enough to speak from inside.

He had returned early.

Or not early.

He had returned when return became the work.

Below the beam, in the dark, he held Mara's ugly bird and thought of the line of stones near the cave edge.
A boundary made in daylight for a body that might move in darkness.

Perhaps that was all a teaching could be.

Not a commandment against every fall.

A small line of stones.

A remembered voice.

A person nearby if possible.

A way back.

From the other side of the room, Maryam said, "Sleep."

Daniel smiled.

"How did you know?"

"I am your mother."

Still not an explanation.

Still load-bearing.

He closed his eyes.

Tomorrow, someone would come with a door, a question, a debt, a roof, a goat mark, a hinge, a story, or a hunger.

He would not have answers for all of it.

Good.

Answers without rooms were dangerous.

Tomorrow had rooms.

He slept.
