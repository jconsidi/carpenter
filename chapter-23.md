# Chapter 23 — Healings Without Power

The first person who came to be healed brought a broken stool.

This was considerate.

It allowed everyone to pretend, for several minutes, that the matter was furniture.

The man’s name was Tobiah.
He was thin in the way of men who spent more strength appearing cheerful than eating, with a beard trimmed unevenly and a cough he kept turning aside as if coughs respected politeness.
He placed the stool in Yosef’s courtyard and said, “It leans.”

The stool did lean.

So did Tobiah.

Daniel noticed both and immediately mistrusted the symmetry.

Yosef inspected the stool.

Maryam inspected the man.

Mara, who had been drawing an improved version of the can-they-leave mark on a scrap of wood, looked from one to the other and whispered, “Two stools.”

Daniel stepped on her foot.

She kicked him back.

Tobiah laughed, then coughed long enough that no one else did.

When the cough ended, his face had changed color.

Maryam brought water.

He accepted it with embarrassment.

“My wife said to bring the stool,” he said.

Yosef turned the stool over.

“Good.
It is broken.”

Tobiah smiled faintly.

“She said that if I came asking about my chest, I would not come.”

Maryam said, “She knows you.”

“Yes.”

The stool had one cracked leg and a loose crosspiece.
A simple repair if the wood still had enough soundness, which it did, barely.
The man’s cough was less simple.
It sounded wet sometimes, dry others, old, worsened by smoke perhaps, or dust, or damp bedding, or work, or fever not yet visible, or a hundred causes Daniel could not diagnose in a world without imaging, antibiotics, spirometry, modern sanitation, germ theory widely understood, or any right to pretend confidence.

He knew enough to be useful.

He knew enough to be dangerous.

This was now familiar.

Tobiah looked at Daniel with the face people used when trying not to look as if they had come for him.

Daniel hated that face.

Yosef said, “The stool can be repaired.”

Tobiah nodded.

Maryam said, “And the cough?”

The man’s eyes flicked toward Daniel.

There.

The real stool.

Tobiah said, “It is nothing.”

Maryam waited.

He coughed again.

“It is something,” she said.

Yosef continued removing the broken leg as if the room had not tightened.
This was kindness.
It gave Tobiah somewhere to look.

Tobiah said, “People said your son knows rooms.”

“Sometimes,” Maryam said.

“Smoke.
Damp.
Breathing.” He took another sip of water.
“They said he helped Hanina’s child.”

Daniel’s stomach clenched.

Hanina’s child had improved because of less smoke, a drier sleeping place, cloth, time, and perhaps luck.
Not because Daniel healed him.
Not because anyone healed him.
A room had been made less cruel.
A child’s body had done what bodies sometimes could when given a little less to fight.

But rumor had better hands now.
It did not always grab miracle openly.
Sometimes it carried care until care looked like power.

Daniel said, “Hanina’s room changed.”

Tobiah nodded too quickly.

“Yes.
Mine can change.”

Good.

Maybe.

Maryam asked, “Where do you sleep?”

He answered poorly at first because men often described houses as if people did not inhabit them.
Maryam corrected him by asking where his wife slept, where children slept, where smoke went, who coughed at night, whether bedding was damp in morning, where wood was stored, whether rain entered, whether he worked with dust, whether fever came, whether blood came.

At blood, Tobiah looked down.

Maryam’s face did not change.

Daniel’s body went cold.

“How much?” she asked.

“Only sometimes.”

“How much?” she repeated.

Tobiah made a small gesture.

Not enough to know.

Enough to worry.

Mara stopped drawing.

Yosef’s hands paused, then resumed.

Daniel wanted to be in Boston.
In a clinic.
With a chest X-ray.
With sputum testing.
With antibiotics if bacterial.
With sterile instruments.
With oxygen.
With a plan backed by centuries of accumulated medicine.
He wanted evidence, categories, probabilities, charts, machines, controlled doses, follow-up appointments, and someone else responsible for not being God.

Instead, he had a courtyard, a broken stool, smoke questions, water, cloth, herbs of uncertain value, and people looking at him as if knowing a little might become enough if needed badly enough.

He said, carefully, “I cannot heal a lung.”

Tobiah flinched.

Maryam looked at Daniel, but did not correct him.

Good.

Truth first, but not alone.

Daniel continued, “I can look at the room.
We can see smoke, damp, bedding, work dust.
Those matter.
They may help.
They may not be enough.”

Tobiah swallowed.

“People said—”

“People say water changed at a wedding,” Mara said.

Maryam looked at her.

Mara held up both hands.

“Useful correction.”

Tobiah blinked.

Daniel said, “People turn help into story because story hurts less than limits.”

The words came out too polished.

Too late.

Tobiah’s face tightened.

Maryam stepped in.

“And because people need hope,” she said.

Daniel closed his mouth.

Yes.

He had left that out.

Tobiah looked at Maryam.

She said, “We can come see the room.”

He breathed carefully.

“When?”

Yosef fitted the repaired stool leg loosely, checking alignment.

“After this holds.”

Tobiah looked at the stool.

It was absurd, perhaps.
A man coughing blood while carpenters insisted on repairing a stool before inspecting his room.
But the stool mattered.
He had brought it as permission.
Repairing it honored the path by which he had managed to ask.

Maryam saw that.

Yosef saw that.

Daniel was learning.

The stool left stronger than it arrived.

Tobiah did not.

---

Tobiah’s house was not the worst Daniel had seen.

This made it worse in a different way.

Catastrophe was easy to condemn.
Ordinary insufficiency hid everywhere.
A house could be almost adequate and still harm a sick man every night.

The room was small, shared by Tobiah, his wife Liora, two children, and Tobiah’s mother, who sat near the wall with a look suggesting she had already judged everyone’s approach and found it slow.
Smoke from the cooking area had a path out, technically.
The opening was partly blocked against wind and rain.
Bedding lay near a wall that felt dry by afternoon but smelled damp near the base.
Wood shavings from Tobiah’s work clung to a corner cloth.
He carved small handles and cheap pegs for a trader; dust, Daniel thought.
Fine dust.
Repeated.
Years.

No single cause.

Of course.

Liora greeted Maryam with controlled desperation.

She greeted Daniel with hope so naked he looked away.

“Do not look at him like that,” Tobiah’s mother said.

Liora flushed.

The old woman continued, “He is a boy, not a jar of new lungs.”

Daniel liked her immediately and wished she had not needed to say it.

Maryam said, “Your name?”

“Ruth.”

Of course it was.

Not biblical Ruth, Daniel told himself.
Many women had names.
Names were not traps.
The story did not own all of them.

Ruth pointed with her chin.

“The smoke sits there when wind comes wrong.
He coughs worse when he sleeps near that wall.
He says it is nothing because men think naming a thing makes it heavier.
It is already heavy.
Name it.”

Maryam smiled faintly.

“You have seen much.”

“I live here.”

That was the chapter, Daniel thought.
That was everything.
Diagnosis began with the people who lived there.

He said, “Show me morning damp.”

Ruth nodded once, approving perhaps.

Liora showed them the bedding, the wall, the place where smoke moved, the work cloth, the storage, the children’s sleeping spot.
Yosef inspected the roof edge and smoke opening.
Maryam asked who could move, who could not, who would object, who controlled the storage, what work had to remain inside, what could be done outside, what time of day dust was worst, who washed cloth, whether Tobiah could work less.

At that last question, Liora laughed once.

Not because it was funny.

Because money.

Daniel knew.

He wished knowledge paid rent.

They made changes.

Not healing.

Changes.

Move Tobiah’s sleeping mat away from the damp wall.
Put Ruth nearer the old wall only if she agreed, which she did not, and said so with enough force that no one suggested it again.
Rearrange storage so the sleeping place nearest cleaner air went to the coughing man at night and children during the day.
Move the carving dust outside when weather allowed.
Shake the work cloth away from the room, not in it.
Open the smoke vent more during cooking and create a small angled cover to keep rain from entering directly.
Dry bedding in sun whenever possible.
Raise one edge from the floor with thin slats Yosef could make from scrap.
Keep the water jar covered because flies had opinions no one needed.
Burn less damp wood if possible.

“If possible,” Daniel repeated.

He hated how much work hid inside those words.

“If possible means who?” Maryam asked.

Liora said, “Me.”

Ruth said, “And me, though everyone pretends my hands are ornamental.”

Tobiah said, “I can move the carving outside.”

Liora said, “When wind is not cruel.”

Yosef said, “Then a small shade piece.”

Daniel looked at him.

Yosef nodded.

Possible.

Not enough.

Possible.

They worked.

Neighbors noticed.

This was unavoidable.

A man across the lane asked if Yosef’s son was healing Tobiah.

“No,” Ruth shouted before anyone else could answer.
“He is moving smoke because smoke is stupid and so are men.”

The neighbor retreated.

Mara, who had come to carry cord and watch Daniel for excessive meaning, whispered, “I love her.”

Daniel said, “You love too many dangerous women.”

“Yes.”

The room improved by evening.

A little.

The air moved better.
The bedding was less badly placed.
The dust cloth had a new routine.
Tobiah looked exhausted by being helped.
Liora looked more frightened, not less, because changes made the illness more real.
Ruth looked satisfied with the vent and dissatisfied with mortality.

Before they left, Tobiah asked, “Will it heal?”

There it was.

The question beneath all work.

Daniel could not answer.

Maryam did.

“It may help.”

Tobiah closed his eyes.

Daniel expected anger.

Instead, Tobiah nodded.

“Help is not nothing.”

Ruth said, “It is also not enough, so do not become poetic and forget the mat.”

Tobiah smiled weakly.

That might have been the first true improvement.

Not healing.

A breath with less loneliness in it.

---

The rumor ignored Ruth.

This was rude and predictable.

By the next day, people said Daniel had gone to a sick man’s house and made the air move.

This was technically true and therefore dangerous.

By the second day, people said Tobiah slept without coughing.

False.

By the third, someone said the blood had stopped.

Unknown.

By the fourth, a woman arrived with a feverish child.

No broken object.

No stool.

No excuse.

She came carrying the child and terror, which was heavier.

Maryam opened the door.

The woman said, “Please.”

That was all.

No one asked for payment.

No one asked for proof of rumor.

No one told her to bring work.

A feverish child changed rules.

Maryam brought them inside, then outside again because the air was better in the shade and because Maryam did not let panic choose rooms.
Yosef cleared space.
Mara ran for water before being told.
Erel arrived with cloth because he had learned that crises always needed cloth.

Daniel looked at the child.

Small.
Hot.
Limp but responsive to pain and voice.
No obvious rash in the dim light.
Breathing fast.
Mouth dry.
The mother said fever since night, vomiting once, no blood, no seizure, no stiff neck that Daniel could assess with confidence.
He knew enough modern medicine to be terrified by what he could not know.

Hydration.
Cooling without chilling.
Shade.
Watch breathing.
Watch responsiveness.
Get help from women who had seen fevers.
Ask about water, food, stool, urine, insect bites, others sick, recent injury.

He did not know herbs safely.

Maryam knew what local women used and what she distrusted.

Ruth arrived.

No one had sent for her.

Apparently old women heard fever through walls.

She looked at the child, the mother, Daniel.

“Do not stand there looking guilty.
Guilt is not medicine.”

Daniel moved.

Good advice.

They worked.

Cloth with cool water.
Small sips.
Shade.
Loosened clothing.
Mother seated before she dropped the child.
Questions.
Ruth sniffed the child’s breath, examined gums, muttered.
Maryam sent Erel for another woman who had treated children through summer fevers.
Yosef kept the courtyard clear when neighbors gathered.

The child cried.

This was good.

Then slept.

This might be good or bad.

Daniel watched breathing until his own chest hurt.

People gathered outside.

Too many.

Mara went to the doorway.

“Go away.”

They did not.

She pointed to the can-they-leave mark.

“The child cannot leave if you block the air.”

This worked on three people.

Ruth shouted, “If you came to watch, bring clean water or leave.”

This worked on more.

Maryam did not smile, but her eyes acknowledged the strategy.

Hours passed.

Fever did not break.

Did not worsen dramatically.

No miracle.

No clarity.

Only care.

The mother whispered, “They said you could help.”

Daniel said, “We are helping.”

It sounded insufficient because it was.

She began to cry silently.

He wanted to promise.

He could not.

Maryam put a hand on the woman’s shoulder.

“We stay while we can.”

That was the promise.

Not cure.

Presence.

It cost less than a miracle and more than a sentence.

Near evening, the fever lowered a little.

Maybe because of cooling.

Maybe because the illness turned.

Maybe temporarily.

Maybe not.

The child drank more and kept it down.

Good.

Everyone exhaled quietly.

No one said healed.

Ruth looked at Daniel and dared him to say anything grand.

He did not.

The mother stayed until the child could be carried safely.
Maryam sent cloth and instructions.
Ruth went with them for the first part of the road, criticizing the mother’s brother’s household before arriving, which Daniel interpreted as affection.

That night, Daniel shook so badly Yosef took the cup from his hand.

Mara sat beside him.

“You helped.”

“Yes.”

“You hated it.”

“Yes.”

“Because it was not enough.”

“Yes.”

She nodded.

“That is becoming common.”

He laughed once, badly.

Maryam sat across from him.

“This is different work.”

“I know.”

“No,” she said.
“You know roofs.
Rooms.
Smoke.
Shame.
Debt.
You do not know fever the same way.”

“I know.”

“Do you?”

He looked at her.

She was not accusing.

She was bracing.

“I cannot become the person people bring dying children to,” he said.

Maryam closed her eyes.

Yosef looked at the floor.

Mara became very still.

Daniel continued, “I cannot.
I do not know enough.
I will harm someone by making confidence out of fear.
Or they will delay going to someone else.
Or I will be lucky once and trapped forever.
Or unlucky once and still trapped.”

Maryam opened her eyes.

“Then we make rules before the next one.”

Rules.

Thank God.

Not enough.

But rules.

---

The healing rules began in anger and became mercy.

First rule: no one says healed while care is still ongoing.

Second: if there is a known healer, midwife, bone-setter, older woman, or anyone with relevant experience, send for them first or also.

Third: ask what has already been tried.

Fourth: water, air, shade, warmth, food, rest, cleanliness, and room conditions are care, not signs.

Fifth: if Daniel does not know, he says so first.

Sixth: if people gather to watch, they must bring something useful or leave.

Seventh: no one delays other help because of Daniel.

Eighth: no payment for illness care beyond supplies, and never payment that deepens hunger.

Ninth: if someone improves, credit the body, the care, the people, and mercy; do not make a single person the cause.

Tenth: if someone worsens, do not turn failure into hidden sin.

Ruth added the eleventh: men who say “it is nothing” lose speaking priority.

Hanina added: women carrying the sick must be fed.

Mara added: spectators are hazards.

Yosef added: write none of these where fools can worship them.

Natan, when told later, said, “These are not rules.
They are guardrails.”

Daniel said, “Good.”

Maryam said, “Guardrails still need repair.”

Mara drew a new mark despite warnings: a small bowl, a doorway, and an eye crossed out.

Meaning: care, air, no spectators.

It looked like a suspicious soup.

Children loved it.

Daniel objected for three hours and lost.

The mark spread.

The next person came with a swollen ankle.

This was a relief so intense Daniel distrusted it.

A swollen ankle could be assessed, at least somewhat.
Injury, not mystery.
Fall from a low wall.
Pain, swelling, no obvious deformity, could bear weight poorly.
Rest, elevation if possible, cool cloth early, support wrapping, avoid work too soon, watch for worsening.
Daniel knew enough to help and enough to defer if the bone looked wrong.

The man wanted Daniel to touch it and make it better.

Daniel said, “No.”

The man blinked.

“No?”

“No.
I can look.
I can bind.
I cannot make swelling obey.”

Mara whispered, “Swelling lacks governance.”

Daniel ignored her.

Yosef inspected the man’s sandal and found the strap had failed where it joined the sole, causing the fall.

This pleased everyone because objects were involved.

They repaired the sandal, wrapped the ankle, and told the man to rest.

He said he could not.

Maryam asked who depended on the work.

His wife answered from behind him, because of course she knew.

The solution became a work redistribution problem, not an ankle miracle.
Two cousins owed him labor.
One neighbor could trade an afternoon.
His wife could not carry water for both households and also keep him resting, despite his suggestion that she could because she had “strong will.” Ruth, present by chance or omniscience, told him strong will did not reduce jar weight.

The ankle improved over days.

People said Daniel healed a lame man.

Daniel spent an entire afternoon angry.

Maryam let him be angry until he began sharpening a blade too hard.

Then she took it away.

“Would you rather he remained unable to walk so the story stayed accurate?”

He froze.

No.

Of course no.

But the question hurt because some bitter part of him had wanted the improvement to be less visible.

Maryam saw.

He sat.

“I hate that help becomes evidence against me.”

Maryam sat beside him.

“Against you?”

He rubbed his face.

“Against my refusal.”

“Perhaps refusal is not the only good.”

“I know.”

“Do you?”

He almost snapped.

Stopped.

No anger deciding alone.

“No,” he said.
“Not enough.”

Maryam handed him the blade back.

“Then sharpen slowly.”

---

People came.

Not many at first.

Enough.

A woman with headaches after grinding grain in a smoke-heavy room.
A child with a cut that needed cleaning.
An old man with a sore that smelled wrong and required someone more experienced; Ruth sent for a man from another village known for treating wounds, and Daniel spent the night wondering whether delay had already cost too much.
A young mother whose milk had lessened because she was eating too little and being blamed for weakness.
A boy with nightmares after seeing a beating by soldiers.
A man who wanted blessing for his bad back but mostly needed to stop lifting as if pride were a brace.
Two sisters whose father’s room smelled of urine because no one had admitted he needed help turning at night.

Some were medical.

Some were structural.

Most were both.

Daniel learned to ask, with increasing dread:

Where do they sleep?

Who carries water?

Who cooks?

Where does smoke go?

Who eats first?

Who is ashamed?

Who decides?

Who pays?

Who can rest?

Who cannot leave?

Who has seen this before?

What changed recently?

What are you afraid to say because saying it makes it real?

Maryam became the center of most answers.

Good.

Yosef became the center of object and room changes.

Good.

Ruth became the person who said what others avoided.

Excellent.

Mara became crowd control, mark enforcement, and the destroyer of solemn stupidity.

Necessary.

Erel learned to run for water, cloth, shade, witnesses, or the right old woman without making the request sound like panic.

Valuable.

Daniel became less central than rumor wanted and more central than he wished.

Unstable.

One day, a man came asking Daniel to lay hands on his blind father.

Daniel said no so quickly the man recoiled.

Maryam put a hand on Daniel’s arm.

Too quick.

The father sat beside the son, eyes clouded with age or disease Daniel could not name.
He did not look desperate.
His son did enough desperation for both of them.

“Please,” the son said.
“They say—”

“They say wrong,” Daniel said.

Again too sharp.

The blind father laughed.

This startled everyone.

“Good,” the old man said.
“I dislike being touched by strangers with expectations.”

Daniel exhaled.

The old man continued, “But my son hears hope and loses sense.”

The son flushed.

Maryam sat.

“What help is needed?”

The son began to speak of sight.

The father said, “Doorway.”

Maryam turned to him.

“I trip at the doorway.
The floor changed.
Or I did.
One of us is guilty.”

Yosef inspected the doorway.

The threshold had worn unevenly.
A mat edge curled.
A storage jar had been moved near the usual hand path.
The old man had memorized one room, and the room had been changed without telling him.
His blindness was not healed.
His falling could be reduced.

They moved the jar.
Fixed the mat.
Added a small handhold.
Smoothed the threshold.
Taught the son not to move objects without telling the father.
Asked the father what path he used at night.
Adjusted lamp placement not for him but for others helping him.

The son wept because it was not enough.

The father touched the new handhold and said, “Better.”

People later said Daniel gave sight to a blind man’s house.

Mara liked this version.

Daniel did not.

Maryam said, “It is almost true.”

“That is why it is dangerous.”

“Yes.”

Ruth said, “Everything useful is.”

The first death came in late heat.

Not Tobiah.

Not the fever child.

A baby from a household near the lower road.

The child had been sick before they were called.
Too small.
Too hot.
Too dry.
Eyes sunken.
Cry weak.
Mother half frantic, half emptied by fear.
Father gone for help in another place.
Grandmother already bargaining with God in a corner.

Daniel knew death when he saw it approaching and still did not know how to stop it.

They tried.

Water in tiny amounts.
Cooling.
Shade.
Breast, if the child could take it.
Cloth.
Prayer from those who prayed.
Ruth came.
Maryam held the mother when the mother’s hands shook too hard.
Yosef kept the room clear.
Mara stood outside and threatened spectators with a ladle.
Erel ran until he vomited from effort and came back with a woman who had seen many infant fevers.

The baby died before sunset.

No sentence could carry the room.

No metaphor.

No mark.

No repair question.

No useful anger.

Daniel sat outside afterward with bloodless hands and heard the mother make a sound he remembered from the old world: the sound human beings made when structure failed completely and the body continued making air move because it had no permission to stop.

He wanted to leave his body.

He did not.

Maryam came out later.

Her face had changed in the way faces changed when they had held grief and not dropped it.

Daniel said, “I did nothing.”

Maryam sat beside him.

“You did what could be done.”

“It was nothing.”

“No.”

“It ended the same.”

“Yes.”

“Then it was nothing.”

Maryam’s voice sharpened.

“No.”

He looked at her.

She was angry.

Good.

He deserved anger.

She said, “Do not make the child’s last hours empty because they did not become the story you wanted.”

He flinched.

“I wanted—”

“I know what you wanted.”

Her eyes were wet and fierce.

“You wanted the child alive.
So did I.
So did her mother.
Wanting life does not make care nothing when death comes.”

Daniel could not speak.

Maryam continued, quieter.

“She was held.
Her mother was not alone.
Water touched her lips.
The room was cleared.
Someone ran.
Someone came.
We did not save her.
We did not do nothing.”

He covered his face.

“I hate this.”

“Yes.”

“I hate this more than roofs.”

“Yes.”

“Roofs do not ask you to love them.”

Maryam put her hand on his back.

“No.”

They sat until dark.

Inside, grief moved through the household like smoke with no vent.

The next day, rumor did not know what to do.

Some said Daniel failed.

Some said the family came too late.

Some said hidden sin, and Ruth publicly called that version rot from a coward’s mouth.
Some said the baby had been spared suffering, which made Daniel so angry Maryam sent him to split kindling until the danger passed.
Some said at least the mother had not been alone.
That one traveled quietly.

The healing rules gained a twelfth:

Death is not proof that care failed.

And a thirteenth:

Do not explain death to protect yourself from grief.

No one made marks for these.

Good.

---

Tobiah worsened.

Slowly.

Then quickly.

The room changes helped him sleep better for a time.
The blood did not stop.
The cough deepened.
Some days he sat outside in sun and carved small things for shorter hours.
Some days he could not.
Liora learned to move dust away.
Ruth enforced rest with insults.
The children learned to bring water without sloshing.
Yosef made a better stool with arms so Tobiah could rise without coughing as much.
Daniel hated the stool because it was excellent and insufficient.

One afternoon, Tobiah asked to speak with Daniel.

Maryam stayed near.

Good.

Daniel would not be alone with dying hope if he could help it.

Tobiah sat on the new stool, wrapped in cloth despite warmth.

“I am not healing,” he said.

Daniel sat across from him.

“No.”

“Good.
I thought perhaps you would argue.”

“I would be bad at it.”

Tobiah smiled.

Coughed.

Recovered.

“People are disappointed.”

“Yes.”

“I am too.”

That was honest.

Daniel nodded.

Tobiah looked toward the doorway where Liora worked with Maryam.

“My wife sleeps more.”

Daniel looked at him.

“Since the room changed.
Children cough less.
My mother complains louder, which she says means more breath.
I am not healing.
The house is.”

Daniel’s throat closed.

Tobiah continued, “Is that a cruel thing? To make the house better when the man still dies?”

“No,” Daniel said.

Too quick.

But true.

Tobiah watched him.

“I think no also.
But I wanted someone else to say it.”

Daniel nodded.

Tobiah looked at his hands.

“I was angry at the stool.”

“The stool?”

“The first one.
I thought, if they repair the stool and not me, God is making a joke.”

Daniel did not know what to say.

Tobiah smiled faintly.

“Then I sat on it.
It held.
My old stool had made standing worse for months.
Small cruelty, every day.
I had stopped noticing because larger fear shouted.”

Daniel looked at the new stool arms.

Tobiah said, “You notice small cruelties.”

“Sometimes.”

“Do not despise that because you cannot stop large ones.”

Daniel closed his eyes.

Yosef had said similar.

Maryam had.

But from Tobiah, it entered differently.

A dying man giving permission to small repair.

Tobiah added, “Also, tell Ruth not every complaint is prophecy.”

Daniel laughed despite himself.

Ruth shouted from inside, “I heard that.”

Tobiah smiled.

It cost him.

Daniel said, “I cannot heal you.”

“I know.”

“I am sorry.”

“Yes.”

Tobiah nodded.

“Come anyway.”

That was worse.

Daniel looked at him.

Tobiah said, “Not every day.
I do not want your solemn face constantly.
But sometimes.
Bring work.
Bring no miracle.”

Daniel swallowed.

“Yes.”

---

The household changed again.

Healing without power required more structure than repair.

Repairs had endpoints.
A stool held or did not.
A roof leaked or did not, though time complicated this.
Illness created loops.
Visit, adjust, wait, worsen, improve, relapse, care, grief, rumor, return.
There was no completed joint.
Only maintenance inside uncertainty.

Maryam organized visits so Daniel did not become a public well everyone emptied into.

Yosef organized object work tied to health: stools, sleeping frames, smoke openings, water covers, handholds, shade pieces, safer thresholds, carrying frames that did not injure children, work surfaces that reduced dust.
Erel learned these eagerly.
Mara learned the social hazards.
Ruth enforced rules.
Hanina helped identify households where illness was also hunger pretending to be fate.

Natan began sending people with notes only after asking whether they had already sought local help.
This was progress.
He also wanted to discuss suffering.
Maryam made him repair a shelf first.

The repair questions gained health forms, though no one called them that:

Where does air move?

Where does water come from?

Where does waste go?

Where does smoke go?

Who sleeps where?

Who eats last?

Who can rest?

Who cannot admit pain?

Who is blamed?

Who has seen this before?

What would help even if it does not cure?

That last question became important.

It annoyed Daniel because it was both humble and devastating.

What would help even if it does not cure?

A stool.

A mat moved.

A vent.

A covered jar.

A handhold.

Food for the mother.

Quiet for the fevered child.

A witness for the ashamed.

A visit after hope had failed.

A rule against spectators.

A way to say no to touch.

A way to die without being turned into a lesson before the body cooled.

Daniel learned more than he wanted.

Less than people needed.

---

The dangerous moment came with a man who could not walk.

His name was Asa.

He had been injured years earlier by a fall from a wall.
His legs were thin, twisted by disuse or damage Daniel could not parse.
He moved on a low board with small wheels someone had made badly and repaired worse.
Two friends carried him part of the way and dragged the board the rest, arguing loudly about route, dignity, and whether Asa was heavier when irritated.

They arrived during a roof lesson.

Crowd present.

Bad.

Asa looked at Daniel and said, “I hear you do not heal.”

The crowd quieted.

Good opening.

Daniel said, “Correct.”

Asa grinned.

“I like honesty.
I came anyway.”

His friends lowered him.

One said, “He wants a better board.”

Asa said, “I want legs.
But I will settle for a better board because I am not a fool.”

Mara whispered, “I like him.”

Daniel did too.

This was dangerous.

The board was terrible.
Wheels uneven, axles rough, handholds badly placed, height wrong, weight too much in front, causing tipping.
It injured his hands.
It forced friends to lift awkwardly.
It made thresholds enemies.

This was work.

Real work.

No miracle required.

Yosef crouched immediately.

Erel joined him.

Daniel asked Asa, “What hurts because of the board?”

Asa laughed.

“Everything.
But mostly hands, shoulders, pride, and when fools drag me over stones.”

One friend said, “You said faster.”

“I say many things.
You should obey fewer.”

Maryam asked, “Where do you need to go most?”

Asa’s humor dimmed.

“Outside without asking three men.”

The crowd shifted.

Can they leave?

The mark seemed to appear in the air.

Daniel looked at Mara.

She was already looking at him.

They did not heal Asa.

They redesigned the board.

Not completely.
Materials limited.
Tools limited.
Time limited.
But they improved it.
Wider base.
Better axle seating.
Smoother handholds wrapped in cloth and leather.
A slight lift to clear small stones.
A removable rope so pulling did not require bending awkwardly.
A braking wedge he could control on slopes, crude but useful.
Threshold boards for his house would come later.
Friends trained not to drag without warning.
Asa trained to call out stones before they hit because he could see path better from his height than those pulling.

The crowd watched.

Too much.

Daniel stopped work halfway.

“If you watch, work.”

People blinked.

Mara began assigning tasks immediately.

Bring smoother wood.

Bring cloth.

Clear stones from the path.

Hold shade.

Move spectators back.

Ask Asa, not us, whether that grip hurts.

This last one mattered.

People kept asking Daniel if the grip was good.

He kept pointing to Asa.

“His hands.”

By afternoon, Asa moved across the courtyard with less effort.
Not walking.
Not healed.
But moving better, and with more command over his own motion.

The crowd wanted to cheer.

Daniel felt it gather.

Asa felt it too.

He raised one hand.

“Do not applaud a board as if legs grew.”

Silence.

Then laughter.

Good laughter.

Painful laughter.

True laughter.

Asa continued, “You may applaud me for being handsome despite hardship.”

The crowd applauded.

Mara laughed until she cried.

Daniel sat down.

Maryam looked at him.

He said, “That was close.”

“Yes.”

“Asa saved it.”

“Yes.”

Asa, rolling badly but better, shouted, “I heard that.
I am available for future rescue of your reputation at a fair price.”

Yosef said, “We pay in board maintenance.”

“Accepted.”

The story traveled anyway.

A man who could not walk moved through Yosef’s courtyard.

True.

Too true.

But another story traveled with it:

Ask Asa if the grip hurts.

That one mattered more.

That evening, Daniel went to Tobiah’s house.

He brought work: a small adjustment to the stool arm, a cover for the water jar, and a piece of smoother wood for the children’s carrying frame.
Not miracle.
Work.

Tobiah sat outside.

Liora looked tired.

Ruth looked eternal.

Daniel repaired the stool arm while Tobiah watched.

“Asa came?” Tobiah asked.

“Yes.”

“Walked?”

“No.”

“Moved?”

“Better.”

Tobiah nodded.

“Good.”

Daniel looked at him.

“People will say walked.”

“Yes.”

“I hate that.”

“Yes.”

Tobiah coughed.

Ruth muttered about men discussing rumor while stools needed attention.

Daniel adjusted the arm.

Tobiah said, “If they say I was healed after I die, argue with them.”

Daniel’s hand slipped.

The tool nicked his thumb.

Ruth said, “Careless.”

Daniel pressed the cut.

Tobiah waited.

Daniel said, “Do not ask me that.”

“Why?”

“Because I will.”

Tobiah smiled sadly.

“Good.”

“No.
Not good.
I will become angry.
I will make your death into my correction.
I will use truth to avoid grief.”

Tobiah studied him.

Then nodded.

“Then argue once.
Grieve twice.”

Daniel breathed.

Ruth said, “Better.
But do not count grief like coin.”

Tobiah said, “She improves everything by making it harder.”

Daniel said, “I know several women like that.”

Ruth smiled.

Liora brought cloth for Daniel’s thumb.

Tobiah watched the blood bead and stop.

“Bodies are unreasonable,” he said.

“Yes.”

“Mine especially.”

Daniel said nothing.

Tobiah looked toward the house.

“Still.
The room breathes better.”

“Yes.”

“Leave that true.”

“I will.”

---

The chapter of healings, if anyone later named it, was mostly about refusal.

Refuse to promise cure.

Refuse to make care nothing.

Refuse spectators.

Refuse payment that deepens hunger.

Refuse to let illness become proof of sin.

Refuse to let improvement become proof of holiness.

Refuse to touch without consent.

Refuse to answer before asking who has seen this before.

Refuse to let a dying man become only a failure.

Refuse to call a board legs.

Refuse to call a better room resurrection.

But refusal alone could not carry the work.

So there were affirmations too.

Air matters.

Water matters.

Food matters.

Rest matters.

A stool can be mercy.

A doorway can injure.

A handhold can return a room.

A mother must be fed.

A frightened crowd can become labor.

A body may improve for reasons no one owns.

A body may die despite care.

Presence is work.

Limits are not abandonment.

Daniel hated every one of these because they were true and insufficient.

He kept them.

The house grew crowded with marks, tools, cloth, questions, and people who had learned to bring work.
The chest filled with dangerous objects: net needle, Natan’s parchment, jar shard, goat marks, anti-catching mark, care-air-no-spectators mark, a strip from Asa’s old handhold, a scrap from Tobiah’s stool.

Maryam called it the chest of things not to worship.

Mara called it the box of suspicious usefulness.

Yosef called it full.

They needed another chest.

This concerned Daniel.

---

Tobiah died before the rains changed.

Not dramatically.

In the night.

Liora sent for them at dawn, but by then the house had shifted into the stunned quiet after breath stops.
Ruth sat by the doorway, eyes dry and furious.
Liora held Tobiah’s hand.
The children slept in another room because someone had thought to carry them there.
The stool stood beside the bed.

The room breathed well.

This hurt.

Daniel stood in the doorway.

He had come anyway.

Tobiah had asked.

Bring work.
Bring no miracle.

There was no work that could change the body now.

There was still work.

Maryam went to Liora.

Yosef went to the children’s room to see what needed moving before they woke.
Ruth pointed Daniel to the water jar.

“Cover was good,” she said.

He nodded.

“Say something stupid and I will strike you.”

He nodded again.

Mara, who had come carrying cloth, whispered, “She means it.”

“I know.”

Daniel sat by the stool.

Not touching the body.

Not yet.

He looked at Tobiah’s face.
Thinner.
Released from coughing, but not healed.
Death was not healing.
He would fight anyone who said so.

He did not fight now.

Liora looked at him.

“He said to tell you the stool held.”

Daniel closed his eyes.

Ruth said, “It did.”

Daniel opened them.

“Good.”

The word broke.

Maryam looked over.

Daniel breathed.

Tobiah was dead.

The stool held.

Both.

Always both.

The day filled with tasks.
Washing.
Carrying.
Informing.
Food.
Children.
Cloth.
Space.
Neighbors.
Prayer.
No spectators.
Ruth enforced that one with terrifying efficiency.
Mara helped keep the doorway clear.
Erel arrived later and cried because he had liked Tobiah more than he had admitted.

Someone outside said, “But I heard he was better.”

Ruth emerged.

“He was better.
Then he died.
Learn to hold two things or stop speaking.”

The person left.

Daniel almost smiled.

Almost.

At burial, no miracle occurred.

Good.

Terrible.

Good.

That night, Daniel returned home and opened the chest.

He placed inside a small shaving from Tobiah’s stool, with Ruth’s permission.

Maryam watched.

“What will you write?” she asked.

Daniel thought.

Tobiah:

Not healed.

Room helped.

House breathed.

Stool held.

Came anyway.

Argue once.

Grieve twice.

He said the last aloud.

Maryam nodded.

“Good.”

“Ruth said do not count grief like coin.”

“Also good.”

He added that.

Mara sat near the doorway, unusually quiet.

Then said, “Do we need a mark for this?”

Daniel said, “No.”

She nodded.

“Good.”

Weeks later, people still came.

Not because everyone believed Daniel healed.

Because enough people understood that the household might help.

This was better.

Not safe.

Better.

The care rules became known.
The marks spread.
Some people disliked them.
Good.
Some people misused them.
Expected.
Some people came earlier because a room could be changed before a child worsened.
Some came too late.
Some came for miracles and left angry.
Some came for help and found less than they wanted but more than nothing.

Asa returned with the board.

Not to be healed.

To improve the brake.

He rolled into the courtyard with satisfaction and nearly struck Natan, who had come with a student and a sore wrist from writing too much.

Mara said, “Scholar collision avoided by board governance.”

Asa said, “I demand right of way.”

Natan stepped aside.

Good.

Daniel inspected the brake.

Asa said, “People say I walked.”

“I know.”

“I tell them no.”

“Thank you.”

“I also tell them I move better than before and am more handsome.”

“That part may weaken your testimony.”

“It strengthens morale.”

Mara said, “He understands story.”

Asa bowed.

They improved the brake.

Natan watched.

Daniel made him hold the board steady.

The student asked, “Is this healing?”

Asa answered before Daniel could.

“No.
It is repair of the world’s failure to fit me.”

Silence.

Daniel looked at Asa.

Asa looked back.

“What?”

“Nothing.”

“That means something.”

“Yes.”

“Do not make it solemn.”

Mara laughed.

Too late.

The student said, “Repair of the world’s failure to fit me.”

Daniel said, “Do not write that yet.”

Natan said, “Too late.”

Maryam, from the doorway, said, “Then write also: ask the person what fit means.”

Natan wrote.

Good.

The lesson continued.

The board rolled better.

Natan’s wrist improved after rest, altered writing position, and Mara telling him that excessive words were a medical hazard.

By sunset, the courtyard contained Asa rolling in circles to test the brake, Natan trying not to write more sayings, Mara inventing a mark for dangerous spectators that looked like an eye being chased by a ladle, Erel teaching a younger child how to wrap a handle, Yosef fixing the leg of a stool someone had brought while pretending not to ask about fever, Maryam feeding a mother before asking questions, and Ruth sitting in the best shade criticizing everyone into competence.

Daniel stood at the edge of it all.

Not center.

Not outside.

This was becoming a familiar and uncomfortable position.

Maryam came beside him.

“You are quiet.”

“I am watching.”

“What do you see?”

He almost said too much.

Instead, he tried to answer like Yosef taught.
Like Maryam taught.
Like the river man.
Like the wilderness.
Like the lake.
Like Tobiah.
Like Asa.

“People helping without waiting for me.”

Maryam smiled.

“Yes.”

“People still looking at me.”

“Yes.”

“Both.”

“Yes.”

He rubbed his thumb where the small cut from Tobiah’s stool had healed.

A faint line remained.

“Is this how it starts?” he asked.

Maryam did not pretend not to understand.

“Perhaps.”

“I do not want disciples.”

“No.”

“I do not want healings.”

“No.”

“I do not want people to need this much.”

Maryam looked across the courtyard.

“They already needed it.”

Daniel closed his eyes.

That was the truth he kept wanting to avoid.

The need had preceded him.

He had not created sickness, shame, bad rooms, inaccessible thresholds, exploitative debt, smoke, hunger, rumor, grief, or bodies that failed.
He had helped name some of it.
Naming gathered people.
People created story.
Story created danger.
Danger did not erase need.

He opened his eyes.

Asa shouted that the brake worked and then immediately tested it too aggressively, proving it needed further adjustment.

Ruth shouted that men with wheels remained men.

Mara fell over laughing.

Natan wrote something despite warnings.

Yosef took the board back.

Maryam went to feed someone else.

Daniel stepped forward.

Not because he could heal.

Because the brake was wrong.

For now, that was enough.
