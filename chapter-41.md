# Chapter 41 — The Sign of Friendship

The arrest happened in daylight.

This offended the old story.

Daniel had expected night, garden, torches, a kiss.
Fear prefers inherited shapes because at least they provide rehearsal.
Instead, men came midmorning while Maryam was distributing bread and Yosef was repairing a table wedge.

No torches.

No garden.

No darkness.

A delegation: temple guards, two officials, Lemuel, Talmai, and Barak standing just behind legality.

Yehudah was inside, holding an account copy.

Good.

Not absent.

Not yet accused by shape.

The lead guard asked for Yeshua.

Naama said, “Purpose?”

“Questioning under authority.”

“Object?”

The guard looked tired.

“Order.”

Paper, sealed.

Mattithiah arrived too late and not by accident.
He had been delayed.
His face said so.

Maryam read the order.

Not formal condemnation.
Detention for inquiry into public disorder, unauthorized teaching, possible sedition language, and disruption of sacred commerce.

Rome not named.

Rome present.

Yosef said, “He does not go alone.”

The guard said, “Companions may present themselves later.”

Mara laughed once.

“No.”

Talmai said, “Do not obstruct.”

Ruth stood.

“Or what?”

The room held its breath around an old woman willing to become the first injury.

Yeshua stepped forward.

Not because he accepted the order as just.

Because if Ruth was struck first, every later line would begin with her blood.

Maryam saw him decide and hated it.

He said, “I will go with witnesses walking behind.
If they are stopped, your order will be named incomplete before everyone on this street.”

Barak said, “You are not in a position to set terms.”

Maryam said, “He is always in a position to name who sees.”

Crowd gathered.

Of course.

The guard chose procedure over street conflict.

Maryam, Yosef, Mara, Yehudah, Natan, Naama, and Mattithiah followed at distance.
Ruth stayed to keep the house from becoming abandoned prey.
Erel tried to come.
Yosef stopped him with one look and then softened enough to say, “Guard the return.”

Erel obeyed badly but obeyed.

Yehudah walked beside Mara.

She said, “If you betray now, very inconvenient.”

“I know.”

“Are you?”

“No.”

“Good.
Continue not.”

Halfway to the holding room, a man pushed through the watching crowd, reached for Yeshua’s arm, and kissed his cheek.

Not Yehudah.

Seraiah.

The street froze.

Seraiah stepped back, horrified by his own obedience to someone else’s plan.

Barak’s eyes sharpened.

There.

A sign of friendship.

Not betrayal by friend, but use of a frightened watcher shaped into the old symbol.

Yeshua looked at Seraiah.

The man whispered, “They told me if I marked you, they would know you came willingly and no one would be hurt.”

Mara said a word Maryam would have forbidden if Maryam were not staring murder at Barak.

The guards took Yeshua’s arms.

Yehudah stepped forward.

“No,” he said.

Everyone looked.

Yehudah pointed to Barak.

“That was arranged.”

Barak said, “A frightened man greeted a teacher.”

Yehudah held up the cut cord message from two nights before.

“Pattern.”

Natan began speaking witness names.
Naama named watchers.
Maryam named Seraiah before shame swallowed him.

Yeshua said to Seraiah, “Do not let them make your fear the whole of you.”

Seraiah collapsed.

The guards moved.

Mara lunged when one twisted Yeshua’s wrist too hard.

Yosef caught her.

Yeshua looked back.

“Mara.”

The ordinary name he used for her carried more command than any title.

She stopped.

Barely.

He was taken.

Not with torches.

Not at night.

Not by Yehudah.

But the story had found its kiss.

It limped.

Still moved.

At the holding room, companions were refused.

Maryam stood outside.

Yosef beside her.

Mara paced like a caged blade.

Yehudah sat on the ground and put both hands flat before him.

“No private cleverness,” he whispered.

Daniel, inside the room, was asked his name.

“Yeshua,” he said.

“Called what by followers?”

“Yeshua.”

“Claimed as what?”

He looked at the men.

“Ask what you want the title to carry.”

Talmai smiled.

“Still evasive.”

“No.
Still objecting to false measures.”

A slap came.

Not from Talmai.

From a guard tired of cleverness.

Pain burst across his face.

For one second, Daniel was back under falling wood.

Then Yeshua tasted blood and remained upright.

No miracle.

Only body.

Questioning began.
