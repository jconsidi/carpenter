# Chapter 37 — The Garden

The garden was chosen because it had two exits.

This was not poetic.

It was Yosef.

He refused three other places before approving it.
One was too near the road.
One had a low wall too easy to watch from.
One was quiet in the wrong way, which Daniel understood without being able to define.
The fourth place lay beyond a press and a line of old trees, close enough to the city to return before morning if needed, far enough to breathe, with a slope on one side, a stone wall broken in two places, and two paths out if men arrived with certainty.

Mara approved the exits.

Maryam approved the women who knew the owner.

Yehudah approved that no single person had arranged access.

Natan approved that there would be no writing.

Ruth, who was not coming because there was only so much Jerusalem could survive, approved the idea of everyone sleeping instead and was ignored.

The garden belonged to a cousin of Naama's dead aunt's husband's brother, which in local terms meant everyone had a claim to know and no one had a claim to own the explanation.
It held olive trees, a small press, low stones, bitter smell, old leaves, and the sense of having seen men bring burdens there long before Daniel arrived with his.

He did not want to go.

That was partly why he did.

The meal had left too many rooms inside him open.

Barak's version still moved.
The correction moved too, limping as intended, but damage had entered the city.
Men now spoke of a faction meal, funds, women agitators, a Galilean refusing order, a teacher who said bread was many hands, a cup one could refuse, a treasurer who denied being treasurer, a promised one who would not say whether promise meant crown, table, or grief.

Seraiah carried corrections.
Joazar carried witness.
Shifra carried strategy.
Natan carried too many words with less damage than usual.
Yehudah carried no single purse and therefore seemed both safer and more suspicious.
Maryam carried food and the ability to make a room exist where one had not yet formed.
Yosef carried tools.
Mara carried the authority of someone who would deny authority while using it.

Daniel carried the story.

Not alone.

Never alone.

Still, his shoulders hurt.

The garden was not to decide.

Maryam made that clear.

"No vows tonight," she said.

They stood in Naama's courtyard as evening came down, the city loud beyond the walls.

Daniel smiled faintly.

"That rule returns."

"Because men keep becoming foolish at night."

Yosef tied Daniel's tool roll closed.

"No irreversible work from a single measure."

"Yes."

Mara pointed at him.

"And no martyrdom without consultation."

"Yes."

"Explicitly."

"Yes."

Yehudah stood near the wall, arms crossed.

"No private cleverness," he said.

Daniel looked at him.

"From me or you?"

"Both."

Good.

Erel, who had been allowed to come only as far as the lodging threshold and no farther, looked miserable.

Daniel said, "Your work?"

Erel swallowed.

"Stay.
Sleep if possible.
If not, help Naama with water in the morning.
Do not follow because fear makes my absence important."

Good.

He had learned.

He hated it.

Both could be true.

Maryam kissed Erel's forehead, which embarrassed him and steadied him.

Natan asked, "Who goes?"

Maryam answered, "Daniel.
Yosef.
Mara.
Yehudah.
Joazar.
Mattithiah if he arrives.
I will come to the gate path and then return."

Daniel turned.

"No."

Maryam looked at him.

"Do you say no to protect me or to protect yourself from being seen leaving me?"

He opened his mouth.

Closed it.

Damn.

"Both."

"Then I come to the gate path."

She won.

Of course.

Ruth, from the corner, said, "If all of you die because no one slept, I will be very angry."

"Comforting," Mara said.

"It is not comfort.
It is scheduling."

They left after food.

Always after food.

---

Jerusalem at night was not quiet.

It rearranged noise.

Day sounds folded inward: animals restless, men arguing behind doors, women drawing water later than they should because day had been claimed by others, sandals on stone, distant prayers, laughter too sharp, a child's cough, a cart wheel protesting, coins somewhere, always coins, even in memory.

Daniel walked with the others in a formation that no one named.

Yosef near him but slightly ahead.

Mara to one side, watching alleys.

Yehudah behind and to the other side, which Daniel noticed and disliked noticing.

Joazar carried the small lamp.

Maryam walked with them until the gate path narrowed.
Then she stopped.

Daniel stopped too.

She adjusted his cloak though it did not need adjusting.

"Do not go into the garden to become alone in a way that proves something."

"I won't."

"Do not ask God for clarity you would not ask people to help carry."

He looked at her.

"That's unfairly good."

"Yes."

"Did you prepare it?"

"I am your mother."

Not an answer.

An answer.

She touched his face.

"Fear is allowed.
It is not allowed to become king."

He nodded.

She looked at Yehudah.

"Bring him back if bringing back is the work."

Yehudah bowed his head.

"Yes."

To Mara:

"Strike him if needed."

"With what?"

"Judgment."

"Less satisfying, but available."

To Yosef:

Maryam said nothing.

Yosef touched her hand.

This said enough.

She left with Natan, who had walked only this far to ensure no one could later say no learned man witnessed the route.
He hated that he was useful as social furniture.
Mara told him to enjoy his new category.

Then the smaller group continued.

Daniel looked back once.

Maryam did not.

Good.

If she had, he might have followed.

---

The garden received them without interest.

This was its best quality.

Trees did not care about titles.
Stones did not ask whether he was promised.
The press sat heavy and dark, smelling faintly of old oil and crushed fruit.
Olive leaves turned pale undersides to the night breeze.
The city lay behind them, not absent, never absent, but muffled by distance and slope.

Yosef inspected the wall breaks first.

Mara approved lines of sight.

Joazar set the lamp in a sheltered place.

Yehudah checked the path back.

"Now what?" Mara asked.

Good question.

Prayer?

He had prayed in this life, though never easily.
Habit, family, synagogue, road, grief, table, river, storm.
But old Daniel's prayer had been culturally leftover, emergency flare, intellectual artifact, angry mutter, childhood memory.
New Daniel's prayer had become partly language, partly belonging, partly terror that someone might answer.

In the garden, answer felt dangerous.

He said, "I need to think."

Mara said, "Historically unreliable."

"Yes."

Yosef said, "Think with us first."

Good.

So they sat among stones.

Not kneeling.
Not dramatic.
Sitting.

Barak's story.

Yehudah listened too still.

"What is broken tonight?"

Good.

Of course.

Friends and enemies could want the same clean act for different reasons.

Yehudah nodded.

"Yes."

"You knew?"

"I suspected."

"And did not say?"

"I thought it obvious."

Mara made a strangled sound.

Yosef said, "Say obvious things."

Yehudah nodded.

"Yes."

Daniel felt anger rise.

Yehudah's "obvious" could become omission.

Small betrayal paths.

Yehudah said, "I should have said."

"Yes," Maryam would have said.

She was not there.

Daniel said it.

"Yes."

Good.

Yehudah did not defend.

Better.

"Garden successful.
One obvious thing named."

---

After talking, Daniel moved apart.

Not far.

Far enough to be alone without making others chase him.

Mara objected with her face.
Yosef allowed it with his.
Yehudah watched the path.
Joazar pretended not to watch anyone.

Daniel knelt because standing felt too ready to move and sitting felt too ready to sleep.

The ground was cold through his knees.

He did not know how to begin.

God.

Father.

Lord.

The old words came with too much history.
The new ones with too much intimacy.
The local prayers with community.
The old-world ones with doubt.
He was tired of every word carrying ten rooms.

"I don't know."

Good.

Honest.

"I don't know what You want, and I don't know how much of that is because I don't want to know."

The garden did not answer.

Leaves moved.

He continued.

"I do not want to die."

There.

The sentence lay on the ground like a body.

He had written around it for chapters of his life.

Now it was spoken to dirt, trees, night, God, memory, fear.

"I do not want to be brave about dying.
I do not want to make peace with being used.
I do not want people to turn my body into meaning before my mother has finished holding it.
I do not want my death to feed men who should have fed children.
I do not want the story to decide that suffering is proof."

His breath shook.

"I also do not want to run so far from death that I let others be crushed.
I do not want fear of the cross to make me silent before tables that bite.
I do not want to preserve myself by calling cowardice patience.
I do not want to walk into danger because fear of cowardice dared me."

The old words surfaced: let this cup pass.

He hated the cup.

Not the cup from the meal, chipped and optional.

This cup: a future-shaped pressure, bitter, carried in every story he knew.

He whispered, "Let this cup pass."

The words were not noble.

They were desperate.

Good.

He added quickly, angrily, "And do not let anyone make that sentence small."

The garden stayed quiet.

He waited.

No angel.

No voice.

No strengthening beyond breath continuing.

He laughed once, badly.

"Of course."

Then, because old words demanded completion and he distrusted them, he spoke his own.

"If there is a path that does not spend bodies, show it through people who can correct me.
If there is danger that love requires, do not let me call it destiny before it is work.
If I must choose, let me choose with those who love me, not with fear pretending to be obedience."

His forehead touched his hands.

"Not my fear alone."

That was as close as he could come.

Not thy will.

Not mine alone.

It felt insufficient.

It was honest.

For now.

Commerce? Azriel and Dinah first.

Money? Yehudah, Rinnah, Naama first.

Gatherings? Maryam and Naama first.

Teachings? Natan, Mara, Erel's written note first.

Promised status?

The room shifted.

"Yet the center remains unnamed.
People gather because of him."

All eyes turned.

Daniel felt the pull.

Answer first.

No.

"I want it to carry truth."

Mara muttered, "Liar."

"Does he speak for you all?"

Naama said, "But he spoke truly there."

Barak tried again.

"Are you the promised one?"

The room held its breath.

Daniel did not look at him.

He looked at the objects.

The table wedge.
The fair weight.
The route map.

The broken seal.

The marks.
The people.

Barak opened his mouth.

At present.

For now.
