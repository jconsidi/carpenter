# Chapter 17 — Rumor Has Better Legs Than Truth

The wedding story reached home before the wedding guests finished sleeping.

This was impressive, because several of the guests had slept where they sat, one beneath a table, and one, according to Mara, in a posture suggesting he had been assembled incorrectly.

Rumor did not require sober witnesses.

Rumor required motion.

By midmorning, a woman at the well said the bride’s family had run out of wine and somehow not run out of wine.
By noon, a boy claimed Maryam had known before the jars emptied.
By afternoon, a man at the lower road reported that Yosef’s son had advised the women how to make weak wine strong.
By evening, someone swore that water had been carried behind a curtain and returned changed.

This last version caused Daniel to sit down hard on the threshold.

Maryam looked up from sorting lentils.

“What?”

He had just returned from delivering the repaired cup, which had become a small bowl for holding salt because not every broken cup deserved the burden of pretending to be a cup.

“They are saying water changed.”

Maryam’s hands paused.

Yosef, near the tool chest, closed it slowly.

Mara, who had been using a scrap of charcoal to improve the goat mark on a rejected board, said a word Maryam did not approve of and was not entirely wrong to use.

Daniel stared at the floor.

“No.”

Maryam set the lentils aside.

“Who?”

“Two women near the potter’s lane.
A boy before that.
Someone told the cup woman.”

Mara said, “The cup woman repeats everything.”

Daniel looked at her.

“She said she heard from a cousin of the groom’s uncle.”

Yosef sighed.

The groom’s uncle had been honored with visible wine and a repaired table.
This gave him both memory and confidence, two materials from which many bad structures were made.

Daniel stood too quickly.

“I have to correct it.”

Maryam said, “Wait.”

“I have to.”

“Wait.”

He stopped because her voice was quiet, and quiet from Maryam usually meant the roof had already started to move.

She looked toward the door, then back at him.

“Correct whom?”

“Everyone.”

Mara made a strangled sound.

Daniel rounded on her.

“What?”

“Nothing.”

“What?”

“You said everyone as if everyone is a basket you can pick up.”

He hated her.

He hated more that she was right.

Yosef leaned against the wall.

“A story has no handle.”

Daniel began pacing.

“There was no miracle.”

“No.”

“No water changed.”

“No.”

“I did not touch the jars.”

“No.”

“Maryam did the work.”

Maryam’s expression shifted.

“Many women did the work.”

“Yes.
That is my point.”

“No,” Maryam said.
“Your point is that you did not.”

Daniel stopped.

The room held that.

Mara looked down at the goat board.

Yosef remained still.

Daniel felt heat rise in his face.

“That is not—”

Maryam waited.

He closed his mouth.

It was partly true.

Not wholly.
But partly, and the part that was true had enough weight to require bracing.

He was afraid the rumor would make him into the old story.
He was angry that the women’s logistics had been converted into a sign around him.
He was also, in the most shameful corner of his mind, relieved that the rumor named him because it meant he could fight it directly.
A story praising the women would have been harder to handle because it would not have threatened him the same way.

That was ugly.

He sat again.

Maryam returned to the lentils.

“Good,” she said.

This was apparently praise for sitting before becoming stupid.

Daniel took it.

“What do we do?” he asked.

Yosef said, “First, eat.”

Daniel stared.

“Rumor is spreading.”

“It spreads hungry or fed.
You become foolish hungry.”

Mara said, “More foolish.”

Maryam gave her a look.

Mara amended, “Differently foolish.”

This was not better.

Still, Daniel ate.

He tasted nothing.

---

Correcting a rumor required knowing its load path.

This was Maryam’s phrase.

Daniel hated that it was excellent.

A rumor did not move randomly.
It followed relationships, needs, resentments, entertainments, debts, authority, boredom, and the human appetite for a story shaped better than life.
It traveled faster along some paths than others.
Women at the well did not carry rumor the same way men at the wine seller did.
Children carried it by making it stranger.
Old people carried it by connecting it to older stories.
Clients carried it when repairs left them with gratitude they wanted to spend publicly.

The wedding rumor had several possible sources.

The bride’s mother knew there had been shortage, and knew Maryam had helped.
She had nearly looked toward Daniel before Maryam redirected her.
That look, even interrupted, might have grown legs.

The groom’s uncle knew the table held, had received visible wine, and had the kind of mind that made his own importance central to every event.

The younger cousin knew jars moved but did not understand why.

The children knew they received sweet water and special cups.

The musicians knew they had been fed at a useful time and resumed playing without fully knowing why.

Mara knew everything and claimed innocence.

Daniel believed her, though not because she deserved it.

Maryam decided they would not confront the rumor as a whole.

“You cannot hit fog with a broom,” she said.

Mara immediately mime-swung a broom at fog.

Maryam ignored her.

“You cool the places where it thickens.”

“That sounds slow,” Daniel said.

“Yes.”

“The rumor is fast.”

“Yes.”

“So we lose.”

Maryam looked at him.

“A fast wrong can travel far and still arrive weak if no one feeds it.”

Daniel wanted that to be true.

Yosef said, “And if it arrives strong, we see what it breaks.”

Daniel wanted that less.

The first step was the bride’s mother.

Maryam went alone.

Daniel objected.

Maryam looked at him until he stopped.

“If you come,” she said, “the visit becomes about you.”

“I can stay outside.”

“Your outside is loud.”

Mara nodded.

Daniel considered throwing something at her, but Maryam was watching.

Maryam took a small bundle of cloth as excuse and left.

Waiting was worse than work.

Daniel tried to plane a board and nearly ruined it.
Yosef took it away without comment.
He tried sorting pegs, but the categories became rumor sources, rumor carriers, correction opportunities, irreversible damage, likely exaggerations, and possible theological disaster.
This was not a useful peg taxonomy.

Mara took the pegs from him.

“Go draw something.”

“I do not want to draw.”

“Then walk.”

“I do not want to walk.”

“Then sit and suffer more quietly.”

He sat.

Suffering quietly was harder than it sounded.

Maryam returned before dusk.

The bride’s mother had not fed the worst version.
Good.
She had said Maryam helped and that the women made the wine last.
Good.
She had also said Daniel saw what was needed before the men did.
Bad.
Not false, exactly.
Not helpful.
The groom’s uncle had improved this into Daniel making weak wine stronger because he did not understand planning and wanted his own cup to have been part of something impressive.

“Did she correct him?” Daniel asked.

“Yes.”

“Did he accept correction?”

Maryam smiled faintly.

“He accepted being told he had been honored and therefore need not inspect the kitchen.”

Daniel blinked.

Yosef smiled.

Mara said, “That is beautiful.”

Daniel said, “That is not correction.”

“It is redirection,” Maryam said.

“It leaves the false part.”

“It removes his hunger for it.”

Daniel rubbed his eyes.

Truth work was more complicated than joinery and less satisfying because the pieces kept having pride.

“What next?”

“The cup woman,” Maryam said.

Mara brightened.

“I can do that.”

“No,” said everyone.

---

The cup woman’s name was Rivka, and she collected stories the way other people collected jars: many, mismatched, some cracked, none discarded.

She lived near the potter’s lane in a house with an excellent threshold and a terrible shelf.
Daniel had repaired the cup for her because she had come to Yosef with six broken items and four unrelated stories and somehow left with five stories, a salt bowl, and an unpaid question about her cousin’s wall.

Maryam did not go to Rivka.

She sent Hanina.

This startled Daniel.

Hanina had no reason to be involved in the wedding story.

That, Maryam explained, was the point.

Rivka expected Maryam to correct her.
Correction from the person implicated became defense.
Correction from Hanina, who had received help from Yosef’s household and knew the difference between help and wonder, might enter differently.

Also, Hanina was angry on behalf of practical women.

Maryam valued this.

Hanina arrived at their house before going to Rivka, carrying honest onions and wearing the expression of someone ready to move a roof beam with her forehead if required.

“She says you made wine,” Hanina said to Daniel.

“I did not.”

“I know.
If you could make wine, Maryam would have better onions.”

Mara collapsed into laughter.

Maryam closed her eyes.

Yosef turned away.

Daniel, despite everything, laughed too.

This was why Hanina was useful.

She had no reverence available for nonsense.

Hanina listened while Maryam explained the plan: not a denial shouted, not an accusation, not a full technical explanation no one would repeat correctly.
A story with better handles.

The women managed the feast.

The shortage was paced.

No water changed.

The bride’s mother kept dignity.

The old uncle was served generously and confused gratitude with miracle.

This last part Hanina liked.

“Men do that,” she said.

Yosef looked at the ceiling.

Hanina went to Rivka.

By evening, Rivka had revised the story.
This was faster than expected and therefore suspicious.

The new version: there had been a wine shortage, the women saved the feast through clever management, Maryam saw the danger, Yosef’s boy helped count without touching jars, and the groom’s uncle had mistaken being honored for witnessing a sign.

This was much better.

It was also funnier.

Funny traveled well.

By the next day, people were laughing at the groom’s uncle.

Daniel felt guilty for the relief this gave him.

Maryam said, “Better he carry a little foolishness than the bride carry shame.”

That was probably true.

Yosef said, “And better foolishness than wonder.”

Also true.

Mara said, “The world needs worriers and confused uncles.”

Daniel told her not to improve the saying.

She improved it immediately.

---

For three days, the rumor bent away from miracle.

Not entirely.

Rumor did not surrender; it adapted.

Some versions retained water.
Some retained Daniel.
Some retained Maryam knowing too quickly.
Some became simply that the bride’s household nearly ran short and the women were clever.
One version claimed the musicians saved the feast because they played so well no one noticed hunger or thirst.
Mara said musicians probably started that one.

The dangerous version weakened.

Daniel began to breathe again.

This was when the teacher arrived.

Not the older teacher from the city.
A different one.
Younger, sharp-faced, with clean hands and traveling dust arranged in a way that suggested he noticed how people noticed him.
He came to Yosef with a minor repair: a writing box whose lid would not sit true.
A scholar’s object.
Good wood once, poorly treated recently.
The hinge had loosened.
The lid had warped slightly.
The box wanted attention.

Yosef received him politely.

Daniel stayed near the tool chest and tried to be furniture.

Mara was outside pretending to repair a basket while clearly listening through the doorway.

The teacher introduced himself as Natan.

He had heard of Yosef’s work.

This was normal.

He had heard of the boy who taught foundations in dirt.

This was less normal.

He had heard of the wedding.

This was bad.

Yosef inspected the writing box.

“Old hinge,” he said.

Natan watched Daniel instead of the box.

“And your son?”

“Old enough to know hinges loosen,” Yosef said.

Daniel looked down to hide a smile.

Natan smiled too, but differently.

“I hear he saw a shortage at the feast before the hosts did.”

Daniel said nothing.

Yosef worked the hinge pin loose.

Natan continued, “A useful gift.”

Yosef said, “Counting is useful.”

“So it was counting?”

“Mostly women’s work,” Daniel said.

He had meant to remain furniture.

Failed.

Natan turned toward him with the satisfaction of a man whose hook had found fish.

“Women’s work?”

“The feast continued because women understood the household.”

Natan looked amused.

“And you understood the women?”

Daniel should have stopped.

But the tone.

The tone carried condescension toward Maryam, toward the bride’s mother, toward the practical intelligence that kept whole structures from collapsing while men asked questions at comfortable distances.

Daniel said, “No.
I understood jars.
My mother understood people.
People are harder.”

Mara made a sound outside.

Yosef’s hand paused.

Natan’s eyes sharpened.

“A builder’s answer.”

“Yes.”

“Are people built?”

“No.”

“Then perhaps the answer is limited.”

“Yes,” Daniel said.

This surprised him.

Good.

“Every answer is limited by the material it knows.”

Natan studied him.

The room tightened.

This was how it happened.
A question, an answer, interest, attention, shape.
Daniel felt the edge of the city again: teachers leaning closer, mind hunger mistaken for safety.

Yosef closed the writing box.

The lid sat better now.

“Your box will hold if kept dry.”

Natan looked at him.

The repair was done.

The conversation, in Yosef’s view, should be done too.

Natan paid.

Fairly.

Too fairly, perhaps.
Payment could also be a way of remaining in the room.

He looked once more at Daniel.

“Do you study formally?”

Daniel said, “Doors.”

Yosef looked pleased.

Natan smiled.

“Doors are good teachers.”

“Yes.
They reveal who wants entrance without asking the wall.”

Yosef closed his eyes.

Mara whispered outside, “Bad at boring.”

Natan heard.

His smile widened.

“I see.”

He left with the writing box.

Daniel waited until his footsteps faded.

Then he said, “I know.”

Yosef opened his eyes.

Maryam, who had appeared in the doorway during the exchange with terrifying silence, said, “Do you?”

Daniel groaned.

---

Natan returned the next day without a broken object.

This was unacceptable.

Yosef told him as much by not inviting him fully inside.

Natan smiled as if thresholds were philosophical opportunities.

“I brought no repair.”

“Then I cannot help you,” Yosef said.

“I hoped to speak with your son.”

“No.”

Daniel, inside, froze.

Mara, outside, nearly fell from her listening place.

Natan looked surprised.

It was the surprise of a man unused to having polite intent denied.

“Only briefly.”

“No.”

“I mean no harm.”

“No.”

Maryam came to stand beside Yosef.

Natan looked from one to the other.

“He is old enough to answer for himself.”

Maryam said, “He is old enough to be protected from men who arrive empty-handed.”

Daniel’s chest tightened.

Natan’s face changed.

There it was.
Not anger exactly.
Offense dressed as dignity.

“I did not come to take.”

Maryam looked at his empty hands.

The silence did the work.

Natan lowered his gaze first.

Clever enough to know when a room had turned against him.

He said, “Another time, perhaps.”

Yosef said nothing.

Natan left.

Daniel remained inside, both relieved and furious.

Maryam turned.

“You want to object.”

“Yes.”

“Then object.”

“I could have handled him.”

“Yes.”

“That makes it worse.”

“Yes.”

“He thinks—”

“He thinks with appetite,” Maryam said.

Daniel stopped.

Yosef added, “You felt it.”

He had.

That was the worst part.

Not that Natan was cruel.
He was not obviously cruel.
Not that he was foolish.
He was not.
Not that his questions were useless.
They were interesting.
Dangerous because interesting.
A man could arrive empty-handed and still take work, attention, answers, the outline of a boy’s mind.
He could make curiosity sound like respect and leave carrying a story.

Daniel sat.

Mara entered without permission.

“Empty-handed men are suspicious,” she said.

Maryam looked at her.

Mara lifted the basket she had failed to repair.

“I brought a broken basket.”

“Then sit.”

Mara sat, triumphant.

Daniel almost laughed.

---

The rumor changed again after Natan.

Not because he spread it widely.
He was too careful for that.

He made it denser.

Before Natan, the wedding story had been domestic gossip: shortage, women’s cleverness, an uncle’s confusion, maybe Yosef’s boy counting jars.
After Natan, a few people began asking Daniel questions that were too shaped.

“Is a feast generous if the cup is small but never empty?”

“What makes a household honorable: abundance, or how it bears lack?”

“If a mother sees need before others, is that wisdom or something more?”

Daniel hated all of them.

Not because they were bad questions.

Because they were good questions sharpened for him.

Someone had described not just the event but the kind of answers he gave.

Mara suggested answering every question with goats until interest died.

Daniel said this would create a goat sect.

She looked intrigued.

Maryam forbade it preemptively.

Yosef chose a different approach.

He gave Daniel more ordinary work in public.

Not special work.
Not teaching.
Not questions.
Work.

Repairing a stool leg in front of a client.

Sharpening tools badly enough once that Yosef corrected him.

Carrying water and spilling some.

Holding a board while Erel made the critical cut.

Sweeping.

A lot of sweeping.

Daniel objected after the third day.

“You are making me look incompetent.”

“No,” Yosef said.
“Human.”

“I am human.”

“Yes.”

“Then why stage it?”

Yosef leaned on the broom.

“Because people prefer signs to sweat.”

Daniel had no answer.

So he swept.

Mara watched with delight.

“You missed a corner.”

“I did not.”

“You did.”

He had.

She pointed.

He swept it.

A passing woman laughed.

The laugh was ordinary.

Blessed, humiliating, useful.

Later, Maryam said, “A person who is seen dropping pegs is harder to turn into a wonder.”

Daniel looked at the pile of pegs he had dropped under instruction.

“Is that why you made me carry the small basket at the well years ago?”

Maryam smiled.

“No.
That was because the basket was yours to carry.”

He could not tell if she was lying.

Probably not.

That was the problem with Maryam.

Her practical actions often became symbolic only because they had been practical enough to endure interpretation.

---

The dangerous rumor finally met resistance from an unexpected place.

The groom’s uncle.

He arrived at Yosef’s house five days after Natan, red-faced and loud even before speaking.

“I am not a fool,” he announced.

This was not a promising start.

Yosef, who was repairing a cart pin, looked up.

Maryam came to the doorway.

Mara, present by coincidence so frequent it had ceased pretending, whispered, “Evidence pending.”

Daniel stepped on her foot.

The uncle pointed at Daniel.

“You.”

Daniel braced.

“You did not make wine.”

“No.”

“I know that.”

“Good.”

“I was honored with the good jar.”

“Yes.”

“The women managed the feast.”

“Yes.”

“I said this.”

Daniel blinked.

“What?”

“I said this, and Rivka laughed at me.”

Mara turned away, shoulders shaking.

The uncle continued, injured dignity gathering force.

“I said I had seen something remarkable.
I meant the feast did not fail.
I meant the bride’s mother kept honor.
I meant your mother moved through that house like a commander with bread.
I meant the table held after breaking under my hand, which was also remarkable because I did not press hard.”

Yosef’s eyebrows rose.

The uncle ignored this.

“Then people said I saw water become wine.”

He looked offended in a way that suggested the rumor had finally insulted his intelligence rather than enlarged his importance.

“I know wine,” he said.
“That was wine.
Weak at times, but wine.”

Mara whispered, “A scholar.”

Maryam said, “Mara.”

The uncle turned.

“And you.
I heard you told people I confused honor with miracle.”

Mara looked at Maryam.

Maryam looked at Mara.

Mara said, “I may have repeated a correction already traveling.”

The uncle frowned.

Then, unexpectedly, laughed.

“Well.
Perhaps I did.”

The room eased.

The uncle sat uninvited, which seemed to be his custom.

Maryam gave him water because one gives water even to loud men when they arrive carrying useful truth.

He drank.

“I told three men at the market,” he said.
“No water changed.
Wine ran thin.
Women stretched it.
I was served well.
Yosef’s boy sat on a wall looking as if death had asked him a riddle.”

Daniel closed his eyes.

Mara whispered, “Accurate.”

The uncle continued, “If people want wonder, let them wonder that a feast survived men drinking and women thinking.”

Maryam’s face went still.

Not displeased.

Something else.

The uncle had stumbled into truth wearing muddy sandals.

Daniel said, “That is a good sentence.”

The uncle looked pleased.

Mara said, “Too good.”

Maryam said, “Let that one travel.”

So it did.

A feast survived men drinking and women thinking.

By evening, it had legs.

By the next day, the wedding rumor had bent again, this time toward comedy and respect.
It did not erase the miracle version everywhere.
Some people preferred the old shape.
But the uncle’s correction carried authority because he was exactly the sort of person expected to exaggerate a miracle if one had occurred.
His offended insistence that no water had changed did what Daniel’s denial could not.

He did not try to defend Daniel.

He defended his own competence as a witness.

Maryam said this was often more effective.

Daniel added another principle to his mental list:

Truth travels better when it lets someone keep dignity.

Mara suggested:

Truth travels better when it insults the right uncle.

Both were retained.

---

Natan did not return for several weeks.

Daniel tried to be relieved.

Part of him was.

Another part, the ugly curious part, wondered what Natan would have asked.

He hated that part.

Maryam did not.

This surprised him.

“You like questions,” she said one evening.

They were sorting dried herbs.
Daniel had over-sorted them into categories Maryam had not requested, including useful, probably useful, smells important, and suspicious.

“That is not the problem.”

“No.”

“The problem is people making me into answers.”

“Yes.”

“I should avoid questioners like him.”

“Some.”

“Not all?”

Maryam separated one of his suspicious herbs into the useful pile.

“Some questions are doors.
Some are hooks.
Some are hands reaching in the dark.”

Daniel considered this.

“How do I know which?”

“You do not always.”

“Unhelpful.”

“Yes.”

She handed him another bundle.

“Natan?”

“Hook.”

Daniel nodded.

“Mostly.”

He looked up.

Maryam’s face was thoughtful.

“A hook may catch because part of you wants to be caught.”

He looked away.

“Yes.”

She did not press.

That was mercy.

He said, “I liked the city teachers.”

“I know.”

“I liked that they asked.”

“I know.”

“I miss it.”

There.

Said.

Maryam’s hands slowed.

Daniel continued, quieter.

“I miss being asked questions by people who care whether the answer holds.”

Maryam placed the herbs down.

“That is not shameful.”

“It feels dangerous.”

“It is.”

“Both again?”

“Yes.”

He laughed softly.

Both had become less a doctrine than weather.

Maryam said, “Your father teaches you to inspect materials.
I teach you to inspect people.
Perhaps you must also learn to inspect questions.”

Daniel looked at the herbs.

“What do good questions look like?”

Maryam smiled.

“That is one.”

---

The household began inspecting questions.

This was partly serious and partly Mara’s fault.

Mara proposed categories:

Goat questions: loud, destructive, and probably trying to eat something.

Basket questions: flexible, useful if handled gently.

Hook questions: catch and pull.

Door questions: allow entry, but only if opened.

Roof questions: shelter many people but collapse if badly framed.

Maryam accepted door and hook.

Yosef accepted roof.

Daniel objected to goat on principle.

Everyone else accepted goat.

This led to several weeks of classifying questions in daily life.

“Why does smoke gather there?” Door.

“Who is to blame for the cracked beam?” Hook, unless asked after shoring.

“Can I stand on this basket?” Goat.

“Why do men eat first at some feasts when women worked hardest?” Roof, according to Mara.
Maryam said also fire.

“Will the river preacher come here?” Hook, door, and maybe roof.
No one liked that one.

“Can a story be refused?” Daniel asked once.

No one answered quickly.

Finally Yosef said, “Foundation.”

Mara said, “With goats nearby.”

Maryam said, “And a door.”

Daniel accepted all three because the question was large enough to require poor architecture.

The categories helped.

Not because they solved anything.

Because they slowed him.

A question came; before answering, he asked what kind it was.
Hook? Door? Roof? Goat? Did it need answer, refusal, redirection, witness, silence, work, or food?

Food solved more questions than philosophers admitted.

Natan’s questions had been hooks shaped like doors.

The older teacher in the city had asked door questions with roof weight.

Mara asked goat questions that sometimes opened doors by damaging walls.

Maryam asked foundation questions.

Yosef asked tool questions that became moral after you picked them up.

Daniel began to see that teaching was not only answering.
It was choosing what kind of structure a question was allowed to become.

This made him slower.

Better, perhaps.

Less satisfying.

---

The last flare of the wedding rumor came from a child.

This seemed unfair.

Adults generated trouble with more resources, but children often delivered it cleanly.

The child was one of the boys who had received special sweet water.
He found Daniel near the lower wall repairing a small frame with Erel and Mara.
The boy watched for a while, then said, “My mother says you made the children’s cups special so no one would know the wine was low.”

Daniel kept his hands steady.

“That was mostly my mother.”

The boy nodded.

“She says your mother is clever.”

“She is.”

“She says you were frightened.”

Mara looked up sharply.

Daniel did too.

The boy was not mocking.
He was reporting.

“What else does she say?” Daniel asked.

The boy shrugged.

“That frightened people can still help if they do not pretend not to be frightened.”

Daniel stared.

The boy, having delivered a theological treatise disguised as domestic gossip, picked up a shaving and asked if he could keep it.

“Yes,” Daniel said.

The boy ran off.

Mara watched him go.

“Children are dangerous.”

“Yes.”

Erel, who knew less of the hidden story but enough to sense deeper currents, said, “That was good though.”

Daniel looked at the frame.

“Yes.”

It was.

Not because it protected him from rumor.

Because it made a truer story possible.

Frightened people can still help if they do not pretend not to be frightened.

He wondered which woman had said it.

The bride’s mother? One of the aunties? Maryam? Someone who had seen him sitting badly on the wall?

Maybe it did not matter.

No.
It mattered.

But he might never know.

Truth sometimes traveled without its maker.

That, too, was a kind of dignity.

---

Natan returned at the end of the month.

This time he brought a broken stool.

A real one.

Daniel inspected it suspiciously.

Natan noticed.

“It is broken.”

“Yes.”

“I thought it best to arrive with work.”

Maryam, from the doorway, said, “Good.”

Yosef accepted the stool.

One leg had split near the peg.
Poor angle.
Repeated rocking.
User leaned back too often, Daniel guessed, perhaps while arguing.
The seat was worn smooth.
A scholar’s stool, but used hard.
That improved Natan slightly.

Yosef examined it.

Daniel stayed back.

Natan said, “May I ask a question while the stool is repaired?”

Yosef said, “You may ask.
We may not answer.”

Natan nodded.

This also improved him.

He looked at Daniel.

Daniel asked, before the question could land, “What kind of question?”

Natan blinked.

Mara, who had appeared at the edge of the courtyard with uncanny timing, whispered, “Classification begins.”

Natan considered.

“Perhaps a door.”

Daniel waited.

“Perhaps a hook,” Natan admitted.

Better.

Maryam’s expression changed.
Interest, cautious.

Yosef began removing the broken leg.

Natan said, “At the wedding, people wanted a sign.
Some still say there was one.
Others laugh and say only women’s management saved the feast.
Which is true?”

Daniel felt the old tightening.

But the question had shown its possible hook.

That made it less dangerous.

He said, “Both can be false.”

Natan smiled slightly.

“How?”

“A miracle story can hide the women’s work.
A comedy story can hide the fear and skill.
Saying ‘only management’ can make it sound small.”

Natan’s smile vanished.

Good.

Daniel continued, “The feast nearly failed.
People helped.
Some helped by seeing.
Some by carrying.
Some by distracting.
Some by serving.
Some by not speaking.
Some by being ridiculous later in a useful way.”

Mara whispered, “The uncle.”

“Yes,” Daniel said.

Natan looked toward her.

She stared back until he looked away.

Daniel said, “If you need it to be a sign, you may miss the work.
If you need it not to be one, you may also miss the work.”

The courtyard became quiet.

Yosef’s hands continued.

Maryam watched Daniel with a face he could not read.

Natan said, “And what do you call it?”

Daniel looked at the stool.

One broken leg.
Not useless.
Not sound.
Repairable if the split was cleaned, joined, braced, and if whoever sat on it stopped leaning back as if gravity were a theological opponent.

“Mercy with logistics,” he said.

Mara made a small sound of approval.

Yosef looked pained.

Maryam closed her eyes briefly, which could mean prayer or endurance.

Natan repeated it softly.

“Mercy with logistics.”

Daniel regretted it immediately.

Too late.

Natan nodded.

“That may be a better answer than the question deserved.”

“Yes,” Daniel said.

Natan laughed.

The stool was repaired.

Natan paid.

Before leaving, he bowed slightly to Maryam.

“Your work at the wedding was well spoken of.”

Maryam said, “Then let it also be well understood.”

Natan nodded.

He left carrying the stool.

Mara waited until he was gone.

“Mercy with logistics.”

“Do not.”

“Too late.”

Yosef said, “It is memorable.”

“I know.”

Maryam said, “It is also true.”

Daniel groaned.

Both again.

---

That night, Daniel added the wedding rumor to the list.

Not the old list exactly.

Not the fit and not-fit piles of stones and straw.

A newer list.
More flexible.
Less about proving or disproving the old story, more about how events became meaning before anyone gave them permission.

Wedding:

No miracle.

No changed water.

No public sign.

Maryam noticed need.

Women stretched shortage.

Daniel advised from fear.

Washing jars untouched.

Rumor made water change anyway.

Correction worked through dignity, comedy, and better story.

Natan turned event into question.

Answer: mercy with logistics.

He hated the last line.

Kept it.

Then he added:

Truth needs carriers, not only facts.

That one felt important.

Too important.

He almost removed it.

Instead, he told Maryam.

She was mending by lamplight.
Yosef was already asleep or doing an excellent imitation.
Mara had gone home.
The house was quiet.
The beam overhead held without commentary.

“Truth needs carriers,” Daniel said.

Maryam looked up.

“Not only facts.”

She considered.

“Yes.”

“That is why correction is hard.”

“Yes.”

“Because facts move poorly when they shame the carrier.”

Maryam’s hands stilled.

“Sometimes.”

“And lies move well when they make the carrier important.”

“Often.”

He sat beside her.

“So we need true stories that let people carry them.”

Maryam resumed mending.

“Careful.”

“I know.”

“No,” she said.
“That sentence can become a tool or a weapon.”

Daniel thought of the wedding.
The uncle’s dignity.
Hanina’s onions.
The city teachers.
Natan.
Future stories moving through mouths he would never meet.

“How do I know the difference?”

Maryam smiled sadly.

“You keep asking that as if one answer will release you.”

He leaned back against the wall.

“Yes.”

“It will not.”

“I know.”

“Are you learning?”

He looked at the mending in her hands.

Thread through cloth.
Tear aligned, not erased.
Reinforcement where strain would return.
Repair visible if one knew where to look.

“Yes,” he said.

She nodded.

“Then carry that.”

---

The next morning, the wedding story had become three stories.

In the first, water changed.
This one persisted among people who preferred wonder to work.

In the second, a foolish uncle confused honor for miracle.
This one persisted among people who enjoyed seeing loud men corrected.

In the third, a feast nearly failed and was saved by women, timing, smaller cups, and a frightened boy sitting on a wall trying not to become important.

This one traveled quietly.

It did not go as far.

But where it went, it held.

Daniel decided, reluctantly, that this might be enough.

Not because truth had won.

Truth did not win like that.

Truth needed maintenance.

Like roofs.

Like foundations.

Like doors.

Like dignity.

Like memory.

He spent the morning repairing Natan’s payment problem without intending to.

The stool had been paid for with coin, but Natan had also left a question.
Questions, Daniel was learning, could overpay or underpay depending on what they took.
This one had taken sleep.
It had also given something.

He drew a stool in dirt for Erel and Mara.

One leg broken.

“What fails?” he asked.

“The leg,” Erel said.

“The sitter,” Mara said.

“The habit,” Daniel said.

They looked at him.

“If the person keeps leaning back after repair, the stool breaks again.”

Mara said, “So the problem is scholars.”

“Sometimes.”

Erel crouched.

“Then repair the stool and the sitter?”

“Maybe teach the sitter what the stool cannot bear.”

Mara looked delighted.

“Stools have boundaries.”

Daniel pointed the stick at her.

“Do not make that a saying.”

She smiled.

From the lane, a boy shouted, “Stools have boundaries!”

Daniel dropped the stick.

Mara laughed until she fell over.

Erel grinned.

Daniel looked toward the house.

Maryam stood in the doorway, smiling in the most unhelpful way possible.

Rumor had better legs than truth.

But apparently truth could run too, if Mara gave it a push.

Daniel picked up the stick.

“Fine,” he said.

He drew the stool again.

“Then let us at least make the saying useful.”
