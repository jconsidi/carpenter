# Chapter 5 — The Story Fragments

The first song came back to Daniel in winter.

Or what passed for winter.

He had stopped trusting seasons to introduce themselves properly.
Cold arrived without calendar.
Rain came when it wanted.
Heat lingered in stone after sundown.
The world did not divide itself into months with printed names, school terms, fiscal quarters, or weather apps issuing confident lies seven days in advance.

Still, there was a kind of winter.

People shut doors more carefully.
Smoke stayed lower.
Bread hardened faster.
Maryam kept cloth closer to hand.
Yosef returned with stiff fingers and worked them before the fire until they obeyed him again.

The song came while Daniel was half asleep against Maryam’s side, watching smoke gather in the rafters before finding the small dark path out.

He did not hear the words at first.
Only the shape.

A rise.
A pause.
A fall.

A melody from another world, thin and bright and impossible.

He followed it without meaning to.

Not with his mouth.
Not fully.
He hummed.
A child’s hum.
Aimless enough that Maryam did not notice at first.

Then the tune turned a corner.

Daniel went cold.

He knew it.

The room disappeared for half a breath.

Electric lights.
Paper cups.
Wet boots on the floor of a school gym.
Children in bathrobes tied badly over winter clothes.
A cardboard animal cutout leaning against cinderblock.
Parents holding phones up too high.
Someone’s baby crying in the back.
A teacher at a piano trying to keep thirty seven-year-olds within shouting distance of rhythm.

Daniel had been one of the children, though he remembered this from outside himself now, as if watching through glass.

He had hated the robe because it itched.

He had forgotten one line and moved his mouth anyway.

His mother, Linda, had waved from the third row.

His father, Robert, had pretended not to cry.

The melody in his throat sharpened.

No.

Daniel stopped humming so suddenly that Maryam looked down.

“What is it?”

He did not answer.

The answer was absurd.
Dangerous.
Stupid.

He had remembered a Christmas song.

Not even a hymn he could respect as culturally durable.
A children’s arrangement.
Something simple and sweet and slightly out of tune in memory.
A song about a child in a place like this.
Or not like this.
That was the problem.
His old world had turned poverty into costume, animals into scenery, exhaustion into candlelight, and blood into a tasteful absence.

He looked around the room.

There was no music here.

Only smoke.
Cloth.
Lentils.
Yosef’s tools.
A crack in the plaster Maryam had been meaning to patch.
A jar near the wall, safely away from the edge.
The goat outside making a noise that suggested it had survived another day without moral improvement.

Not a stage.

A room.

He clung to that distinction.

Maryam’s hand moved over his hair.

“You remembered something,” she said.

Daniel held still.

She did not ask what.

This was one of her habits.
She left doors closed after noticing them.
Daniel had never met anyone so able to not ask a question.

He did not know whether to be grateful.

The song did not return that day.

It returned the next.

And the next.

Not always the same song.
Not always songs.
Sometimes images.

A painted woman in blue, face tilted down.

A man holding a staff, usually uselessly.

A baby too clean.

Animals posed as if they understood narrative composition.

A star.

Candles.

Gold paper crowns.

A book with thin pages in a hotel drawer.

An old man in a dark suit reading at a funeral.

Television snow falling on a desert that had clearly been filmed in California.

Claire, years ago, drinking coffee in his kitchen and saying, “You know a surprising amount about Christmas for someone who complains about Christmas.”

Daniel had said, “I know construction defects in nativity sets.”

She had laughed.

He had catalogued the laugh as a good sign and then, because he was Daniel, failed to do anything useful with the information.

The fragments came without order.

He knew enough to be afraid of them and not enough to trust them.

One evening, while Yosef repaired a handle by lamplight, Daniel sat nearby and tried to assemble what he remembered.

He did not write it down.
He could not write well enough in this language, and writing anything dangerous seemed unwise.
Instead, he used scraps.

A small peg for the child.

A shaving for the mother.

A darker chip for the father.

A pebble for the room.

A second pebble for the town, though he refused to name it even in thought.

A bit of straw for the feeding trough.

He arranged them on the floor.

Then stared.

This proved nothing.

A child.
A mother.
A father.
A room.
A town.
A trough.

The world contained these things in unreasonable quantities.

He added another shaving for travelers.

A sliver for danger.

A curled chip for a ruler whose name he would not think.

The pattern became worse.

Daniel swept the pieces apart with one hand.

Yosef looked over.

“What are you building?”

“Nothing.”

Yosef considered the scattered scraps.

“That is difficult work.”

Daniel scowled.

Yosef returned to the handle.

Daniel gathered the scraps again, then forced himself to mix them with the rest of the shavings.

Nothing, he told himself, could be built if the pieces were refused.

This was not true.

Refused pieces had a way of turning up in the structure anyway.

---

The old world returned unevenly.

For days Daniel would be almost content inside the new one.
He would carry pegs for Yosef, sort wood scraps, help Maryam by moving things more slowly than she could have moved them herself, and follow older children at a cautious distance from walls, carts, goats, wells, tools, animals, fires, and each other.

Then some ordinary thing would open under him.

A man lifted a child onto his shoulders, and Daniel remembered Robert lifting him to see over a crowd at a parade.

Maryam pressed warm bread into his hand, and he remembered Linda burning toast and scraping it over the sink while insisting it was still edible.

Rain hit packed earth outside the door, and he was back on the job site, one hand full of a boy’s hoodie, the beam already falling.

He developed rules.

Do not hum unfamiliar melodies.

Do not arrange scraps into patterns.

Do not ask about rulers, taxes, travelers, or old prophecies.

Do not look too long at feeding troughs.

Do not compare Maryam to paintings.

Do not compare Yosef to figurines.

Do not let any thought beginning with *in the story* continue to its end.

This last rule failed constantly.

Because the world kept offering openings.

A group of men came through one morning, asking questions in the lane.
Not soldiers.
Not exactly.
Men with the confidence of being attached to someone else’s authority.
They wanted names, origins, households, obligations.
Yosef spoke with them outside.

Daniel heard only fragments.

Maryam kept him inside and gave him a lump of dough to occupy his hands.

He pressed his fingers into it and listened.

The men asked about family.

Yosef answered.

The men asked about travel.

Yosef answered.

The men asked something that made Maryam’s hand still over the worktable.

Yosef’s voice remained even.

Daniel’s mind supplied old fragments: census, decree, king, fear, flight, massacre.

He crushed the dough flat.

No.

The fragments were not knowledge.
They were debris.
They came from songs, stories, films, paintings, half-remembered readings, cultural sludge.
He did not know what belonged where.
He did not know what was text and what was tradition, what was history and what was ornament, what had already happened and what would never happen because this world was real and not a page.

The men left.

Yosef came in with his face carefully arranged.

Maryam looked at him.

He shook his head once.

Not danger, then.

Or not immediate danger.

Daniel looked at the dough under his hands.
He had pressed it thin enough that his fingers nearly tore through.

Maryam gently folded it back on itself.

“Again,” she said.

He looked up.

She pressed the dough flat, folded it, turned it, pressed again.

“Not too hard.”

She placed his hands over hers.

Together they pressed.

The dough yielded without tearing.

“Again,” she said.

Daniel breathed.

Again was one of the words that made this life possible.

Not fixed.
Not solved.
Not safe.

Again.

---

His language improved enough to make lying possible.

This was an important milestone.

Before that, concealment had depended on silence, confusion, or the general incompetence of toddlerhood.
Once he could speak in whole sentences, adults began expecting his words to correspond to his mind.
This was unreasonable.
No one’s words did that, not even adults’.
Especially not adults.

Still, he had to learn.

Maryam asked, “Why were you crying?”

Daniel said, “Hungry.”

This was sometimes true.

Maryam looked at the half-eaten bread beside him.

Daniel amended, “Also sad.”

This was safer because it contained no details.

Yosef asked, “How did you know the rope was weak?”

Daniel said, “It looked tired.”

This was acceptable.
Everything looked tired if watched long enough.

The old woman asked, “Where did you hear that tune?”

Daniel said, “I made it.”

This was false.

She narrowed her eyes.

Daniel added, “Bad tune.”

She laughed and let it go.

He learned that lies did not need to be large.
Large lies invited tools.
Small lies could be used like wedges: not permanent, not admirable, but sometimes necessary to hold a dangerous gap until a better answer could be found.

He did not like this.

He had spent much of his old life accusing other people of lying to buildings.
Covering rot.
Hiding cracks.
Calling temporary permanent.
Saying fine.

Now he lied because truth had no safe load path.

He was not Daniel, but he was.

He was this child, but not only.

He remembered a future that might not exist, a past that might be stories, and a life that had ended so violently his new body still flinched at loud cracks.

There was no sentence that could hold that.

So when Maryam asked why he watched the road whenever strangers came, he said, “Dust.”

Maryam looked at the road.

Then at him.

“Yes,” she said.
“Dust.”

She did not believe him.

She also did not press.

This made the lie heavier.

---

Yosef began teaching him names of tools formally.

Not because Daniel did not know the objects.
He often knew them too well.
But Yosef insisted names mattered because hands alone could not pass work from one person to another.

“You must ask for what you need,” he said.

Daniel thought of all the things he needed and could not ask for.

Then Yosef held up the mallet.

Daniel said the word.

Yosef corrected the pronunciation.

Daniel repeated it.

Chisel.

Saw.

Cord.

Peg.

Wedge.

Daniel made a face.

Yosef noticed.

“Wedge,” he said again.

Daniel looked away.

“Wedge,” Yosef repeated.

“Traitor,” Daniel said.

Yosef stared at him.

Maryam, across the room, slowly lowered the cloth she was folding.

Daniel realized he had said the word in English.

Not loudly.
Not clearly, perhaps.
But not in the language of the room.

Silence spread.

Yosef’s eyes sharpened.

“What word is that?”

Daniel’s mouth went dry.

He searched for an answer and found only bad ones.

“A goat word,” he said finally.

Maryam covered her mouth with the cloth.

Yosef continued staring.

“A goat word,” he repeated.

Daniel nodded with the grave commitment of a liar who had already stepped off the roof and could only hope the ground approved.

“For wedges?”

“For bad wedges.”

Maryam made a sound suspiciously close to laughter.

Yosef held up the wedge.

“Traitor,” he said, badly.

Daniel flinched.

The English word in Yosef’s mouth was wrong.
Not morally.
Structurally.
Like seeing modern steel in an ancient wall.

Yosef frowned.

“Did I say it?”

Daniel did not answer.

Yosef set the wedge down more gently than necessary.

“Where did you hear it?”

Daniel looked at Maryam.

Maryam’s amusement had vanished.

He could lie again.

A bigger lie this time.
A dangerous one.
He could say he invented it, dreamed it, heard it from a traveler, heard it from the girl with quick hands.
Each explanation would become a room requiring more beams.

He was tired.

“In sleep,” he said.

This was not exactly a lie.

Yosef absorbed the answer.

Maryam came closer.

“You hear words in sleep?”

Daniel nodded.

“From before?” she asked.

The room tilted.

Yosef looked at her sharply.

Maryam did not take her eyes from Daniel.

He had not taught her that word.
Before.
She had found it herself, or guessed it, or known to place it where his silence was weakest.

Daniel looked down at the tools.

Mallet.
Chisel.
Saw.
Cord.
Peg.
Wedge.

Objects behaved.
Names did not.

“Yes,” he said.

It was the first true thing he had told them about the wrongness.

No one spoke for a long moment.

Then Yosef asked, very carefully, “Before what?”

Daniel could not answer.

His throat closed around too many lives.

Maryam touched Yosef’s arm before he could ask again.

“He does not know how to say.”

That was charitable.

Daniel was not sure he knew how to know.

Yosef nodded once, but the movement cost him.

“Then we teach the words for now,” he said.

He picked up the wedge.

The ordinary word.

Daniel said it.

Yosef corrected him.

Daniel said it again.

Maryam returned to the cloth.

The English word remained in the room anyway, invisible and out of place.

---

After that, Maryam watched his sleep.

Daniel knew because he woke once and found her already looking.

Not with fear.

Fear would have been easier.

She looked as if she were listening to a door on the other side of which someone was speaking too softly.

He pretended to wake slowly.

She pretended to have been mending.

They both pretended badly.

In the days that followed, she began asking careful questions when Yosef was out or when the room was noisy enough to hide them.

“Do you dream of people?”

“Yes.”

“People you knew?”

“Yes.”

“Are they dead?”

Daniel hesitated.

“I am.”

Maryam’s fingers stilled.

He had not meant to say it that way.

A pot simmered near the wall.
Outside, the goat complained.
Life continued with no respect for revelation.

Maryam sat back.

“You died.”

Daniel nodded.

This should have been impossible to say.
It was not.
Perhaps because he had been carrying it alone for so long that even a child’s small words felt like setting down stone.

“How?”

He looked up.

The ceiling here was sound.
Yosef had checked it after the last rain.
Daniel knew every visible joint.

“Wood fell.”

Maryam closed her eyes.

When she opened them, they were wet.

This startled him.

He had expected shock.
Disbelief.
Questions.
Perhaps prayer.
Not grief.
Not for the old body of a man she had never met in a world she could not imagine.

“It hurt?” she asked.

“Yes.”

“Long?”

“No.”

This mercy, at least, he could give her.

She reached for him, then stopped before touching.
Asking without words.

Daniel moved into her arms.

He was too old now to be held like an infant and too young to resist needing it.
Maryam gathered him close, and for a while there was only her breath above him and the dull sound of the pot.

“Do you want to go back?” she asked.

The answer was yes.

The answer was no.

The answer was Linda and Robert and Claire and the apprentice and the unfinished work and the beam and the body under it.

The answer was Yosef’s hands and Maryam’s voice and the better roof and the sorted pegs and the goat he hated and the girl who thought knowing was boring.

The answer was impossible.

“I want them to know,” he said.

Maryam understood.
Not the old world.
Not the phone call that might have happened there.
Not the impossible cruelty of a death without goodbye.
But she understood enough.

“That you lived?”

Daniel pressed his face into her shoulder.

“That I tried.”

Maryam held him tighter.

Later, when Yosef returned, she told him.

Daniel did not know whether to be angry.
It was his truth.
But the household was not made of separate rooms.
What Maryam carried, Yosef would carry too, because that was how they survived.

Yosef said nothing for a long time.

Then he went outside.

Daniel thought he had done it wrong.
Whatever it was.
Died, confessed, been reborn, spoken.
All of it.

But when Yosef came back, he carried a piece of wood.

Not a tool.
Not a repair.

A small piece of good wood, saved from something else.
Olive, perhaps.
Hard, smooth-grained, with a curve running through it.

He sat near Daniel and began to carve.

No explanation.

No speech.

The knife moved slowly.
Carefully.
More carefully than the object required, because sometimes care was not for the object.

Daniel watched.

The piece became a small beam.

Not a true beam.
A child’s object.
A length of wood with two squared ends, a shallow notch, and a tiny peg seated properly through a joint that held nothing but meaning.

Yosef placed it in Daniel’s hands.

“For the wood that fell,” he said.

Daniel closed his fingers around it.

The little beam was smooth and warm from Yosef’s hand.

Daniel had no words.

Yosef touched his head once and rose.

Maryam watched from the doorway.

That night, Daniel slept with the small beam tucked beside him.

He dreamed of rain, but not only rain.

He dreamed of Robert teaching him to hold a board steady.
He dreamed of Linda waving from a school gym.
He dreamed of the apprentice standing up in the mud.

He did not know whether that last dream was memory, hope, or mercy.

He kept it anyway.

---

The world narrowed after confession.

Not externally.
Externally, life continued its campaign of maintenance.

Roofs leaked.
Animals escaped.
Neighbors borrowed tools.
Children invented injuries.
Bread burned if ignored.
Water went where it should not.
Men with authority continued to ask questions in lanes.
Women continued to know more than men said aloud.
Yosef worked.
Maryam watched.
Daniel grew.

But inside him, something had changed.

Before, secrecy had been a wall.
After, it became a roof.

Still sheltering.
Still necessary.

But no longer enclosing him alone.

Maryam did not ask about every dream.
Yosef did not ask for impossible explanations.
Neither demanded that Daniel describe the old world in full, perhaps because they sensed that full description would not make it less impossible.

Occasionally, practical questions emerged.

“Were houses better there?” Yosef asked one evening.

Daniel considered the answer.

“Yes.”

Yosef nodded, accepting the wound.

Then Daniel added, “Also worse.”

Yosef looked at him.

“Bigger mistakes,” Daniel said.

Yosef smiled faintly.

This answer seemed to restore something.

Maryam asked, “Did mothers have more help?”

Daniel thought of hospitals, washing machines, grocery stores, medicine, strollers, paid leave too rare, family too far, advice too abundant, loneliness disguised as convenience.

“Yes,” he said.

Then, after a moment, “Also less.”

Maryam understood that answer too well.

The old woman asked, after overhearing enough to earn partial truth, “Did goats exist?”

“Yes.”

“Then it was not paradise.”

Daniel could not argue.

He told them little pieces.

Not electricity.
Not engines.
Not planes.
Those were too large and would only make the old world sound like magic, which was exactly the wrong lesson.
He told them about water coming into houses through hidden pipes, and Yosef reacted with the awe of a man who understood both the miracle and the maintenance disaster.
He told Maryam about light held in glass, and she asked first how often it broke.
He told them that some buildings rose higher than hills, and Yosef grew silent in a way that was not envy exactly, but kinship with distant workers whose names he would never know.

Daniel avoided the story fragments.

The ones that mattered.

The child.
The mother.
The father.
The feeding trough.
The travelers.
The ruler.
The road.
The adult years waiting somewhere ahead like weather over hills.

He confessed the old life, not the possible future.

He told himself this was because the possible future might be wrong.

That was true.

It was also because saying it aloud would make him responsible to it.

---

His plan to become ordinary began on a market day.

He had gone with Yosef early, before the air grew crowded with heat and smells and negotiation.
Market days exhausted him.
Too many voices.
Too many objects placed badly.
Too many people carrying weight through spaces not designed for flow.
Too many small risks multiplying into motion.

Yosef had repaired a box for a seller who argued about the price until the box lid fell off in his hands.
The negotiation improved.

Daniel sat beside the tool bundle and watched feet.

Feet told the truth about crowds.

A man in a hurry placed his heel too near a basket.
A woman carrying jars adjusted her path before collision without looking down.
A boy dragged a rope that would catch.
Daniel opened his mouth, then closed it.

Ordinary children did not manage traffic.

He picked up a pebble instead.

A few stalls away, a man with a loud voice told a story to a growing knot of listeners.
Daniel did not care at first.
Loud men were common.
Stories were a form of weather.
Then he heard a familiar shape.

A child born under strange signs.

A mother afraid.

A ruler warned by travelers.

A danger avoided.

A boy who would grow to—

Daniel stood so quickly that the pebble fell from his hand.

Yosef noticed.

“What?”

Daniel could not answer.

The storyteller continued.
The details were wrong.
Not wrong compared with truth; Daniel did not know truth.
Wrong compared with three different versions jumbled in his head.
The man stitched names and signs and prophecies and rumors into something that made the listeners lean closer.

People liked stories that turned fear into pattern.

Daniel felt sick.

He was not hearing his life.
Probably.
The story was old, or borrowed, or local, or imported, or invented for coins.
Every culture had dangerous babies.
Every tyrant feared replacement.
Every mother hid something.
Every crowd wanted a child on whom the future could be placed without asking permission.

Still.

The shape fit too well.

A woman near the storyteller glanced toward Daniel.

Not because she knew.
Because he was standing motionless, staring like a child in a trance.

Yosef stepped between him and the crowd.

“Tool bundle,” he said quietly.

The words were ordinary.

The command beneath them was not.

Daniel sat.

Yosef packed the tools faster than usual.

They left before noon.

On the road home, Daniel walked until he stumbled, then refused to be carried, then was carried anyway.
Yosef did not ask what he had heard.

That made it worse.

At home, Maryam saw their faces and sent the old woman out on some errand that fooled no one.

Yosef told her about the storyteller.

Maryam looked at Daniel.

Daniel said, “Stories are everywhere.”

Neither adult contradicted him.

This should have comforted him.

Instead, the sentence opened inside him.

Stories were everywhere.

So avoid them.

That was the answer.

Not all stories.
That was impossible.
But dangerous stories.
Public stories.
Crowds.
The kind of help that made people talk.
The kind of speech that made people remember.
The kind of accuracy that sounded like prophecy because no one had watched the crack widen.

He could live small.

Work.
Learn.
Eat.
Sleep.
Fix only what came to the house.
Speak carefully.
Avoid gatherings.
Avoid men who shouted in markets.
Avoid rivers with preachers, cities with teachers, roads with soldiers, hills with crowds, rooms where people wanted meaning more than bread.

He would be ordinary.

The relief of the plan was immediate and false.

Daniel knew false relief.
It felt like a tarp over rot.

Still, he chose it.

“I will be normal,” he said.

The word did not translate cleanly.
It came out more like ordinary, or proper, or the way expected.

Yosef’s face changed with something like sorrow.

Maryam’s did not.

“No,” she said.

Daniel stared.

“No?”

“You will be careful,” she said.
“You will be kind.
You will learn.
You will not chase every falling roof.”

Daniel waited.

“But you will not make yourself small and call it goodness.”

The sentence angered him instantly.

“I am small.”

Maryam looked at him with infuriating gentleness.

“Yes.”

Yosef turned away, perhaps to hide his expression.

Daniel felt heat rise in his face.

“You do not know,” he said.

Maryam said nothing.

This was unfair because of course she did not know.
That was his point.
She did not know what shapes waited in story.
She did not know how crowds worked when they had decided what a person meant.
She did not know that wood could become more than wood in the worst possible way.
She did not know that men could be killed by symbols as surely as beams.

Or perhaps she knew enough.

Maryam came closer and knelt.

“I know that fear can pretend to be wisdom,” she said.

Daniel looked away.

The little beam Yosef had carved lay near his sleeping place.
Its tiny peg was seated perfectly.

Maryam followed his gaze.

“You may be ordinary,” she said.
“But not because you are hiding.”

He hated her then, briefly and completely, with the pure hatred owed to people who are right too early.

That night, he lay awake under the sound beam and held the small carved one in his hand.

The plan remained.

Be ordinary.

But Maryam had damaged it.

He could already see the crack.

---

Spring, or something like it, came with mud.

The world softened at the edges.
Paths became negotiations.
Walls sweated.
Stored grain had to be watched.
Children reappeared outdoors in greater numbers, as if grown from damp ground.
The goat became more ambitious.

Daniel tried ordinary.

He played badly but sincerely.

When children ran, he ran with them until they approached a wall, a cart, an animal, a ditch, a well, a tool, a fire, loose stones, standing water, stacked jars, or each other.
Then he became supervisory and was told to go away.

He practiced not correcting adults.

This lasted almost half a day.

He avoided looking at roofs.

This produced neck pain.

He stopped saying things were tired.

This failed when a rope, clearly exhausted beyond reason, snapped during lifting and dropped a bundle of reeds into mud.
Daniel did not say anything.
He only looked at Maryam.

Maryam looked back.

“Careful,” she said.

“I said nothing.”

“Loudly.”

He tried to help Yosef without turning every task into a lesson in future methods.
This was hardest of all.
A modern solution would appear in his mind, bright and useless, and he would have to crush it before it escaped.

Make a jig.

No.

Standardize the pegs.

No.

Set up a better drainage channel before—

Well, sometimes.

He learned the difference between speaking because he knew and speaking because someone could actually do something with the knowledge.

This, he suspected, was what Maryam had meant by care.

One afternoon, Yosef found him arranging scraps again.

Daniel froze.

The pieces lay in a pattern too close to old fears: child, mother, father, road, danger, escape, return.
He had not meant to do it.
His hands had wandered while his mind circled.

Yosef sat beside him.

Daniel waited for a question.

Instead, Yosef picked up a scrap and moved it.

The pattern changed.

Daniel stared.

Yosef moved another.

Now the arrangement looked like nothing.
Or like a roof plan.
Or like firewood.
The meaning loosened.

“You can move pieces,” Yosef said.

Daniel looked at him.

“Not all,” Yosef added.

That was the most honest comfort Daniel had ever received.

He nodded.

Yosef stood and went back to work.

Daniel left the scraps where they were.

Not destroyed.

Not arranged.

Available.

---

Near the end of that season, a traveler stayed with neighbors for two nights.

He was not important.
That was what Daniel told himself.

Travelers came and went.
Men carried news the way animals carried burrs.
Most news fell off elsewhere.
Some lodged and irritated everyone until removed.
This man had a beard streaked with gray, a limp, and the voice of someone who liked being asked to repeat himself.

At dusk, people gathered.

Daniel tried not to listen.

This was impossible.
Listening was one of the few activities no adult could effectively forbid him without removing his ears.

The traveler spoke of unrest.

Taxes.

Bandits.

A preacher somewhere.

A sign seen by someone’s cousin.

A child in another village said to have dreamed of fire.

A man healed by a holy person.

A woman who saw an angel.

A family that fled by night.

A ruler suspicious of sons not his own.

Stories.

All stories.

The crowd accepted them not as fact exactly, but as material.
They turned them over, weighed them, compared them with older stories, fitted them into fears.
No one believed everything.
No one dismissed everything.
Truth here was not a document.
It was a structure built in public from memory, need, suspicion, and hope.

Daniel felt, with sudden clarity, that this was more dangerous than lying.

A lie could be exposed.

A story could be repaired indefinitely.

The traveler, warmed by attention, began another tale.

This one had a child in it.

Daniel stood.

Yosef, across the courtyard, saw him.

Daniel walked away before anyone could stop him.

He went to the workspace and sat beside the tool chest.
The latch was closed.
He did not open it.
He only placed one hand on the lid.

After a while, Maryam came.

She sat beside him without speaking.

The traveler’s voice continued faintly in the distance.

Daniel said, “If people need a story, they will make one.”

Maryam nodded.

“If there is no story, they will make one.”

“Yes.”

“If there is a small story, they will make it large.”

“Sometimes.”

“If I do nothing, they may still—”

He stopped.

Maryam waited.

The sentence had led somewhere he was not ready to go.

He tried again.

“If I do wrong, story.
If I do right, story.
If I hide, story.”

Maryam rested her hands in her lap.

“Then perhaps story is not the thing to obey.”

Daniel looked at her.

“What is?”

She smiled sadly.

“You ask as if mothers are given a list.”

He almost laughed.

She touched the tool chest.

“When your father works, does he ask first what tale people will tell about the work?”

Daniel shook his head.

“What does he ask?”

Daniel looked toward the courtyard where Yosef stood among neighbors, half in shadow, listening with his arms folded.

“What is broken,” he said.

Maryam nodded.

“What else?”

“What can be done.”

“What else?”

Daniel thought of Yosef’s hands on wood, measuring not the imagined perfect piece but the real one.

“What it can bear.”

Maryam’s face softened.

“There,” she said.

The traveler’s voice rose on a dramatic phrase.

Daniel did not turn.

Maryam stood.

“Come inside before the air cools.”

He obeyed.

Not because he had solved anything.

Because he had not.

---

That night, Daniel dreamed of the song again.

This time the children in the school gym had no faces.
The cardboard animals stood under beams that sagged more each time the piano struck a note.
Linda waved from the third row.
Robert stood to fix something, but his hands were Yosef’s.
Maryam was on the stage, holding a child too clean to be real.

The children sang the wrong words.

Daniel tried to stop them.

The tune became the sound of rain on plywood.

Then hammering.

Then a heartbeat.

He woke before dawn.

For a moment, he did not know which life was the dream.

The room was dim.
Maryam slept.
Yosef breathed near the door.
The small carved beam lay under Daniel’s hand.
Outside, some animal shifted.
The roof held.

He whispered his old name.

“Daniel.”

It sounded farther away than it had before.

This frightened him so badly that he whispered it again.

“Daniel.”

Still there.

But distant.

He whispered his new name next.

Not loudly.
Not willingly.
Almost experimentally.

It answered faster.

He pressed the carved beam against his chest.

“I am Daniel,” he whispered.

The room did not disagree.

“I am Daniel,” he said again.

Then, after a long time, because the truth had become inconveniently larger:

“And I am here.”

Morning entered slowly.

When Yosef woke, Daniel was already sitting by the door, watching the line where the first light touched the threshold.

“What are you doing?” Yosef asked, voice rough with sleep.

Daniel looked at the road.

Then at the tools.

Then at Maryam, still sleeping.

Then up, to the sound beam overhead.

“Being ordinary,” he said.

Yosef considered him.

“That is difficult work.”

Daniel nodded.

“Yes.”

Yosef sat beside him.

For a while, they watched the morning arrive.

No angels appeared.
No star moved.
No voice spoke from the sky.
The road remained only a road, the tools only tools, the beam only wood, the sleeping woman only Maryam, the day only another day that would need work.

That helped.

It did not prove anything.

But it helped.

When Maryam woke, Daniel was sorting pegs by size and pretending not to be pleased that Yosef had joined him.

The pattern was useful.

Not prophecy.

Not proof.

Just order, made briefly by hand before the day scattered it again.
