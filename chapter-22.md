# Chapter 22 — Fishers of Men

The basket full of holes belonged to a boy named Peli, who insisted it had not been full of holes when he left home.

This was unlikely.

Baskets did not usually become perforated through moral surprise.

Daniel set it on the work surface and inspected the weave.

Mara leaned over his shoulder.

"Fish escaped?"

"There were no fish."

"Then the holes worked."

"The holes were not the purpose."

"Do not impose your values on baskets."

Peli watched this exchange with the grave attention of a child deciding whether adults were wise or merely tall.

The basket was old, patched twice, overburdened often, and repaired once by someone who believed hope could substitute for tension.
Several reeds had cracked where the load changed direction.
The bottom sagged.
One side bulged outward, not dramatically, but enough to warn that anything smaller than an onion might reconsider loyalty.

Daniel liked this problem.

It was direct.
Honest.
A basket with holes declared its failure.
No one mistook it for a prophecy, unless Mara was left unsupervised.

"Can you fix it?" Peli asked.

"Maybe."

"Will it hold fish?"

Daniel looked at the basket.

"Not well."

Peli's face fell.

Mara said, "Fish are untrustworthy anyway."

Peli considered this seriously.

Daniel said, "It might hold larger things.
Cloth.
Kindling.
Onions.
Not grain.
Not fish unless wrapped."

"Wrapped fish?"

"Better than loose fish."

"Loose fish are funnier," Mara said.

"Not for the person carrying them."

"Depends who is watching."

Daniel worked the broken reeds free and began showing Peli how to reinforce the bottom with new material.
It would not be a good basket.
It would become an honest basket.
Honest objects were underrated.

As he worked, he found himself thinking of nets.

Holes by design.

Holes by failure.

The difference mattered.

A basket full of unintended holes lost what it carried.
A net full of intended holes held shape by letting water pass through.
A fence kept things apart.
A sieve separated.
A snare trapped.
A window opened.
A wound leaked.
A mouth spoke.
A story also had holes, though people often filled them with certainty before checking whether the structure needed air.

He stopped.

Mara noticed.

"Your face is making meaning again."

"No."

"It is.
Peli, observe.
That is the face of a man being attacked by metaphor."

Peli looked fascinated.

"I am not being attacked."

Mara tapped the basket.

"Basket with holes.
Nets are mostly holes.
Fishermen.
Men.
Catching people.
You are in danger."

Daniel stared at her.

Peli said, "Catching people?"

"No," Daniel said immediately.

Mara said nothing.

This was worse than if she had spoken.

Peli looked from Daniel to Mara.

"People do not go in baskets."

"Correct," Daniel said.

"Except babies."

"Briefly."

"And small thieves," Mara added.

"No."

Peli looked delighted.

Daniel pointed a reed at both of them.

"No catching people.
No basketing people.
No putting people in nets.
No making marks about it."

Mara raised one hand.

"What about a mark meaning do not catch people?"

Daniel paused.

That was annoying.

"Maybe."

Mara smiled slowly.

"No."

"You said maybe."

"I retract maybe."

"Too late."

Peli looked at the basket.

"What does catching people mean?"

Daniel placed the reed down.

There it was.

The phrase had entered through a hole.

He could refuse to answer, and it would become mysterious.
He could answer badly, and it would become memorable.
He could answer carefully, and Mara would improve it into trouble.

Maryam saved him by entering with flour on her hands.

"It means some people forget that helping is not owning."

The room settled.

Peli nodded as if this were obvious.

Mara looked impressed and irritated that the best answer had not been hers.

Daniel looked at Maryam.

She had not come by accident.

Of course.

Peli said, "My uncle says tax men catch people."

Maryam said, "Sometimes."

"Can baskets catch people?"

"If debt is in the basket," Maryam said.

Peli looked down at his basket in new alarm.

Daniel said, "This basket will not catch anyone."

"Good."

"It will barely catch kindling."

Peli seemed relieved.

They repaired the basket.

When he left, it held larger things if carried with respect and not swung, kicked, soaked, overloaded, loaned to foolish cousins, or used for fish.

Peli promised most of these.

Mara drew a small mark on scrap wood after he left: a net with a person standing outside it, arms crossed.

Daniel looked at it.

"No."

"It means do not catch people."

"It looks like a person refusing laundry."

"Then it works for two things."

"No."

Maryam took the scrap, studied it, and set it near the dangerous marks.

"Later," she said.

Daniel groaned.

Later was where dangerous things went to gather allies.

---

The first message from the lake arrived two days after the basket.

Not by messenger exactly.

By fish.

Shimon had sent dried fish, a coil of usable rope, and an oarlock that had not broken yet but was "thinking badly," according to the boy who brought it.
The boy was Shimon's nephew, or Andrai's cousin, or a lake child who had been handed objects and sent inland because children moved through kinship like water through reeds.

He gave the bundle to Yosef and looked around the house with curiosity.

Daniel saw the oarlock and sighed.

Yosef smiled.

"Work came."

Maryam looked at Daniel.

Mara said, "Door."

Erel, who had been waiting for any excuse to discuss boats again, practically vibrated.

The oarlock was not failing catastrophically.
It had wear on one side, a slight widening in its seat, and scrape marks suggesting someone was pulling at a bad angle or compensating for another imbalance.
Sent before breaking.
Good.
The lake had learned something.

Or Dalia had threatened them.

Also good.

Daniel inspected it with Yosef and Erel.
They discussed grain, wear, motion, seating, whether a simple replacement would solve the issue or merely hide the load path.
The answer depended on how it sat in the boat.

"Need to see the boat," Erel said.

Everyone looked at him.

He flushed.

"But maybe," he added.

Yosef nodded.

"Correct."

Erel glowed.

Daniel hated how proud he felt.

The lake boy said, "Andrai said if you say that, he wins."

Daniel closed his eyes.

Mara laughed.

"What else did Andrai say?" Maryam asked.

The boy looked at her with the instinctive respect clever children gave women who asked plain questions.

"He said tell the builder's son that work came, but wonder can stay home."

Daniel covered his face.

Mara whispered, "I like him."

Maryam smiled faintly.

Yosef said, "We will send word."

The boy nodded.

Then added, "Shimon said if you come before the next good catch, bring your face."

"My face?" Daniel asked.

"He said it scares luck into order."

Dalia had not supervised that message.

Clearly.

Mara said, "Your face does many things."

"Apparently."

The boy left with bread, because Maryam did not allow moving children to leave without food, and with a warning not to repeat Shimon's sentence near anyone who sold luck professionally.

That evening, they discussed whether to return to the lake.

Yosef thought they should, eventually.
The oarlock needed context.
Erel should learn.
The kinship obligation remained active.
Payment had included future work.
Practical reasons.

Maryam thought they should not go immediately.
Too soon made Daniel look summoned.
A delay allowed the lake people to inspect, compare, and perhaps send better information.
Social reasons.

Mara thought Daniel should go disguised as someone boring.

Daniel pointed out that he was already trying.

Mara said, "Try with less face."

Natan, who had arrived with a repaired writing case to return and had been invited to eat because he had brought lamp oil, asked carefully, "Is the danger that they will make you into a sign, or that they will make you into a leader?"

The room quieted.

Daniel looked at him.

Natan held up both hands.

"Door question."

Mara said, "Hook also."

"Yes," Natan admitted.

Maryam looked at Daniel.

Daniel answered because the question deserved it and because Natan had named the hook.

"Both.
But leader is worse."

Natan nodded slowly.

"Why?"

"Signs can be denied by showing ordinary causes.
Sometimes.
Leadership asks for decisions.
Direction.
Loyalty.
Leaving."

The word leaving reached Maryam.

Daniel continued more carefully.

"If people make me into a sign, I can point to work.
If they make me into a leader, pointing may become part of leading."

Yosef said, "A finger can become a road."

Everyone looked at him.

He seemed mildly surprised to have spoken so well.

Mara whispered, "Dangerous."

Daniel said, "Very."

Maryam said, "Then we do not go as answer.
We send questions."

This was excellent.

They composed them together.

When does the wear appear?

Who rows at that side?

What load was carried?

Was the boat turned differently after repair?

Did the oar scrape in rough water or calm?

Who noticed the problem?

Who fixes it when away from us?

What can be changed before replacing wood?

The lake boy had already gone, so the questions would travel with the next trader.
This was inconvenient and good.
Distance slowed them.
Slowness could be a brace.

Mara added, "Has Shimon's face been inspected for luck damage?"

Maryam did not include that.

Officially.

---

The phrase arrived before the answer.

Not from the lake.

From Peli.

This was how disaster preferred to move: through children with repaired baskets.

Peli returned after three days with the basket, which still held kindling and therefore deserved praise.
He also carried a piece of scrap wood with Mara's forbidden mark copied badly on it: a net, a person standing outside, arms crossed, and something that might have been a goat in the corner.

Daniel stared.

Mara leaned in.

"That goat is unauthorized."

Daniel looked at her.

"That is your concern?"

"First concern."

Peli said, "It means do not catch people."

Maryam, who had been kneading, paused.

Yosef looked up from a stool leg.

Natan was not present, which was merciful.
Hanina was, which meant honesty would soon become unavoidable.

Daniel said, "Where did you hear that?"

"From Mara."

Mara pointed at him.

"No.
I proposed a mark.
I did not authorize distribution."

Peli said, "You drew it."

"Drawing is not law."

"Then why do marks exist?"

Mara opened her mouth.

Closed it.

Hanina laughed.

Daniel would have enjoyed this more under different circumstances.

Peli continued, "My brother said people catch people when they make them owe too much.
My aunt said men catch women with promises.
My grandmother said Rome catches everyone.
My cousin said nets catch fish and fish are people if you are a fish."

"Your cousin is not a reliable teacher," Daniel said.

Peli nodded.
"He eats clay."

Maryam wiped her hands.

The mark had already become a lesson.

Badly controlled.

Possibly useful.

Dangerous.

Both.

Daniel crouched in front of Peli.

"Do you know what it should mean?"

"Do not catch people."

"Yes.
But what does that mean?"

Peli looked at the scrap.

"Do not make them unable to leave?"

Daniel froze.

The child had gone straight to the beam.

Maryam's face changed.

Hanina said, softly, "Good."

Mara looked at Peli with new respect.

Daniel nodded.

"That is a good beginning."

Peli brightened.

Daniel continued, "Also, do not use help to own someone.
Do not use teaching to make someone ashamed to go home.
Do not use debt to make work impossible.
Do not use praise to make refusal harder."

Peli frowned.

"That is too many."

"Yes."

"Can the mark mean all?"

Mara said, "No, according to some people."

Daniel looked at the scrap again.

A person outside a net.

Arms crossed.

Refusing.

Perhaps too defiant.
But maybe defiance belonged there.

"Maybe the mark means: can they leave?"

Maryam nodded.

Yosef said, "Useful."

Hanina said, "Very."

Mara looked betrayed by usefulness becoming communal.

Peli said, "So if someone makes me carry something, I ask can I leave?"

"No," Maryam said immediately.

Good.

Peli looked disappointed.

Maryam crouched too.

"Children must sometimes obey.
That does not mean every command is a net.
Ask also: who is caring for you? Who is using you? Who can you tell?"

Peli considered.

"Too many again."

"Yes," Maryam said.
"Life is rude that way."

He accepted this.

The mark was revised.

Not by Daniel alone.

This was important.

The person outside the net remained.
The arms became one hand raised, not in refusal only, but in question.
Mara insisted on keeping the goat out.
Hanina suggested adding a gap in the net.
Yosef said a gap made it look broken.
Maryam said that was the point if the net was for people.
Daniel said if the net had a gap, someone might think it meant repair here.
Everyone looked at him until he admitted this was less important than not catching people.

Final mark: a net shape with an open side and a small standing figure outside it, one hand raised.

Meaning: can they leave?

Secondary meaning: ask who is being caught.

Forbidden meaning: laundry refusal.

Mara mourned the loss.

The mark spread by evening.

Of course.

---

Once a mark spread, requests followed.

This was now a law of their life.

A woman came because her brother-in-law kept sending her son to carry loads and calling it training.
A man came because he owed work to a creditor and wanted the new mark declared over his doorway, which Maryam refused until hearing the debt.
Two children marked a sleeping uncle as unable to leave, which was judged inaccurate because he could leave but did not want to.
Erel found the mark on a cart owned by a man known for bad payment practices and said nothing until Yosef asked what he saw.

"A net," Erel said.

"Who is caught?"

"The men he hires."

"What can be done?"

Erel hesitated.

"Not by me alone."

Yosef nodded.

Good.

The mark slowed people.

This was useful.

It also made everyone suspicious of every obligation.

This was exhausting.

A society was made of obligations.
Some were nets.
Some were beams.
Some were roads.
Some were hands.
Some were cages disguised as gratitude.
Some were duties that gave life shape.
Some were exploitation wearing family words.
Some were love with work attached.
The mark could not decide among them.

Daniel said this twelve times.

No one repeated that part.

Mara suggested adding submarks: good net, bad net, fish net, people net, debt net, marriage net, Roman net, goat net.

"No."

"You say no because you fear governance."

"I fear you governing."

"As you should."

Maryam said, "No submarks."

Mara sighed.

The problem came when the mark appeared near Natan's lodging.

Not on his door.

On the wall beside it.

Small.
Scratched carefully.

Can they leave?

Natan came to the house with the piece of wall dust still under his fingernails and a real broken lamp hinge under one arm.

He placed the hinge first.

Good.

Then he said, "Someone marked me."

Mara looked too innocent.

Daniel said, "Not us."

"I know."

Maryam said, "Do you?"

Natan paused.

"Assume."

"Better," Maryam said.

Natan accepted correction.

Daniel inspected the hinge because objects were safer.
The hinge had bent from a fall, not wear.
Repairable.

Natan sat outside the threshold until invited.

Progress.

Maryam invited him after looking at Daniel.

He entered.

"The mark means can they leave," Natan said.

"Yes."

"Do people ask that of teachers?"

"They should."

He nodded.

"That is hard."

"Yes."

Natan looked at the floor.

"I have students sometimes.
Younger boys.
Men who want interpretation.
People who want language for what they already intend."

"Yes."

"Some stay because I am useful.
Some because I am difficult to leave."

"Do you make leaving difficult?"

Natan's face tightened.

"Sometimes by accident."

Maryam said, "And?"

He looked at her.

"Sometimes not."

Good.

The room did not let him escape into accidental virtue.

Natan continued, "I thought the mark was accusation."

"It may be," Daniel said.

"It may also be a question."

"Yes."

"Door and hook."

Mara whispered, "He learns."

Natan smiled faintly.

"Against my will."

Yosef repaired the hinge.
Natan held when told.
Erel watched.
Maryam asked, "Who marked the wall?"

Natan said, "A boy who attends my readings."

"Can he leave?"

Natan did not answer quickly.

Good.

At last he said, "He can walk away.
He may not feel able to disappoint me."

Maryam nodded.

"Then the mark was well placed."

Natan closed his eyes.

Daniel felt pity and respect and caution.

All at once.

Natan said, "What do I do?"

Daniel nearly answered.

Stopped.

Looked at Maryam.

She said, "Ask him what he fears losing if he leaves."

Natan opened his eyes.

"Good."

Yosef said, "And do not answer for him."

Natan nodded.

Mara said, "And bring work."

He smiled.

"And bring work."

Daniel handed him the repaired hinge.

Natan paid.

Before leaving, he paused.

"The mark should stay."

Daniel looked at him.

"On your wall?"

"Yes."

"People may talk."

"They already do."

"Good," Maryam said.

Natan bowed slightly and left.

Mara watched him go.

"I dislike when he improves."

Daniel said, "Me too."

---

The lake response arrived with better information and worse timing.

Andrai sent it through a trader who knew Dalia, which meant the message came wrapped in fish, cord, and commentary.

The oarlock wear appeared after heavy rowing against side wind.
Yonah usually rowed that side, but after the repair Shimon had taken it twice "to prove the boat liked him," which Dalia judged a cause worth noting.
The boat carried heavier load after the good catch.
The scrape happened in rougher water.
The seating itself seemed sound, but the angle of pull had shifted because the repaired frame flexed differently.
Andrai had tried adjusting rope position; helped some.
Mattai said the old oar had a worn grip causing twist.
Dalia said Shimon's pride was still not properly braced.

This was excellent information.

Daniel read it three times.

Yosef read it twice.

Erel read it slowly and then said, "The oar."

Daniel looked at him.

"What?"

"Maybe the oarlock is showing the oar problem."

Yosef smiled.

Daniel felt the particular joy of seeing someone else see the hidden load path.

"Yes."

Erel flushed.

Maryam said, "Then what do you send?"

Not what do you go fix?

What do you send?

Good.

They sent back questions and one suggestion: inspect the oar grip and blade wear; compare with the other side; ask who rows when the scrape appears; do not replace the oarlock until the oar is checked; if possible, mark the oar orientation and see whether the scrape returns.

Erel drew the marks.

Daniel let him.

Erel's marks were not as elegant as Daniel wanted.

Good.

They were Erel's.

The trader left with the message, a small sample wedge showing what to look for in uneven wear, and no Daniel.

This felt like success.

Until the next morning, when two boys arrived from the lake.

Not with work.

With themselves.

Andrai had not sent them, they admitted after Maryam asked the correct number of questions.
Shimon had not sent them.
Dalia would be furious.
They had come because they wanted to learn "the repair questions."

Daniel sat down.

Maryam looked at the boys.

Ages perhaps thirteen and fourteen.
One bold, one trying to be bold enough not to be left behind.
Both carrying little food, no proper permission, and the moral confidence of boys who had converted curiosity into journey.

"Can you leave?" Mara asked.

They blinked.

"What?"

She pointed to the new mark scratched on a board near the doorway.

"Can you leave? Go home?"

"We came to learn."

"That was not the question."

The bold one, whose name was Sela, frowned.

"We can leave."

"Then leave," Mara said.

Daniel looked at her.

She did not look back.

The quieter one, Rafi, shifted.

"We cannot go back yet."

Maryam stepped closer.

"Why?"

Sela said, "Because then we failed."

Ah.

Net.

Not Daniel's net, perhaps.

But a net.

Yosef said, "Who knows you came?"

Silence.

Maryam closed her eyes.

Daniel stood.

"No."

Sela looked at him.

"No?"

"No.
You do not learn here by making your mothers afraid."

Rafi's face changed.

Sela hardened.

"We are not children."

"You traveled like children."

The words were sharp.

Too sharp?

Maybe.

Maryam did not stop him.

Daniel continued, "You left without permission, without enough food, without telling the people who carry the result.
That is not apprenticeship.
That is stealing worry."

The phrase landed.

Mara whispered, "Good."

Maryam whispered, "Careful."

Daniel breathed.

"Eat.
Rest.
Then you go home with a message.
If your households agree, if there is work, if Dalia does not murder you first, then perhaps you come again properly."

Sela looked humiliated.

Rafi looked relieved.

Good.

Humiliation could become stubbornness.
Relief could become truth.

Maryam gave them food.

Yosef inspected their sandals because apparently all moral crises ended at sandals.
One strap was near failure.
He repaired it without comment.
This made Sela look more ashamed than any lecture had.

Mara sat beside them while they ate.

"You should know," she said, "Dalia will know before you arrive."

Sela looked alarmed.

"How?"

"Women."

Rafi nodded as if this explained much.

Daniel wrote a message to Dalia and Andrai:

They came.
They are safe.
They are being sent back.
They were foolish, not harmed.
If they come again, send permission, work, and who carries the result.

Maryam added a line Daniel did not see.

Yosef added a note about the sandal strap.

Mara added a drawing of the can-they-leave mark.

Daniel objected.

Maryam allowed it.

The boys left with an older trader after noon.

Before leaving, Rafi asked, "What are the repair questions?"

Daniel almost refused.

Then said, "First question: who is left carrying the result?"

Rafi nodded.

Sela looked away.

They left.

Daniel watched until they disappeared.

Mara stood beside him.

"They followed."

"Yes."

"You turned around."

"Yes."

"Good."

He hated that she was right.

---

Dalia's response arrived faster than seemed physically possible.

It contained no fish.

This was ominous.

It contained a message, carried by a woman named Tamar who delivered it with visible enjoyment.

Dalia wrote, or more likely dictated:

The boys returned.
Mothers angry.
Good.
Sela now knows sandals are moral objects.
Rafi cried before his mother could decide whether to strike him, clever.
If you teach repair questions, teach mothers first or do not teach.
Boys are holes with legs.
Andrai says the oar is guilty.
Shimon denies pride but adjusted grip.
Boat improved.
Do not come yet unless called.
If boys come again without permission, keep them and send us better ones.

Daniel read this twice.

Mara demanded the "boys are holes with legs" line three times.

Maryam smiled in a way that made Daniel think she had written something similar.

Yosef approved of sandals as moral objects.

Erel looked nervous.

Daniel said, "You are not a hole with legs."

Erel said, "Thank you?"

Mara said, "Do not be too sure."

The important line was clear.

Teach mothers first or do not teach.

It should have been obvious.

Of course.

If boys learned questions that unsettled work, obligation, debt, repair, and authority, the people who carried consequences needed to know first.
Otherwise teaching became another form of catching: exciting the young, burdening the households, leaving women to manage aftermath.

Daniel put the message in the chest.

Not because it was dangerous.

Because it was correct.

---

The first proper lesson happened a week later.

Not at the lake.

At Hanina's house.

Maryam arranged it, which meant it was real before Daniel understood it.

Hanina, Dalia's cousin Tamar still visiting, two women from neighboring households, Erel, Mara, and three children old enough to hold tools without weaponizing them gathered in the courtyard.
Yosef brought a broken stool, a cracked basket, a worn sandal, a small roof reed section, and a hinge.

Daniel thought he was there to help set up.

Then Maryam said, "Begin."

He stared.

"No."

"Yes."

"No."

"Women first," she said.

He looked at Hanina.

Hanina crossed her arms.

"I brought onions."

This apparently settled authority.

Daniel began badly, which Maryam approved of with her eyes.

He did not give teachings.

He gave objects.

Stool.

Basket.

Sandal.

Roof reed.

Hinge.

For each object, he asked:

What is broken?

What can be done?

What can it bear?

Who uses it?

Who fixes it after?

Who carries the result?

Can they leave?

That last question did not fit every object.
Good.
They discussed why.
A sandal could leave if on a foot.
A hinge could let others leave.
A basket might make someone carry too much.
A roof reed could trap smoke if arranged badly.
The stool carried whoever sat, but also the person asked to repair it each time someone leaned back.

The children liked the stool.

Mara liked the bad questions.

Hanina liked who carries the result.

Tamar liked can they leave and immediately applied it to marriage arrangements, labor debts, fishing apprenticeships, and a neighbor's impossible mother-in-law.

Daniel mostly held objects and tried not to become central.

Maryam made others answer.

Yosef corrected technical details.

Erel demonstrated a hinge and forgot one step, then corrected himself.

Mara drew marks in dirt, including the can-they-leave mark, spider-hand, water path, split circle, goat, and a new provisional mark for "ask who carries it" that looked unfortunately like a person crushed under a basket.

"No," Daniel said.

Hanina said, "Accurate."

Maryam said, "Too accusing."

Mara revised it into two people carrying a basket between them.

Better.

By the end, no one had become a follower.

No one had left home.

No one had called Daniel teacher without being corrected by Maryam, who said, "The objects are teaching.
We are learning together."

This was not fully true.

But it was a useful truth shaped for carrying.

Daniel accepted it uneasily.

Afterward, Hanina said, "This should travel."

Daniel's stomach tightened.

Maryam said, "Slowly."

Yosef said, "With work."

Mara said, "And marks."

Tamar said, "And mothers."

Everyone looked at Daniel.

He thought of the lake boys.
Dalia.
Andrai.
Nets.
Holes.
Catching.
Turning.

"Only where there is a room," he said.

Maryam smiled.

"Good."

---

The phrase did not become fishers of men.

Not yet.

But something near it began to move.

People said:

The builder's household teaches repair questions.

No, the women teach them.

No, the objects teach them.

No, the questions are for households.

No, the marks show what to ask.

No, it began with a goat.

This last version spread fastest.

Daniel no longer fought goat-origin stories unless lives were at stake.

The lake sent another message: the oar was indeed the problem.
Erel celebrated so intensely that Mara marked him with suspicious goat.
Dalia requested the repair questions "for women first, boys later, men if they survive." Maryam began planning a journey she did not call a journey.
Yosef inspected tools.
Daniel pretended not to notice.

Natan came with two young students.

He stopped outside.

"They asked to come," he said.
"I told them they could leave before I did."

Good.

Maryam asked the students whether this was true.

They said yes.

Mara asked if they had eaten.

One had not.

No lesson began until food happened.

This became a rule immediately.

No questions before food if hunger was present.

Natan accepted this with an expression suggesting he was learning how much philosophy had been poorly timed.

During that lesson, the can-they-leave mark became clearer.
One of Natan's students said, "If they cannot leave, perhaps it is not teaching but capture."

Silence.

Daniel felt the old phrase flare.

Catching people.

He said, "Then do not call capture teaching."

Natan wrote that down.

Daniel said, "Do not write that down."

Natan looked up.

"It should be carried."

Maryam said, "Then carry it with the warning."

Natan waited.

Maryam said, "Some people must stay for love, duty, children, debt honestly owed, work honestly shared.
Leaving is not the only sign of freedom."

Natan wrote more slowly.

Daniel said, "And staying is not proof of consent."

Natan closed his eyes.

Mara whispered, "Too many."

Daniel nodded.

"Yes."

The students looked overwhelmed.

Good.

Better overwhelmed than recruited.

At the end, one student asked Daniel, "Should we come again?"

Daniel almost answered.

Then turned around.

Literally.

Mara laughed.

He looked back at them.

"Why?"

The student blinked.

"To learn."

"For what work?"

The student did not know.

Daniel said, "Find that first.
Then come with it."

Natan smiled faintly.

Mara murmured, "Following needs turning."

The student heard.

"What?"

Daniel said, "If you follow someone, they should turn around and ask why."

The student considered.

"What if they do not?"

Maryam said, "Then be careful."

Yosef said, "And eat before going too far."

This also became part of the saying, unfortunately.

---

By the time the second lake journey was arranged, the problem had changed.

Daniel was no longer merely afraid that fishermen would follow him.

He was afraid they had already begun following something he had helped make.

Not him.

Good.

Not exactly.

Less good.

The repair questions, marks, and refusal to catch people had begun forming a practice.
Practices attracted people who liked usefulness.
Usefulness attracted repetition.
Repetition attracted authority.
Authority attracted men who wanted to stand near it and name it.

Maryam saw this.

Yosef saw it.

Mara saw it and said, "You accidentally built a small net for not catching people."

Daniel sat down.

"That is exactly the problem."

"I know."

"Do not say things like that."

"Then stop doing them."

"I am trying."

Maryam said, "Nets are not evil because they catch.
They are evil when they catch what should not be caught."

Daniel looked at her.

She continued, "A net for fish feeds.
A net over a doorway traps.
A net between people may hold them from falling or keep them from leaving."

"How do we know which?"

"We ask."

"The questions become the net."

"Then we ask who can cut them."

Daniel stared.

Mara whispered, "That is good."

Yosef nodded.

"A good net has a knife nearby."

Daniel looked at him.

"What?"

"If a net tangles a man in water, you cut it.
You do not admire the knot."

The room went quiet.

Yes.

That.

Daniel said, "Then every teaching needs a knife."

Mara brightened.

Maryam said, "Not literally."

Mara dimmed.

Natan, present with a hinge, said, "A way to refuse."

"Yes," Daniel said.
"A way to correct.
A way to leave.
A way to say the teaching failed here."

Hanina said, "And someone allowed to say it."

Maryam said, "Not only allowed.
Expected."

Yosef said, "Then teach failure with the repair."

Daniel felt something settle.

Not safely.

Structurally.

A practice that could not be corrected was a trap.

A teacher who could not be refused was a net.

A follower who could not go home was caught.

A saying that could not be tested had become ornament or weapon.

When they went to the lake, this would go with them.

Not as doctrine.

As knife.

---

They returned to the lake with Maryam, Yosef, Daniel, Erel, and, this time, Mara.

This required negotiation.

Mara argued she had become necessary to mark governance, anti-catching protocol, and Daniel's face.
Maryam argued that necessity was a dangerous word.
Yosef argued that Mara should not go unless her household agreed.
Mara revealed that her aunt was already traveling partway to visit kin and had agreed, which meant she had arranged the structure before presenting the case.

Maryam looked almost proud.

Almost.

Mara came.

Daniel objected weakly.

She said, "Following needs turning.
I am here to make sure you turn."

That was hard to refute.

The road smelled of fish before Daniel was ready.

Dalia greeted Mara by inspecting her head to toe.

"You are the mark girl."

Mara stood straighter.

"Depends which mark."

Dalia smiled.

"You will do."

This was terrifying.

Shimon greeted Daniel by shouting, "Luck face!"

Dalia struck him with a cloth.

Daniel said, "Work face."

Shimon considered.

"Less catchy."

"Good."

Andrai came from the nets, saw Mara, and looked at Daniel.

"Reinforcements?"

"Inspection," Mara said.

Andrai bowed slightly.

"Then we are in danger."

"Probably."

Yonah said, from behind a net, "Good."

The lake had changed and not changed.

The boat repair had held better after the oar adjustment.
Erel's marks had been used and improved by Mattai, who claimed he had always known the oar was suspect.
Shimon denied this loudly.
Dalia had begun using the repair questions in household disputes, which made several men uneasy and several women more direct.

The two runaway boys, Sela and Rafi, appeared at a distance with their mothers.

Good.

Permission.

Work.

Witness.

Dalia had arranged the first proper lake lesson.

Women first.

Men close enough to hear but far enough to deny learning.

Boys where mothers could see them.

Objects in the center: net, oar, basket, debt tally, torn sail, sandal, fish hook, cracked jar, child-sized carrying frame.

Daniel saw the arrangement and nearly panicked.

This was too much like teaching.

Maryam touched his arm.

"Objects first."

He nodded.

Mara whispered, "Turn around often."

He did.

The lesson began with Dalia, not Daniel.

She lifted the oar.

"This lied through the oarlock."

Shimon said, "It did not lie.
It wore."

Dalia looked at him.

"Lying by wear."

The women laughed.

Daniel said, "What is broken?"

"The grip," Erel said.

"The habit," Mara added.

"The rowing," Yonah said.

"The pride," Dalia said.

"The oar," Shimon insisted.

Yosef nodded.

"All may be true.
Which can be repaired first?"

That grounded them.

Object, habit, person, story.

They moved through the questions.

What is broken?

What can be done?

What can it bear?

Who uses it?

Who fixes it after?

Who carries the result?

Can they leave?

What cuts the net if it catches wrongly?

The last question caused silence.

Good.

Dalia pointed at Sela.

"If you come inland without telling me again, what cuts the net?"

Sela swallowed.

"My mother."

His mother said, "And your feet carrying you home."

Rafi's mother said, "And food for the road if permission is given."

Good.

A boy asked, "Can a man leave his father if his father makes him fish and he hates it?"

The air tightened.

Real question.

Not object question.

Room question.

Daniel did not answer.

He turned.

Maryam looked at the boy, then at the adults, then at Dalia.

Dalia said, "Who asks?"

The boy looked down.

No one moved.

His mother said, "My son."

The boy's father, standing back with crossed arms, looked as if a hook had entered him.

Danger.

Maryam stepped in slowly.

"Then this is not for everyone here."

Dalia nodded.

"Later.
With those who carry it."

The boy flushed but looked relieved.

The father looked angry, then uncertain.

The lesson continued.

Not because the question was ignored.

Because it was protected.

Daniel learned something then: not every good question should be answered in the room where it appears.

Some needed a smaller room before truth could avoid becoming spectacle.

Mara wrote that in dirt and erased it before he could object.

At the end, no one pledged anything.

No one left boats.

No one followed Daniel down the shore.

Instead, several people stayed to examine objects.
The mothers of Sela and Rafi argued with their sons about permission and work.
Yonah asked Erel about the marks.
Shimon tried to rename the anti-catching mark "free fish," was overruled by everyone, and accepted defeat loudly.
Natan, who had not come but whose habits haunted the day, would have called it successful teaching.

Daniel called it work that had not failed yet.

---

The boy who hated fishing came with his parents at dusk.

His name was Elam.

He stood between a mother with tired eyes and a father whose anger had been braced for so long it had become posture.
Dalia came too.
Maryam.
Yosef.
Daniel.
Mara sat at a distance but within sight, because Elam had asked if the mark girl could stay.

This surprised Mara into silence.

The father said, "He is my son."

The mother said, "He vomits every time the boat goes far."

Elam looked at the ground.

The father said, "Boys vomit."

Dalia said, "Men too."

The father glared.

Yosef placed an oar, a net needle, and a small repaired stool between them.

Objects.

Good.

Maryam asked Elam, "What is broken?"

Elam whispered, "Me."

"No," Daniel said before he could stop himself.

Everyone looked.

Too sharp?

Maybe.

But no.

He softened.

"No.
Begin again."

Elam's eyes filled.

"I cannot do the boat."

The father said, "Won't."

Maryam looked at him.

He stopped.

Yosef asked, "What can be done?"

Silence.

Mara said, unexpectedly gentle, "Not all work near fish is in boats."

Dalia nodded.

"Net mending.
Salting.
Counting.
Repair.
Trade.
Baskets.
Lines.
Smoke.
Markets."

The father looked trapped between pride and practicality.

"No."

Good.

True.

Daniel continued, "So I should not decide."

That surprised him.

It surprised everyone.

Maryam's face softened.

Daniel looked at the father.

"But if a support cracks whenever used, you inspect whether the load belongs there."

Yosef said, "And whether another support can carry better."

The mother said, "He mends nets well."

Elam looked up.

The father frowned.

"He is slow."

Dalia said, "Slow nets catch more than vomited boats."

Mara whispered, "Dalia."

The father almost smiled.

Almost.

The conversation did not resolve neatly.
Real family structures rarely did.
But by the end, Elam would spend two weeks on shore work under Mattai's eye, with one short boat trip only if he agreed and with his father present not to shame him.
If he still could not do boat work, other work would be counted.
Counted mattered.
Invisible work became traps when not counted.

Maryam made the father repeat the arrangement.

Dalia made him repeat it louder.

Mara showed Elam the can-they-leave mark and added a smaller mark beside it: not leave forever, but step out before drowning.

Daniel thought that was too specific.

Elam understood.

That mattered more.

After they left, Daniel sat on the shore and shook.

Mara sat beside him.

"That was catching-adjacent."

"Yes."

"But you turned."

"Yes."

"And gave decision back."

"Maybe."

"Mostly."

He looked at her.

She nudged his shoulder.

"Mostly is good.
For fish people."

He laughed.

The lake moved in darkness.

---

On the final morning, Andrai asked the question Daniel had been avoiding.

Not directly.

Directly would have been easier.

They were mending a net with Mattai, Erel, Mara, and two of the lake boys.
The work was quiet, repetitive, tolerable.
Daniel held.
Andrai knotted.
Mara inspected holes for moral character.

Andrai said, "Some of us may come inland for the next room lesson."

Daniel's hands tightened.

"Some?"

"And me."

There.

Not follow.

Come.

Still dangerous.

"For what work?" Daniel asked.

Andrai smiled.

"Good.
You turned."

Daniel did not smile.

Andrai sobered.

"Elam.
Debt tally marks.
Boat repair questions.

Andrai laughed.

"No."

"Then bring a knife."

For now.

---

They left the lake with fish, rope, two better oar measurements, a revised mark, an invitation limited by work, and more relationships than Daniel wanted.

Mara looked shocked.

Dalia said, "You ask sharp.
Keep it sharp.
Do not stab for pleasure."

Mara nodded solemnly.

Shimon shouted something about luck face and was struck again with cloth.

Yonah said, "Rope," to Daniel.

Daniel said, "Rope."

This had become meaningful despite containing no meaning Daniel could translate.

Andrai clasped his forearm.

"If work comes."

"If work comes."

"With a knife."

"Yes."

"Practice knots.
Ugly first."

Not followers.

Not disciples.

People.

He held that word carefully.

"You know," she said, "if people keep coming for work, other people will say they are following you."

"I know."

"And if you keep turning around, they may like that more."

"I know."

"Still?"

Mara nodded.

"Good."

"Good."

They walked.

Behind them, the lake continued being a lake, full of fish with no moral center, boats compromising with drowning, nets made mostly of holes, and people trying not to catch one another while still bringing food to shore.

Ahead, home waited.

And with it, work.

Always work.

Daniel touched the net needle in his pouch.

A tool for making holes useful.

A dangerous gift.

He would put it back in the chest.

Not to hide it.

Yosef followed with tools.
Erel carried cord.
Mara carried nothing and claimed she had carried governance.
Daniel paused at the door.

The beam held.

He stepped inside.

That evening, before sleep, he added to the list:

Fishers:

Do not catch people.

Ask if they can leave.

If they follow, turn around.

Ask why.

Ask who they left.

Ask whether they ate.

Ask what work remains.

Bring a knife for the net.

Women first where households carry the result.

Boys are holes with legs.

He paused.

That last line was Dalia's and probably should not become doctrine.

He kept it anyway, with caution.

From the other side of the room, Maryam said, "Sleep."

Daniel smiled.

"Yes."

Outside, something moved in the dark.
Goat, probably.
Or wind.
Or rumor on four legs.

Inside, the tools rested.

For now, no one had been caught.

For now, those who came could leave.

For now, that had to be enough.
