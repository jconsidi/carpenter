# Chapter 24 — Demons, Shame, and Speech

The first demon arrived with a rope around his wrists.

This was not a promising beginning.

He was perhaps twenty.
Perhaps younger.
Hunger, fear, and public handling made ages unreliable.
His hair had been cut badly, not from style but from convenience or restraint.
His face was bruised on one side.
His tunic hung crooked.
Two men held the rope, one at each end, as if leading an animal that had disappointed them by being related.

A woman followed behind them.

She did not touch the rope.

She looked as if she wanted to.

A crowd came with them, because crowds grew naturally around shame when shame was dragged outdoors.

Daniel saw them from the courtyard and felt his stomach drop.

Mara, beside him, said softly, "No."

Maryam came to the doorway.

Yosef set down the board he had been planing.

Ruth, who had been criticizing Asa's brake adjustment from the best shade, stood with surprising speed.

The men stopped at the edge of the courtyard.

One said, "We hear you heal."

Daniel said, "No."

Too quickly.

The man blinked.

The other said, "Then help."

Better.

Worse.

The young man with bound wrists stared at the ground.
His lips moved.
No sound came at first.
Then words, too quiet to catch, except for one: fire.

The woman flinched.

Maryam saw.

Of course.

She stepped forward, not close to the rope.

"What is his name?"

The first man said, "He is not himself."

Maryam did not look at him.

"What is his name?"

The woman answered.

"Yair."

The name entered the courtyard and changed the air.

A person with a name was harder to handle as a demon.

Good.

Yair heard it and squeezed his eyes shut.

The second man said, "He shouts at night.
Breaks things.
Runs.
Says voices speak.
He struck his brother.
He bit himself." He lifted the rope slightly, as if presenting evidence.
"There is something in him."

Yair whispered, "Fire."

The crowd murmured.

Daniel's mind ran in too many directions.

Psychosis.
Trauma.
Epilepsy? Fever? Injury? Poisoning? Grief? Abuse? Panic? Sleep deprivation? Substance? Infection? Spiritual interpretation.
Social fear.
Family exhaustion.
Actual danger.
Self-harm.
Risk to others.
No hospital.
No antipsychotics.
No sedatives he trusted.
No clean categories.
No right to translate everything into modern names he could not treat.

No power.

No exorcism.

No easy refusal.

Maryam looked at the rope.

"Untie him."

The men tightened their grip.

"No."

Ruth said, "Then leave."

Everyone looked at her.

She pointed at the courtyard.

"This is not a market for frightened men to display a tied boy."

The first man flushed.

"He is dangerous."

Maryam said, "Then step back and make space.
But untie his wrists."

The woman moved.

The first man caught her arm.

Bad.

Yosef took one step forward.

Not fast.

Not threatening exactly.

Enough.

The man released her.

She went to Yair.

"Mother," Maryam said quietly, and the word answered what no one had said.

Yair's mother untied the rope with shaking hands.

Yair did not run.

He crouched instead, arms drawn close to his body, wrists marked raw.
He rocked once, twice, then pressed his forehead to his knees.

The crowd leaned inward.

Mara moved to the doorway and pointed to the care mark: bowl, doorway, crossed-out eye.

"No spectators."

No one moved.

Asa rolled forward on his improved board, brake still imperfect but dignity considerable.

"She said no spectators," he said.
"If you need translation, I charge."

That moved the first few.

Ruth added, "Bring water, cloth, or distance."

That moved more.

Yosef quietly shifted the workbench so it stood between Yair and the remaining crowd without making a cage.

Maryam crouched several paces from Yair.

"Yair," she said.
"You are in Yosef's courtyard.
Your mother is here.
No one is holding rope."

He did not lift his head.

"Fire," he whispered.

Daniel knelt beside Maryam, far enough not to crowd.

"What fire?" he asked.

Yair's hands clamped over his ears.

Bad question.

Too direct.

Maryam did not look at Daniel, which was worse than a rebuke.

She said, "Not yet."

Daniel nodded and shut up.

Ruth brought water.
Not to Yair's mouth.
Near him.

Yair's mother sat behind him, not touching, though her hands kept moving toward him and stopping.

Maryam asked her, "Has he slept?"

The mother shook her head.

"How long?"

"Little.
Days.
Then he sleeps like dead."

"Food?"

"When he can."

"Fever?"

"No."

"Did this begin suddenly?"

The mother's face changed.

Yes, then.

She looked at the men.

Maryam followed the look.

"Who are they?"

"Brothers," the first man said.

"His?"

"Mine," the mother said.
"My brothers."

Uncles.

Daniel disliked them immediately and tried to set that aside.
Sometimes exhausted relatives did cruel things because they had run out of tools.
Sometimes they were simply cruel.
Often both.

Maryam asked, "What happened before?"

The first uncle said, "A demon happened."

Ruth said, "Men say demon when they are tired of remembering."

The courtyard froze.

Yair made a sound like a wounded animal.

His mother covered her mouth.

Maryam did not soften the question.

"What happened before?"

The mother closed her eyes.

"Soldiers came for levy grain.
His father argued."

Silence.

The uncles looked away.

Yair began rocking harder.

Daniel felt cold move through him.

The mother continued, voice flat now because feeling would break it.

"They struck him.
In the yard.
Yair was there.
The storage caught fire after.
Or a lamp.
Or someone kicked it.
I do not know.
There was smoke.
His father died two days later."

Yair whispered, "Fire."

Not demon.

Not only demon, anyway.

Memory.
Grief.
Violence.
Smoke.
Helplessness.
Maybe more.
Maybe voices.
Maybe the mind breaking under weight.
Maybe the body refusing to leave the yard.

The crowd outside had gone quiet enough to hear.

Bad.

Maryam stood.

"Leave," she said.

The uncles began to object.

Maryam's voice sharpened.

"If you brought grief here to be stared at, leave it and go."

Yosef stood beside her.

Ruth said, "I will name every man still watching."

That worked.

Names were dangerous when attached to spectators.

The courtyard cleared slowly, grudgingly, imperfectly.

Mara stayed.

Maryam looked at her.

Mara said, "I am work."

Maryam considered.

"Water."

Mara ran.

Good.

His mother remained behind him.

She did well.

Truth and harm, knotted.

Good.

Important.

Naamah nodded, then looked ashamed.

Maryam said, "Rest from those who frighten him is not dishonor."

Naamah began to cry.

Yair lifted his head.

His eyes were red, unfocused, not empty.

"Mother?"

She moved toward him too quickly.

He recoiled.

She stopped.

Good.

Painful.

Maryam said, "Slow."

Yair stared at it.

Did not take it.

But did not turn away.

That was the first improvement.

No one called it healing.

No one breathed loudly enough to make it a moment.

The problem became where Yair could go.

Not home, perhaps.
Not if the yard, the charred doorpost, and the uncles kept tearing the wound open.
Not to Yosef's house indefinitely; they were not equipped, and making their courtyard into a shrine for broken minds would help no one.
Not bound.
Not displayed.
Not sent into fields alone.

The question was not "what demon?"

The question was "what room?"

Maryam said this, and Daniel hated how many problems became architecture.

No soldiers had entered there.
No fire.
Two older daughters in the household.
A husband who feared trouble but respected Maryam's older cousin.
This was not safety.
It was a possible brace.

Yosef and Daniel inspected the room.

Yair did not come at first.

Good.

The room smelled of stored grain, old cloth, and mice.
Mice were unfortunate but less traumatic than charred wood.
The window was small.

A person should not be trapped.
A door that could be closed for quiet but opened from inside.
Bedding away from wall damp.
No loose blades.
No hanging cords.
A water jar covered but reachable.
A low stool, stable.

"Yes."

"No."

"Then?"

"Someone sits outside if danger is high.
Not to trap.
To witness."

"A net with a knife."

"Maybe."

"All metaphors are bad near enough."

Better.

Good.

She still agreed.

"Better."

Not cure.

A room.

The chapter of demons became more dangerous than the chapter of healings.

Illness frightened people.

Demons gave them someone to blame.

Sometimes on an enemy.
Sometimes on a sin.
Sometimes on a woman speaking.
Sometimes on a child who would not obey.

Daniel did not know what demons were.

This mattered.

He could not honestly say none existed.
He had modern skepticism and ancient uncertainty living in the same body, neither fully trustworthy.
He had seen minds break without needing spirits.
He had also awakened in a world where his own life made strict materialism feel underbuilt.

No one would be displayed.

No one would be tied for convenience.

No one would be named by their wound before their name was spoken.

No one would be touched without cause.

No one would have illness, grief, or truth treated as spectacle.

No one would use demons to hide theft if Maryam could reach the boundary stones.

The demon guardrails became more precise after Yair.

First: ask the name before the wound.

Second: remove spectators.

Third: remove restraints unless immediate danger requires them, and even then with witness and purpose.

Fourth: ask what happened before.

Fifth: ask who benefits if the suffering is called demon.

Sixth: ask what room, object, habit, danger, or lie keeps the wound open.

Seventh: do not deny what you do not know.

Eighth: do not use not knowing as permission for cruelty.

Ninth: do not make the sufferer perform improvement.

Tenth: do not name someone else's wound without consent.

Eleventh: if fire, soldiers, debt, assault, hunger, grief, or theft is present, do not let the word demon hide it.

Twelfth: if someone says "I should have lifted the beam," be very careful.

The twelfth was not written.

Yair improved by refusing neatness.

He still shouted.
Less often.
He still ran once, but to Sera's courtyard, not the burned yard.
He still heard his father's voice sometimes.
He began saying when it happened.
Naamah learned not to argue with the voice directly, but to ask whether Yair wanted water, ground, light, or company.
Sera learned which uncle not to let in.
Yosef returned with two others to dismantle the unsafe part of the burned storage, with Yair watching from a distance and later handing over one piece of wood himself.

They did not erase the ruin.

They made it less able to kill again.

Kezia did not return to her husband immediately.
Her case became tedious, which was good.
Tedious meant witnesses, terms, boundary stones, grain measures, and her aunt's roof.
Possession stories faded because land records bored gossip.

The boy with fits still had fits but fewer burns.

The silent girl spoke once to Mara, apparently to say that one of the goats looked like a dead uncle.
Mara considered this a major breakthrough in art criticism and told no one the actual words until the girl gave permission.

Asa painted the no-spectators mark on his board and used it to back into crowds.

Natan began asking students at the start of lessons, "Can you leave?" This caused confusion, then better questions, then one student leaving, which Natan found painful and necessary.

Ruth became impossible to manage and more necessary than ever.

People still said demons.

Daniel did not stop them all.

He learned to ask, "What do you mean?"

Sometimes they meant fear.

Sometimes violence.

Sometimes illness.

Sometimes memory.

Sometimes a person speaking truth inconveniently.

Sometimes a pattern no one understood.

Sometimes, perhaps, something darker than any room could explain.

When he did not know, he said so.

This did not satisfy people.

Good.

Satisfaction was overrated near suffering.

---

The last incident of that season began with laughter.

Bad laughter.

A group of boys had surrounded another boy near the lower well.
The boy in the center was small, sharp-faced, and speaking in a high, mocking voice that was not his own.
At first Daniel thought performance.
Then he saw the boy's eyes: terrified, trapped by the attention he had created and could not escape.
The voice imitated an old woman from the market.
Then a tax assistant.
Then Shimon, somehow, though the boy had never met him.
The other boys howled.

Mara arrived before Daniel.

Of course.

She stepped into the circle and said, "Can he leave?"

The boys quieted.

"What?"

She pointed at the small boy.

"Can he leave? Or did you make a net because you like the sound it makes?"

The laughter died badly.

The small boy kept speaking in Shimon's voice.

"I am luck face," he said, and then began to cry while still smiling.

Daniel crouched at the edge of the circle.

"What is his name?"

No one answered.

Mara looked around.

A girl at the edge said, "Neri."

Daniel said, "Neri.
You are at the lower well.
Mara is here.
Daniel is here.
No one needs another voice."

Neri clapped both hands over his mouth.

His shoulders shook.

The boys looked ashamed now, which could become cruelty if cornered.

Maryam, arriving breathless, saw everything.

She said to the boys, "Bring water to the shade."

They moved.

Good.

Work gave shame a channel.

Neri sat under the fig tree with Mara beside him.
He did not speak for a long time.
Then, in his own voice, he whispered, "They laugh when I do it."

Mara said, "Yes."

"I cannot stop."

"Also yes."

Daniel waited.

Maryam waited.

Neri said, "When I stop, they ask for another."

Mara looked at Daniel.

Can they leave?

There it was again.

A performance could become a cage if applause locked the door.

Maryam asked, "Who first asked you?"

Neri named an older cousin.

"Do you like doing voices?"

A tiny nod.

"Do you like not being able to stop?"

A shake.

Mara said, "Then we need a knife for the net."

Neri looked at her.

She took a stick and drew a mark: an open hand cutting through a loop.

"Stop means stop," she said.

Daniel blinked.

Simple.

Too simple?

Useful.

Maryam nodded.

The boys returned with water.

Mara made them practice.

Neri did one voice.

One boy laughed.

Mara lifted the cutting mark.

"Stop."

Neri stopped.

Everyone waited.

Nothing terrible happened.

They practiced again.

Not because children were tools.

Because bodies learned with acts.

By the end, Neri could do one voice and stop.
The other boys could laugh and then stop asking.
Not perfectly.
Better.

No demon.

No casting out.

A gift with a door.

A performance with a knife.

A child allowed to leave his own joke.

Daniel watched Mara teach it and felt something shift.

She was getting good at this.

Not safe.

Good.

That night, Daniel added to the list.

Demons:

Ask the name before the wound.

No spectators.

Rope off first if possible.

Ask what happened before.

Ask who benefits from the word demon.

Rooms can hold wounds open.

Doors should open from inside.

Do not name another's wound.

Grief is not a demon.

Truth may sound mad when lies own the room.

Bodies cannot carry beams alone.

Some nets are applause.

Stop means stop.

He paused.

The list was too long.

All important lists became too long because life refused elegance.

He added:

If you do not know, say so before power borrows your voice.

That one frightened him.

He kept it.

Mara, who had been sitting nearby repairing the unauthorized goat carrying a beam mark, looked up.

"What did you add?"

He read it.

She nodded.

"Good."

"Dangerous."

"Obviously."

Maryam, from the doorway, said, "Then where does it go?"

Daniel looked at the chest.

Full.

Yosef had started a second one.

This felt like institutional failure.

Or preparation.

Both.

He placed the list scrap in the new chest.

Mara added the stop-means-stop mark.

Ruth had sent a piece of charred wood from Yair's old storage, with permission.
It went in too.

The chest of things not to worship had become two chests.

Daniel did not like this.

Maryam touched the lid.

"More weight needs more structure."

Yosef said, "And better handles."

Mara said, "And labels."

"No labels," Daniel said.

"Bad governance."

"Good."

They closed the chest.

Outside, the night held ordinary sounds: insects, distant voices, a goat doing something regrettable, wind against the wall.
Somewhere, Yair might be sleeping or not.
Kezia under her aunt's roof.
Asa rolling badly but better.
Tobiah gone.
Neri perhaps practicing stopping.
The baby still dead.
The river still running.
The lake still catching light and fish badly.
The story still moving.

Inside, the beam held.

For now.

Daniel lay down.

From the other side of the room, Maryam said, "Sleep."

He smiled in the dark.

"Yes."

He slept badly.

That was honest.

In the morning, someone came with a broken door and a question about forgiveness.

Daniel almost laughed.

Then he got up.
