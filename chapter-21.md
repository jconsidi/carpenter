# Chapter 21 — Fishermen

Fish were worse than goats.

Daniel had not expected this.

Goats were at least committed to their crimes.
A goat wanted something: food, freedom, elevation, another goat's food, a forbidden cloth, the exact basket just declared important.
A goat had purpose, however destructive.

Fish had no evident moral center.

They flashed under water, appeared where they were not needed, vanished when expected, died quickly, stank almost immediately, and required men to build entire economies around not knowing where they were.

Daniel distrusted fish on structural grounds.

This became relevant because Yosef's cousin had a cousin with a boat.

The exact chain of kinship was explained to Daniel twice and remained less stable than a poorly braced roof.
It involved Yosef's mother's people, a marriage, a debt, a repaired axle, and a man named Shimon who, according to everyone, was either generous, loud, unlucky, clever, impossible, or all of those depending on weather and whether he owed money.

The boat was not in their village.
It was near the lake, where several families worked nets, traded fish, mended boats, argued about taxes, and smelled as if the world had been salted and left in sun.

Yosef had been asked to help repair a frame and two cracked oarlocks.

He said yes because the work was real, the kinship was real enough, and the payment included dried fish, which Maryam claimed was useful despite Daniel's objections to its personality.

Daniel did not want to go.

This had become a common beginning.

Maryam noticed.

"Because of the story?" she asked.

They were packing tools.

Daniel tied cord around a bundle.

"Yes."

"Which part?"

He looked at her.

"All of it."

She waited.

He sighed.

"Fishermen."

"Yes."

"Followers."

"Yes."

"Boats.
Nets.
Crowds near water.
Men leaving work.
Catching people."

Maryam's mouth tightened at that last phrase.

"Catching people?"

"Old story wording.
Or close.
Fishers of men, maybe.
I hate it."

"I also dislike it."

That helped.

Yosef entered with the tool roll.

"What do you dislike?"

"Catching people," Maryam said.

Yosef paused.

"As a general principle?"

"Yes."

"Good."

Daniel almost smiled.

Yosef set the tool roll down.

"We are repairing oarlocks."

"Near fishermen," Daniel said.

"Yes."

"After the river."

"Yes."

"After wilderness."

"Yes."

"Do you see the issue?"

Yosef looked at him.

"I see a boat with broken parts."

Daniel closed his eyes.

Mara, sitting on the threshold with a half-finished goat mark stencil no one had authorized, said, "That is his answer to everything."

"No," Yosef said.
"Sometimes I see two boats."

Mara approved.

Daniel did not.

Maryam folded cloth into the travel bundle.

"If you do not go, why?"

"Because of fear."

"Only?"

"No."

"What else?"

He hated this method.
Maryam never let fear be either dismissed or enthroned.
It had to stand with its accomplices.

"Because going may create a shape."

"Yes."

"Because not going may also create one."

"Yes."

"Because I need to learn how to enter water-adjacent work without acting like every shoreline is a prophecy."

Yosef nodded.

"Good."

Daniel looked at him.

"That means go?"

"That means oarlocks break whether stories exist."

Mara said, "Deep."

Yosef looked at her.

"It is a useful depth."

She considered.

"Acceptable."

Daniel tied the cord too tightly and had to untie it.

Maryam said, "Who goes?"

"Yosef and me," Daniel said.

Maryam gave him a look.

"And?"

He looked at Mara.

"No."

Mara brightened.

"You are thinking it."

"No."

"Your face is making space for yes."

"My face has been slandered for years."

Maryam said, "I may go."

Everyone stopped.

Yosef looked at her.

Daniel looked at her.

Mara lowered the stencil.

Maryam continued folding cloth.

"The work is near kin.
There will be women there.
If rumors travel with you, I would rather hear where they begin."

Yosef nodded slowly.

Daniel felt relief and alarm together.

Mara said, "Then I should go."

"No," said Maryam.

Mara looked betrayed.

"You need someone to make rumors boring here."

"I am always given government without authority."

"Correct."

Mara scowled.

Daniel said, "Also, Hanina's smoke cover needs checking after yesterday's wind."

Mara pointed at him.

"Do not use real tasks against me."

"You taught us that method."

She accepted this as tribute.

Erel would go instead, because he had been working steadily with Yosef and needed to learn boat repairs if he was to become useful beyond stools, doors, and resentments.
He arrived thrilled and trying not to show it.
He had never seen the lake.

Daniel envied him for this uncomplicated excitement.

Then envied him less when Erel asked, "Will there be large fish?"

Mara said, "If one eats Yeshua, bring back the fish.
We can mark it as hazardous."

Maryam said, "Mara."

"What? For public safety."

Daniel took the bundle and stepped over the threshold.

The beam held behind him.

Ahead: road, water, boats, men whose names he already feared knowing.

---

The road to the lake was not the road to the river.

This mattered.

Daniel repeated it silently.

Not the river.
Not the river man.
Not washing.
Not declaration.
Not wilderness.
Not old script, unless every road with dust and need became script, in which case living was impossible.

This road carried trade.

Fish inland, grain outward, oil, cloth, salt, tools, gossip, tax demands, kinship obligations, and complaints about all of these.
The closer they came to the lake, the more the air changed.
Salt was wrong word, perhaps, but Daniel's old mind supplied it before correction.
Wet.
Mineral.
Fish.
Mud.
Rope.
Tar.
Wood swollen and dried and swollen again.
Men who worked on water had a different smell from men who worked under roofs; both carried materials with them.

Erel smelled the air and grinned.

Daniel said, "It gets worse."

Erel said, "It smells like travel."

"It smells like fish."

"Travel fish."

Yosef said, "Do not encourage him."

Maryam smiled.

They stayed the first night with Yosef's kin, a woman named Dalia whose relationship to Yosef remained impossible but whose competence did not.
Dalia had arms like rope, hair mostly covered, and an expression that suggested she had survived men, weather, fish, and kinship by refusing surprise.

She greeted Maryam with warmth, Yosef with blunt affection, Daniel with inspection, and Erel with the practical contempt reserved for boys near unfamiliar water.

"You know boats?" she asked Daniel.

"No."

"Good.
Men who know boats badly are harder to keep alive than men who know nothing."

Daniel liked her immediately.

She pointed toward the shore.

"Shimon says the frame only needs binding."

Yosef said, "Then it needs more."

Dalia smiled.

"You are kin."

Shimon appeared before sunset, loud enough to arrive before his body.

He was broad, dark from sun, beard in need of arbitration, hands scarred from rope and hooks, and eyes quick with calculation that did not always reach wisdom before speech did.

"Yosef!" he shouted, as if Yosef were across water instead of ten paces away.

They embraced.

Shimon looked at Daniel.

"This is the son who sees cracks before men admit them?"

Daniel closed his eyes.

Yosef said, "This is my son."

"Good.
Can he see fish before they admit themselves?"

"No," Daniel said.

Shimon laughed.

"I like him."

Dalia said, "You like anything that answers quickly."

Shimon ignored this with practice.

Maryam watched him the way she watched overloaded shelves.

Erel watched the lake.

Daniel watched the boats.

There were two near the shore under repair and several farther down where men worked nets in the lowering light.
Boats were not like houses.
They were structures meant to be held by what tried to enter them.
Wood, pitch, rope, ribs, pegs, oarlocks, thwarts, mast, sail, net weights, waterline, balance.
A boat was a compromise with drowning.

Daniel respected this.

Against his will, he stepped closer.

Yosef noticed.

Of course.

"What do you see?"

"Everything wants to move."

Shimon laughed again.

"That is water."

Daniel did not look away from the boat.

"No.
The boat too.
Load shifts.
Men shift.
Fish shift.
Wind shifts.
Water enters.
Wood swells.
Ropes stretch.
Nothing stays where you put it."

Shimon stopped laughing.

Dalia looked at Maryam.

Maryam looked unsurprised.

Yosef said, "Good."

Daniel regretted speaking.

Shimon approached the damaged boat.

"Then tell me where this wants to move."

Ah.

The hook.

Not malicious.

Practical.

Still hook.

Daniel stepped back.

"Yosef knows boats better than I do."

Yosef said, "I know wood.
Shimon knows water.
You know when you are avoiding."

Daniel glared.

Maryam's mouth twitched.

Erel looked between them with joy.

Daniel sighed and approached.

The frame had cracked where a crosspiece met a side support.
Binding had been added twice, badly once and desperately once.
The oarlock on one side had widened its seating.
The other had a hairline split hidden under grime.
The boat had been worked hard and patched under time pressure, probably after a good catch or bad storm.
The issue was not one break.
It was repeated strain transferred around the first bad repair.

"Too much force here," Daniel said, pointing.
"But because this stopped moving."

Shimon frowned.

"Stopped moving is good."

"Not if it should flex."

Yosef nodded.

Shimon looked offended.

The boat, like many objects, did not care about his offense.

Daniel continued, "The binding made it rigid there.
Then the load moved here.
The oarlock widened.
This side began taking more.
That crack is older than it looks."

Shimon bent.

Dalia said, "He told you."

Shimon said, "You knew?"

"I know how you row when angry."

"That is not a boat defect."

"It becomes one."

Maryam looked at Daniel as if to say: see, all structures are social eventually.

Daniel pretended not to understand.

Yosef inspected the repair more fully.
Shimon supplied boat context.
Dalia supplied correction.
Erel supplied eager hands and nearly dropped a mallet into shallow water, then learned quickly because humiliation near water was memorable.

They worked until darkness made precision foolish.

The boat would need more than binding.

Shimon sighed when Yosef said so.

"Cost?"

"Less than sinking," Yosef said.

Dalia said, "You hear? Less than sinking.
Write it on your forehead."

Shimon said, "My forehead already carries burdens."

Mara would have liked him.

Daniel decided this was not necessarily good.

---

Fishing work began before dawn, which Daniel considered an argument against the profession.

He woke to darkness, voices, rope sounds, feet, coughs, and Dalia already moving as if sleep were an optional practice for weaker households.
Maryam joined her.
The two women spoke in low practical tones about food, nets, a sick child in another house, a young wife angry with a mother-in-law, and which men had returned with less fish than boasting.

Daniel sat up.

Yosef was already awake.

Of course.

Erel was not.
He had fallen asleep with lake excitement and woken with adolescent betrayal.

They went to the shore in dim light.

Men were returning from night work.
Some boats low with catch.
Some nearly empty.
Faces told more than baskets.
A good catch made men generous with speech.
A bad one made every rope personally offensive.

Shimon's boat had not gone because of the repair, so he stood on shore criticizing weather, fish, government, nets, cousins, and the moral condition of oarlocks.

Another boat came in almost empty.

Two brothers pulled it: Andrai and Yonah, though Daniel immediately mixed their names and decided not to use either until necessary.
Andrai was lean, quick, and smiled only with half his mouth.
Yonah was heavier, quieter, and looked at nets as if they owed him apologies.

Shimon shouted, "Fish heard you coming and left!"

Andrai shouted back something involving Shimon's oarlock and divine mercy.

Dalia said, "Men."

Maryam said, "Yes."

Daniel watched the empty nets.

Old fragments moved.

Fishermen.
Empty nets.
Teaching from boats.
Great catch.
Follow me.

No.

No.

This was not that.

He was here with Yosef.
He was young.
He was repairing boat parts.
No one had asked him to teach from a boat.
The nets were empty because fishing was uncertain.
Empty nets were a professional reality, not narrative summons.

He focused on material.

The nets needed mending.
Lines worn.
Floats uneven.
Weights mismatched.
Some knots old, some hurried.
A tear near the edge had been patched in a way that pulled the mesh out of shape, making future tears likely.
Daniel understood enough from cloth and rope to see tension; not enough to be useful without instruction.

He knelt near the net.

Yonah said, "Do not touch."

Daniel lifted his hands.

"Good rule."

Yonah blinked.

Andrai laughed.

"Most boys argue."

"I am not most boys."

Maryam, behind him, said, "Careful."

Daniel amended, "In this one matter."

Andrai laughed harder.

Yonah did not, but his face eased.

An older fisherman named Mattai approached, limping slightly.
Not the tax collector from later stories--Daniel shoved the thought away.
This Mattai had hands like net weights and a permanent squint.

He looked at Daniel.

"You see rope?"

"Some."

"You see fish?"

"No."

"Then you are honest enough."

He handed Daniel a torn section.

"Hold.
Do not fix."

Daniel held.

The net was alive with tension.
Not alive.
Wrong word.
But it moved as a system: pull one knot, another shifted; tighten one edge, distort a pocket; repair a tear badly, change how the whole net opened.
It was cloth that had to become shape under water where no one could see it.

He hated that he loved this.

Mattai showed him the knot.

"Not pretty," the old man said.
"Pretty knots feed pride.
Strong knots feed children."

Daniel nodded.

Mara would steal that.
Thankfully absent.

Erel came close.

Mattai put him to work too.

Soon Daniel was holding, Erel was passing cord, Yonah was cutting, Andrai was complaining about fish with artistry, and Shimon was pretending not to watch because his boat repair had not yet begun.

The shore smelled terrible.

The work was excellent.

This was how danger entered.

Not through omen.

Through genuine interest.

Daniel could imagine learning boats.
Nets.
Weather.
Fish patterns.
The geometry of uncertainty.
The social economy of risk.
A world where no structure stayed still and every repair had to move.
It would be useful.
Fascinating.
Full of problems.
Full of men who needed fewer speeches and better oarlocks.
Full of women like Dalia who already knew half the load paths and were tired of men naming them luck.

He could stay a season.

Just to learn.

Just to help.

Just to understand.

He looked up and found Maryam watching him.

She knew.

He looked away.

---

The first crowd gathered because of a tax argument.

This was disappointing, though realistic.

A collector's assistant had come to inspect catches and assess duties, or fees, or penalties, or some other invented category of extraction.
Fishermen gathered quickly.
Men who spent their nights losing arguments to fish were often eager to argue with someone who could answer back.

Shimon became loud.

Andrai became sharper.

Yonah became dangerous by going quiet.

The assistant, a narrow man with good sandals and bad instincts, insisted on counting not only fish brought to shore but expected share based on recent averages.
This was mathematically offensive and morally worse.

Daniel heard the argument from beside the net.

He should not intervene.

He was not from here.
He did not know the arrangements.
Tax structures under occupation were complex, layered, predatory, and locally specific.
A badly timed sentence could hurt people more than help them.
Maryam had taught him that.
Yosef had taught him that.
The river had taught him that water did not walk the road.

Then the assistant said, "If your nets are empty, perhaps your honesty is also."

The air changed.

Yonah moved.

Daniel moved faster.

Not toward the assistant.
Toward a coil of rope beside Yonah's foot.
He kicked it aside.

Yonah's step landed where the rope had been.

Without the rope, he did not trip forward into the assistant.

Without the trip, his hand did not strike.

Without the strike, soldiers did not become involved.

Maybe.

Yonah looked down.

Then at Daniel.

Daniel said, "Rope."

Yonah stared.

The assistant, unaware he had nearly been transformed into a structural failure, continued speaking.

Maryam appeared beside Daniel.

Yosef appeared beside Shimon.

Dalia stepped between the assistant and the loudest men with a basket.

Not a weapon.

A basket.

It gave everyone something else to look at.

"You count fish?" Dalia asked.

The assistant adjusted his dignity.

"Yes."

"Then count."

She placed the basket down.

It contained very few fish.

The men laughed, but not as before.
The violence had shifted into ridicule.
Ridicule was not safe, but safer.

Dalia continued, "If you tax fish that imagined entering the net, I will bring you imagined payment."

Even Maryam smiled.

The assistant flushed.

"You mock authority."

Dalia said, "No.
I ask how many invisible fish fit your tablet."

The crowd laughed harder.

Danger.

But dispersed danger.

Daniel watched the assistant calculate whether escalation would serve him.
He chose irritation over force, perhaps because the crowd was large, perhaps because his sandals were too nice for a fight near fish, perhaps because a senior collector had not empowered him to create a scene over invisible catch.

He took the actual count.

Left angry.

The men celebrated as if they had won more than a delay.

Maryam did not celebrate.

Yosef did not.

Dalia picked up the basket.

Shimon clapped Daniel on the shoulder.

"You moved the rope."

Daniel said, "The rope was in the way."

Yonah looked at him with something like gratitude and dislike.

"Good," he said.

Then walked away.

Andrai watched Daniel.

"You saw him step?"

"I saw the rope."

"Same thing?"

"No."

Andrai smiled.

"Maybe."

Daniel looked for Maryam.

She was already looking at him.

Not angry.

Concerned.

Dalia came over.

"You saved him from becoming expensive."

Daniel said, "I moved rope."

"Yes.
Sometimes rope is where men put their anger."

That was too good.

Everyone here was dangerous.

---

The boat repair took three days.

This was because the damage was worse than Shimon had admitted, which everyone but Shimon had expected.
A rib section needed easing, one binding removed, the oarlock seating reset with better distribution, and the crack cleaned before it could be braced.
The boat had to retain motion without letting motion become damage.

Daniel liked that sentence and immediately distrusted it.

He did not say it aloud.

Yosef taught by doing.
Shimon argued, then learned.
Dalia corrected history.
Erel absorbed everything with eyes wide.
Maryam moved between households, learning more in two hours than Daniel could have learned in a month of direct questions.

By the second day, fishermen came with nets and objects and arguments.

"Ask the builder's son," Shimon said once.

Yosef looked at him.

Shimon corrected, "Ask the men who know what they are doing, and the boy if it involves rope trying to murder someone."

This was better.

Not good.

Better.

Daniel made himself say "I do not know" often.

At first this frustrated people.

Then it helped.

"I do not know fish."

"I do not know wind here."

"I do not know your agreements."

"I do not know what that knot does under water."

"I can see strain."

"I can hold."

"I can ask who uses it."

"I can ask who fixes it after."

These were safe sentences.

Useful.

Boring enough sometimes.

On the second evening, Andrai sat beside him while mending nets.

"You ask who fixes it after."

"Yes."

"Why?"

"Because a repair that leaves work to someone invisible is not complete."

Andrai considered.

"My mother would like you."

"Dalia?"

"No.
My mother.
Dalia would inspect you first."

"She already has."

"Then you are still alive."

"Apparently."

Andrai smiled.

"My mother says men call fishing courage because they are not home when women stretch the bad catch into food."

Daniel looked at him.

"That is also structure."

"I thought you would say that."

"I am becoming predictable."

"Useful men often do."

Daniel did not know how to respond.

Andrai continued mending.

After a while, he said, "Do you teach?"

"No."

"You answer like someone avoiding yes."

"I help with repairs."

"That is not no."

Daniel tied a knot badly and had to undo it.

Andrai watched without comment.

Then said, "Good."

"What?"

"You fixed it instead of pretending."

Daniel smiled despite himself.

This was bad.

He liked Andrai.

---

On the third morning, the repaired boat was tested.

This was unavoidable.

A boat repaired on shore had not yet agreed to be a boat.

Shimon wanted to test it himself.
Dalia insisted Yonah go too because Shimon's optimism required ballast.
Yosef went because the repair was his responsibility.
Daniel did not want to go because boats, water, fishermen, story.
Also because he wanted to go.

Maryam knew.

"Do you need to be in it?" she asked.

"No."

"Does the repair need you?"

"No."

"Does your fear want you away?"

"Yes."

"Does your curiosity want you in?"

"Yes."

She nodded.

"Then ask the boat."

That was not helpful.

Or it was too helpful.

Daniel went to the boat.

The repaired frame looked sound.
Oarlock seating better.
Bindings distributed.
Crack braced without freezing necessary flex.
The boat would behave differently under load; someone needed to watch.

Yosef could watch.

But Daniel had helped plan the distribution.
Not going might be fear.
Going might be pride.
Both had arguments.

He touched the side.

Wood, pitch, damp, old strain.

"What can it bear?" he murmured.

Shimon said, "Men, if they stop talking to it."

Daniel stepped back.

"I should stay."

Maryam watched.

Yosef nodded.

No praise.

Good.

Yosef, Shimon, and Yonah took the boat into shallow water first.
Then farther.
The boat moved.
Flexed.
Complained.
Held.

Daniel exhaled.

Erel hopped from foot to foot.

"It works."

"For now," Daniel said.

Dalia nodded approvingly.

"For now is honest."

The boat returned.

Shimon declared it better than before.

Yosef said, "Different."

Shimon said, "Better different."

Yosef said, "Watched different."

Dalia said, "Maintained different."

Shimon groaned.

"Everyone has become a teacher."

Daniel stepped away before anyone could look at him.

Too late.

Andrai was looking.

---

That night, the fish came.

Not miraculously.

Annoyingly.

The repaired boat went out with Shimon, Yonah, and two others.
Another boat went with Andrai and Mattai's sons.
Weather was decent.
Moon adequate.
Nets repaired better than before.
Men less proud than usual because Dalia had threatened public commentary.
Daniel remained on shore with Yosef, Maryam, Erel, and Dalia.
They did not wait dramatically; they slept in shifts because fishing involved hours of boredom punctuated by cold, strain, and disappointment.

Near dawn, shouts came from the water.

Erel woke instantly.

Daniel sat up, heart racing.

No.

No miraculous catch.

No.

The boats came in low.

Full.

Not impossibly.
Not sinking.
Not tearing nets beyond belief.
Just good.
Very good.
The sort of catch that made men laugh too loudly because relief had no manners.

Shimon shouted before landing.

"The boat held!"

Yonah shouted, "The nets held!"

Andrai shouted, "Fish remain stupid!"

Everyone on shore moved.

Work first.
Meaning later.
Baskets, sorting, gutting, counting, protecting catch from birds, managing buyers, keeping children from being stepped on, preventing Shimon from retelling the catch before the fish were secured.

Daniel helped carry baskets because baskets were morally safer than interpretation.

They were heavy.

Real.

Cold.

Fish slapped against one another with disgusting finality.

Erel laughed while working.

Dalia commanded everyone.

Maryam moved where needed.

Yosef inspected the oarlock immediately.

It had held, but showed new wear at one point.

Good.
Visible.
Correctable.

Shimon came to Daniel with shining eyes.

No.

"Your repair brought luck," he said.

"No."

Dalia said, "The repair kept the boat from wasting work.

Shimon accepted this because he was too happy to argue.

Andrai approached.

"We worked differently," he said.

Good.

Daniel nodded.

"Because the nets opened differently."

"And because Yonah listened for once."

Yonah said, from behind a basket, "I hear lies."

Andrai grinned.

Then his face sobered.

"Still.
Good work."

Not miracle.

Good work.

A good catch after repair would become story anyway.
But perhaps a better one.
A boat held.
Nets held.
Men worked differently.
Fish happened.

No command.

No deep-water instruction.

No overflowing nets splitting by divine drama.

No leaving boats.

Just a good catch after maintenance.

Daniel allowed himself to breathe.

Then Mattai said, "If the builder's son stays, we will all be rich."

No.

The sentence landed like a spark in dry grass.

Men laughed.

Some looked at Daniel.

Some looked at Yosef.

Some looked at the boat.

Maryam looked at Mattai.

Dalia spoke first.

"If the builder's son stays, he will tell you to repair things you enjoy neglecting."

The laughter shifted.

Good.

Yosef said, "The catch came from fish."

Shimon said, "And nets."

Yonah said, "And not breaking the boat."

Andrai said, "And Dalia shouting before we left."

Dalia pointed at him.

"Remember that part."

Not perfectly.

Enough.

Daniel carried another basket before anyone could make him carry meaning.

---

The invitation came from Andrai, not Shimon.

This mattered.

Shimon would have made it loud.
Andrai made it after work, when hands were tired and the catch was being salted.
He sat beside Daniel near the nets and spoke as if continuing a conversation already begun.

"You could come again."

Daniel kept working.

"For repairs?"

"For repairs.
For nets.
For questions.
For saying no at useful times."

"No."

Andrai nodded.

Not offended.

Good.

"Because of the story?"

Daniel's hands stopped.

Andrai watched him.

He knew some? No.
Not the hidden story.
But enough to see that Daniel's no had old roots.

"What story?" Daniel asked carefully.

Andrai shrugged.

"The one people start making when a young man sees rope, water, shame, and taxes before others do."

Daniel stared at him.

Andrai tied a knot.

"I do not know your hidden things.
I know nets.
Once something catches, men ask whether it wanted to."

Daniel looked toward the lake.

Andrai continued, "I am not asking you to become anything.

That was worse.

Better.

Worse because it was fair.

Daniel thought of the old phrase: follow me.

No one had said it.

Instead, Andrai asked him to come again.

Work needs you.

The hook was gentler and therefore harder to refuse.

Daniel said, "I cannot be your answer."

Andrai laughed softly.

"Good.
Answers do not mend nets well."

Daniel looked at him.

Andrai's hands kept moving.

"My brother thinks strength solves what patience should.
Shimon thinks luck is a repair method.
Mattai thinks old knots remember better than young hands.
I think sometimes someone from outside sees the shape because he does not owe the same lies."

"That is too much trust."

"Yes."

"Dangerous."

"Yes."

"Then why ask?"

"Because the boat held."

"That is not enough."

"No."

Andrai looked up.

"Because Yonah did not strike the tax man."

Daniel said nothing.

"Because Dalia likes your mother."

"That may be enough by itself."

Andrai smiled.

"Because when you do not know, you say so."

Daniel looked at the net.

"I may not come."

"I know."

"I may come and say nothing useful."

"Then hold."

"I may make things worse."

"Yes."

This was becoming uncomfortably honest.

Andrai tied another knot.

"If you come, come as work.
Not wonder."

Daniel closed his eyes.

There it was.

A door.

Not follow me.

Come as work.

He could refuse.

He could accept nothing.

He could say maybe.

Maybe was a door without hinges, he had once thought.
But some doors needed hinges made in use.

"I can return if there is work," Daniel said.

Andrai nodded.

"That is all I asked."

Was it?

Perhaps.

For now.

---

Maryam heard about the invitation before Daniel told her.

This was normal and unjust.

Dalia told her.
Or Andrai's mother.
Or the lake itself had a women's network.
Daniel no longer believed information traveled only by visible carriers.

They walked along the shore in the evening while Yosef and Erel checked the repair once more.

Maryam said, "Andrai asked you to return."

"Yes."

"And?"

"I said if there is work."

Maryam nodded.

"Good."

Daniel looked at her sharply.

"Good?"

"You did not say always.
You did not say never."

"I may be stepping toward the old story."

"Yes."

He flinched.

Maryam did not soften.

"You may also be stepping toward work."

"Yes."

"Both."

"I am tired of both."

"I know."

They walked.

Water moved beside them, carrying light badly.

Daniel said, "The old story gathers fishermen."

"So do lakes."

"That is not funny."

"It was not meant to be."

He sighed.

Maryam picked up a small smooth stone and turned it in her fingers.

"Do you remember what you said at the river?"

"Fear deciding alone."

"And work?"

"Return."

She nodded.

"Return is not only to our house."

He stopped walking.

She continued a few steps, then turned.

"Sometimes return means going back to where work remains."

"That sounds dangerous."

"Yes."

"Everything you say lately sounds dangerous."

"Truth often does before use."

He laughed despite himself.

Maryam came back.

"I do not want the story to take you," she said.

The directness struck him.

She rarely said that plainly.

"I know."

"I also do not want fear of being taken to make you smaller than love asks."

He looked away toward the lake.

Men called to one another.
Nets hung.
Fish were salted.
Children ran where they should not.
Dalia shouted.
Yosef and Erel argued over a tool.
Andrai bent over a net.
Shimon laughed too loudly.
Yonah carried baskets with steady strength.

People.

Not symbols.

People near water.

He whispered, "I do not know how to do this."

Maryam said, "Good."

He looked at her.

She smiled sadly.

"Men who know too quickly often catch people."

The phrase returned.

Catching people.

He hated it less in her warning.

"So what do I do?"

"What you have learned."

He waited.

She counted on her fingers.

"Bring work.
Do not decide alone.
Make others visible.
Refuse wonder when it hides labor.
Refuse ordinariness when it hides fear.
Return.
Eat.
Sleep.
Ask who carries the result."

"That is too much."

"Yes."

"I wanted one rule."

"I know."

"And?"

"You did not get one."

The lake moved.

No voice.

Good.

---

They prepared to leave the next morning.

Payment became complicated.

It always did when kinship, repair, fish, and pride met in the same basket.
Shimon wanted to overpay in enthusiasm and underpay in actual useful goods.
Dalia corrected him.
Yosef accepted less than the repair's worth but more than symbolic gratitude.
Maryam arranged fish, salt, cloth, and future obligation in quantities that made everyone feel slightly constrained and therefore respected.

Daniel received nothing separately.

Good.

Then Mattai gave him a net needle.

Bad.

Old, worn smooth, useful, personal.

"For holding first," Mattai said.
"Fix later."

Daniel looked at it.

"I am not a fisherman."

"No."

Mattai's squint deepened.

"Good.
Fishermen think nets are about fish."

Daniel waited.

"What are they about?"

Mattai smiled.

"Holes."

Then walked away.

Daniel stared at the net needle.

Mara would love and misuse this.

He showed Yosef.

Yosef said, "Good gift."

"Dangerous gift."

"Most good gifts are."

Maryam said, "Take it."

He did.

Andrai came last.

No dramatic farewell.
No request to follow.
No vow.
He simply clasped Daniel's forearm.

"If work comes?"

Daniel nodded.

"If work comes."

Yonah approached, looked as if speaking caused him pain, and said, "Rope."

Daniel nodded.

"Rope."

This apparently meant gratitude, warning, and possibly friendship.

Shimon shouted that the next catch would require Daniel's presence, then, under Dalia's stare, amended, "Or not, because fish are faithless and boats are wood."

Better.

Dalia embraced Maryam.

Then inspected Daniel one more time.

"You are afraid of being made into story."

Daniel froze.

Maryam did not.

Yosef did not.

Dalia continued, "Good.
Be more afraid of becoming useless to avoid it."

Then she handed him dried fish.

He accepted both.

The road away from the lake smelled better with each step and worse in memory.

Erel talked for an hour.

Boat ribs.
Nets.
Fish.
Oarlocks.
Mattai's knots.
Andrai's question.
Yonah almost striking the tax man.
Shimon's laugh.
Dalia's basket.
The catch.
The boat holding.
The net needle.
The smell.
The possibility of returning.

Daniel let him.

Erel's excitement was not prophecy.

It was a boy discovering a wider world.

This was allowed.

Maryam walked beside Yosef.

Daniel fell slightly behind.

He took out the net needle and turned it over in his hand.

A tool for making holes useful.

That was terrible.

Too good.

He put it away.

---

Mara met them at the road, because apparently waiting at roads had become her vocation.

She saw the fish bundle first.

"Tragic."

"For you?" Daniel asked.

"For everyone with a nose."

Erel immediately began telling her about the lake.

She listened with visible effort to appear unimpressed.
She failed when he described the tax assistant and Dalia's invisible fish.
Mara laughed so hard she had to sit on a stone.

"I like Dalia."

"You have not met her," Daniel said.

"I recognize excellence."

Erel showed her the goat mark adaptation he had scratched in dirt near the lake to warn children away from a slippery plank.

Mara inspected it gravely.

"Acceptable."

Erel glowed.

Daniel showed her the net needle.

She took it carefully.

"What is it?"

"For nets."

"I know that.
What is it for in your face?"

"My face?"

"You are making meaning."

He sighed.

"Mattai said nets are about holes."

Mara stared.

Then slowly smiled.

"No."

"Yes."

"That is too good."

"I know."

"We must ruin it quickly."

"Please."

She held the net needle up.

"Nets are about holes, fish, knots, men falling in, and smelling bad."

"Better."

"And catching what did not ask to be caught."

Daniel stopped.

Mara saw.

Her smile faded.

The old phrase sat between them again.

Fishers of men.

Catching people.

She handed the needle back.

"Do not catch people," she said.

"I know."

"Do not let them call it another word."

He nodded.

"What if people follow anyway?"

Mara looked toward Maryam and Yosef, then back.

"Then keep turning around."

He stared.

She shrugged.

"If someone follows and you never turn around, you become a story walking away.
Turn around.
Ask why.
Ask if they ate.
Ask who they left.
Ask if they know where you are going.
Ask if they can go home."

Daniel held the needle tightly.

"That is good."

"I know."

"No, very good."

She looked pleased and frightened.

"Then do not make it solemn."

Too late.

He said, "Following needs turning."

She groaned.

"I told you not to."

"It is useful."

"It is mine."

"I will credit you."

"No.
That makes it worse."

Maryam, passing them, said, "Both of you, walk."

They walked.

---

That night, the household ate fish.

Daniel had opinions.

They were not welcomed.

Maryam made it good because Maryam could make usefulness edible.
Yosef ate with satisfaction.
Erel described the lake again.
Mara joined because fish was tragic but food was food.
Hanina came by with onions and stayed because food that could accept onions had apparently requested them.
Natan arrived with a broken writing case and was made to wait until after dinner, which he accepted because he was learning.

Daniel watched the room fill with lake story.

Not miracle.

Work.

A tax argument defused by rope and Dalia's basket.

A boat repaired to move properly.

Nets held.

A good catch after maintenance, luck, fish, labor, and Dalia shouting.

An invitation to return if work came.

A net needle.

Holes.

Following.

Turning.

The story had not been avoided.

It had been made denser with people.

This did not make it safe.

But it made it harder for any single shape to swallow the truth.

After dinner, Natan repaired his own writing case under Yosef's supervision because Maryam said scholars should understand hinges before writing about doors.
Natan accepted this with only slight suffering.

Mara drew a net in dirt.

Then drew people walking behind it.

Then crossed them out.

Then drew the net turned sideways into a fence.

Then frowned.

Daniel watched.

"What?"

She tapped the drawing.

"Nets are bad metaphors."

"Yes."

"They catch.
They tangle.
They also hold food."

"Yes."

"They tear."

"Yes."

"They need mending."

"Yes."

"They are mostly holes."

"Yes."

She looked up.

"This is going to become a problem."

"I know."

She erased the people.

Left the holes.

Maryam, from near the lamp, said, "Perhaps begin with mending."

Yosef said, "And knowing what the net is for."

Hanina said, "And who is made to mend it."

Natan said, carefully, "And whether what is caught is food or captive."

Everyone looked at him.

He raised both hands.

"Question, not teaching."

Mara said, "Door."

Daniel said, "Hook."

Maryam said, "Both."

Yosef said, "Later."

This was the correct answer.

They finished cleaning.

The beam held.

The house smelled like fish, which Daniel considered a structural failure of air.

At the end of the night, before sleep, he took out the net needle and placed it in the chest beside Natan's parchment, the dangerous papers, harmlessly misused marks, the shard from the jar, and other objects that had become too meaningful to leave lying around but too useful to discard.

Maryam watched.

"What will you write on the list?" she asked.

Daniel thought.

Lake:

Not river.

Fishermen real.

Boats are compromises with drowning.

Nets are mostly holes.

Good catch after repair is still dangerous.

Do not catch people.

Come if work comes.

Following needs turning.

He said the last aloud.

Maryam smiled.

"Mara?"

"Yes."

"Good."

He closed the chest.

Outside, somewhere, a goat made a noise that probably meant nothing and almost certainly would be interpreted by someone.

Daniel lay down.

He thought of Andrai mending nets.
Yonah stepping where the rope had been.
Shimon shouting that boats were wood.
Dalia asking how many invisible fish fit on a tablet.
Mattai saying nets were about holes.
Mara warning him not to catch people.
Maryam saying return was not only to their house.

He did not know if he would go back.

He probably would.

Not because the story required fishermen.

Because work might.

That distinction was not strong enough to trust once and for all.

It would need maintenance.

Like everything else.

In the dark, Maryam said, "Sleep."

Daniel laughed softly.

"What?"

"You were about to think too much."

"Yes."

"Sleep."

He did.

In the morning, someone came with a basket full of holes and no fish at all.

That, Daniel thought, was mercifully direct.
