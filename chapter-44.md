# Chapter 44 — Mara Refuses

Mara refused the first miracle before hearing it.

A boy came running on the third morning, gasping, eyes wide with the pleasure and terror of being first.

“The tomb—”

“No,” Mara said.

He stopped so hard he nearly fell.

Maryam looked up.

The room froze.

The boy swallowed.

“The stone is moved.”

No one breathed properly after that.

Maryam stood.

Yosef stood slower.

Ruth said, “Eat first.”

Everyone looked at her as if she had struck a priest.

She pointed at Maryam.

“Bread.
Water.
Then tomb.
Fainting helps no dead or living.”

Maryam took bread.

Did not taste it.

Mara did not take any until Maryam looked at her.

Then they went.

Not all.
Enough witnesses.
Maryam, Mara, Ruth, Naama, Avital, Natan at a distance because words would need supervision, Yosef, and two others who had helped with burial.
Yehudah followed far behind and was not told to leave.
Not welcomed.
Not expelled.

The stone was moved.

The tomb was open.

The body was gone.

That sentence had too many meanings and no permission to choose one.

Maryam entered first.

No one stopped her.

Inside: cloth.
Absence.
A smell of spices, stone, and disturbed air.
No body.
No explanation.
No angel.
No guards declaring theology.
No shining figure.
No tidy answer.

Maryam touched the cloth.

“Yeshua,” she whispered.

Nothing answered.

Mara entered after her and immediately hated every future mouth.

“No,” she said.

Maryam looked at her.

“No one gets to use this yet,” Mara said.
“No one.
Not empty, not risen, not stolen, not sign, not hope, not proof, not victory.
Not yet.”

Avital said, “Good.”

Natan, outside, covered his tablet.

Good.

They inspected because grief did not excuse care.

Who saw the seal last?

Who watched?

Was the stone moved from outside?

Could the body have been taken?

By whom?

Why leave cloth?

Why risk guards?

Were guards bribed?

Did allies move him?

Did enemies steal him?

Was he alive when placed? No.
Maryam’s face ended that question before cruelty could finish it.

Yosef inspected the stone track.

Naama inspected footprints, though morning traffic had already damaged truth.
Ruth questioned the boy who found it until he regretted existence.
Mara looked for anything too convenient and found too many possibilities.

Maryam sat inside the tomb.

Not long.

Long enough.

When she emerged, people were waiting.

Of course.

Someone asked, “What happened?”

Maryam said, “His body is not here.”

No more.

Good.

By noon, no more had become ten stories.

The body was stolen.

The body was raised.

The guards slept.

The guards lied.

The women saw angels.

Maryam saw him.

Mara saw him.

Natan had proof.

Yehudah hid him.

Rome moved him.

God took him.

Barak arranged it.

The disciples stole him, though they were poorly organized enough that Mara almost laughed.

The word resurrection came later that day from someone whose face shone too much.

Mara almost struck him.

Maryam stopped her.

Not because he was right.

Because striking him would make him central.

That night, Mara took charcoal and wrote her account.

Not Natan.

Mara.

She wrote badly, angrily, with crossings out and smudges.

I saw the empty place.

I saw cloth.

I did not see him stand.

I did not see light.

I did not hear sky.

I saw Maryam say his name and not be answered.

I saw men begin building ladders out of absence.

I refuse ladders today.

The body is gone.

This is true.

What it means is not available for stealing.

Then she wrote:

Yeshua was annoying.

Then crossed it out.

Then wrote:

Yeshua was a person.

Then:

Jesus is what future mouths may say.
I do not know that name except as weight in Daniel’s fear.

Then:

Daniel was the old wound inside him.

Then:

None of these names may eat him.

She stopped because she was crying too hard to see.

Yosef found her.

He did not read until she handed it.

He read slowly.

“Good,” he said.

“That is all?”

“It is enough.”

“Nothing is enough.”

“No.”

He sat beside her.

Mara said, “They will make him clean.”

“Yes.”

“They will remove mud.”

“Yes.”

“They will say he knew.”

“Some.”

“They will make Maryam quiet.”

Yosef’s face changed.

“Then write otherwise.”

“I am not a scribe.”

“No.”

“I hate scribes.”

“Often wise.”

She almost smiled.

He continued, “You are someone who can leave.”

She looked at him sharply.

“And sometimes comes back.”

She hated that he remembered.

The next morning, Natan asked if he could copy her account.

“No.”

He nodded.

Then she said, “You may copy this line: The body is gone.
Meaning is not available for stealing.”

Natan wrote it with her name.

Good.

Reports continued.

Some claimed to see Yeshua on the road.
Some in dreams.
Some in rooms.
Some in bread breaking.
Some were grief.
Some lies.
Some hope.
Some perhaps something else.
Mara refused to sort them for other people.

Maryam said little.

But once, near dusk, she stood in the doorway and turned as if someone had called.

No one had.

Mara saw.

Maryam did not explain.

Good.

The ending had opened without asking permission.

Mara refused closure.

This was her witness.

Not denial.

Not belief shaped for men.

Refusal to let absence become a clean table.

She placed an onion beside the empty-tomb note.

For grandeur.

For grief.

For Yeshua, who would have laughed.

Maybe.

No one got to spend that either.
