# Chapter 35 — Judas

The man who would become a problem brought a purse with a broken drawstring.

This was, in retrospect, rude of reality.

He was waiting in Naama's courtyard when they arrived in Jerusalem, seated near the wall with the posture of a man who knew how to occupy space without appearing to claim it.
Not rich.
Not poor.
Clean enough to be intentional.
Dusty enough to have traveled.
His beard trimmed by someone who cared.
His sandals worn but maintained.
His eyes moved before his head did.

Mara saw him first.

Of course.

"No," she said.

Daniel stopped at the entrance.

"What?"

"That man is already a sentence."

The man looked up.

"Is that a local greeting?"

"It can be," Mara said.

Maryam stepped beside Daniel.

Naama came from the inner room carrying a bowl and the expression of a woman who had already decided two arguments before breakfast.

"He came with Joazar's message," Naama said.
"And a purse."

The man stood.

Not too quickly.

Good awareness of threat.
Or performance of harmlessness.
Both.

"My name is Yehudah," he said.

Daniel's stomach dropped.

Not because the name was rare.

It was not.

That made it worse.
The old story had common names, ordinary hands, men whose mothers had called them to meals.
The name did not arrive with thunder.
It arrived with a purse string.

Yehudah bowed first to Maryam.

Good.
Or clever.

Then to Yosef.

Then to Daniel.

Not too low.

Also clever.

"I was told work comes before questions," he said.

He held out the purse.

The leather was good but strained.
The drawstring had frayed where it passed through the mouth, and one side had begun to tear.
Inside, Daniel heard coin weight.

Mara leaned closer.

"Money."

"Yes," Yehudah said.

"Suspicious."

"Often."

Daniel disliked that answer because it was correct.

Yosef took the purse, inspected the seam, and passed it to Daniel.

"What is broken?" Maryam asked.

"The drawstring," Yehudah said.

Mara said, "First answer.
Usually wrong."

He smiled faintly.

"The mouth is tearing because the purse carries more than it was made for.
The cord is good in one place, weak in another.
If it breaks in a crowd, the coins scatter and everyone becomes honest only afterward."

Silence.

Daniel looked at him.

Yehudah looked back.

Good answer.

Too good.

Yosef grunted.

"Yes."

Mara whispered, "I hate when dangerous men understand objects."

Maryam did not smile.

"What is the question?" she asked.

Yehudah glanced toward the back room, where the copied symbols, broken seal, and Jerusalem objects waited.

"Who keeps the money for the work?"

There it was.

Not who are you.

Not are you promised.

Not teacher.

Money.

Finally, someone asked where the grain, travel, copies, food, repairs, witnesses, lodging, debt relief, and room maintenance were actually held.
The most practical question in the world.
Also a knife.

Daniel said, "No one person."

Yehudah nodded.

"Good first answer.
Usually incomplete."

Mara's eyes narrowed.

He continued, "Food routes need coin.
Travel needs coin.
Copying costs material.
Debt witnesses sometimes require fees.
Poor pilgrims cannot always wait for local generosity.
If coin is handled by everyone, no one is accountable.
If handled by one, everyone is vulnerable.
If handled by the generous, praise leaks in.
If handled by the poor, suspicion grows.
If handled by the powerful, the work is bought.
If handled by the careless, the work spills."

Daniel hated every word.

Because he was right.

Maryam asked, "Who sent you?"

"Shifra."

Naama snorted.

"Partly."

Yehudah bowed his head.

"She said I might be useful and should not be trusted quickly."

This sounded exactly like Shifra.

"And Joazar?"

"He asked me to carry the broken seal's account."

"And Barak?" Daniel asked.

Yehudah smiled without warmth.

"Barak dislikes me."

"Why?"

"I understand ledgers."

Mara said, "That is not comforting."

"No."

Good.

Still not comforting.

Yehudah set a small tablet beside the purse.
On it were marks: amounts given, sources unnamed but categorized, food distributed, lodging costs, materials, losses, disputed gifts, gifts refused, gifts renamed, and funds held aside for travelers harmed by confusion at the temple tables.

Natan, who had entered behind them carrying too many writing surfaces despite confiscation, froze.

Yehudah looked at him.

"I used your symbol names.
Poorly in places, perhaps."

Natan took the tablet.

His face changed.

"He has totals."

"Yes," Yehudah said.

Natan looked offended, impressed, and frightened.

Good.

Mara looked at Daniel.

"This is worse than Natan."

"I heard that," Natan said.

"Good."

Maryam sat.

This made everyone else more careful.

"Why bring this to us?" she asked.

Yehudah answered without hesitation.

"Because your work already has money.
Pretending it does not gives the money to whoever notices first."

Daniel closed his eyes.

Damn.

Damn him.

Yehudah continued, "Also because Barak has begun asking who funds the disruptions.
If no account exists, he will invent one.
If a secret account exists, he will expose it.
If an honest account exists, he will call it false, but slower."

The story limped.

So must money.

Maryam said, "Food first."

Yehudah blinked.

"I ate."

"Not the point."

Of course.

They fed him.

He ate like a man who knew refusing would be foolish and overeating would be information.

Daniel disliked him more.

---

The purse repair took most of the afternoon because money made everyone worse.

The purse itself was simple: reinforce the mouth, replace the drawstring, patch the torn eyelets, add a second cord path so weight did not concentrate in one place.
Yosef insisted on two inner divisions after seeing how coin weight shifted.

"A purse that carries everything together lies about what is available," he said.

Yehudah's eyes lit.

Mara groaned.

"Do not encourage him."

Yosef ignored her.

"What divisions?" Maryam asked.

Daniel said, "Food.
Travel.
Emergency."

Hanina, who had come because money and food had begun misbehaving in the same sentence, said, "Food first.
But food coin is not all coin.
Grain, fuel, time, ovens, women carrying.
Do not let purse pretend it feeds alone."

Yehudah wrote that down.

Hanina pointed at him.

"With my name if you use it."

"Yes."

She was surprised.

Good.

Ruth, present because purses attracted stupidity, said, "And no charity purse for men to feel soft while keeping hard accounts elsewhere."

Yehudah wrote that too.

Mara whispered, "He writes too obediently."

Natan, insulted, said, "That is possible?"

"From you, no."

Maryam asked Yehudah, "What do you want?"

Everyone stopped.

Yehudah looked at the purse.

"To keep accounts."

"No," Maryam said.
"That is what you propose to do.
What do you want?"

He paused.

First hesitation.

Important.

"I want the work not to be bought by fools."

"Good," Mara said.
"Not answer."

He looked at her.

She did not blink.

He looked back at Maryam.

"I want to be useful where others are sentimental."

Daniel felt the danger sharpen.

Yehudah continued, perhaps because he knew stopping there would condemn him.
"And I want to know whether this can become more than rooms and repairs."

There.

Not Barak's appetite.

Not Natan's language.

Not Andrai's safety.

Strategy.

Scale.

Movement.

Daniel said, "More?"

Yehudah gestured around the room.

"You have practices that feed, protect, slow fraud, name spectators, prevent followers from being caught, ask who can leave, make stories limp.
Good.
But local.
Fragile.
Dependent on particular women, particular rooms, particular correction.
What happens when Barak outpaces you? When Rome notices? When temple systems adapt? When hunger grows faster than tables? When poor people need more than clever marks? When men with power decide your rooms are disorder?"

Daniel did not answer.

Yehudah leaned forward.

"You need structure at scale."

Mara said, "Large words.
Dangerous."

"Yes."

Maryam said, "Structure can become machine."

"Yes," Yehudah said.
"So can absence of structure."

Good.

Too good.

Yosef ran the new cord through the purse mouth.

"If a frame grows, every joint matters more."

Yehudah nodded.

"Exactly."

Yosef looked at him.

"Do not enjoy that too much."

Yehudah's smile vanished.

Good.

Maryam said, "We will not give one person the purse."

Yehudah bowed his head.

"Good."

"You may help design accounts."

"Yes."

"Accounts are inspected by those carrying food, travel, materials, and those receiving help."

"Yes."

"Women's knowledge counts as record."

Yehudah looked up.

"Yes."

"Not after men's writing.
As record."

He paused.

Then nodded.

"Yes."

Maryam saw the pause.

So did everyone.

Good.

"You will not use numbers to silence faces."

Yehudah said, "No."

"Too fast," Mara said.

He looked at her.

Daniel said, "Numbers make you comfortable."

"Yes."

"Faces?"

"Less."

"Then no alone accounts."

Yehudah breathed.

"Agreed."

Ruth said, "And if coin makes you important, we remove you."

Yehudah looked at the repaired purse.

"How will you know?"

Mara smiled without humor.

"We watch people who become easier to listen to."

He considered.

"Fair."

The purse was repaired by sunset.

It held coin better.

This did not make coin safer.

But it made one danger visible.

For now.

---

Yehudah did not betray anyone that week.

This was worth noting because Daniel's old memory tried to make every gesture foreshadowing and therefore useless for seeing the man in front of him.

He worked.

Well.

Annoyingly well.

He reorganized the material accounts so food, travel, copying, repair, lodging, emergency aid, and disputed gifts were separate.
He insisted every gift include not only amount but condition attached, stated or suspected.
He marked gifts given publicly, privately, anonymously, and manipulatively.
He developed a system for gifts renamed before use, inspired by Barak's bread-hook.
Hanina approved after adding an onion symbol for "food labor not counted by coin."

He found two errors in Natan's copies, one accidental duplication of a phrase that would have made Maryam seem to bless a debt practice she had in fact warned against, and one omission of Dalia's name from a storm note.
Dalia was not present, but Mara said she had felt the insult through the road.

Natan apologized.

Yehudah did not gloat.

This helped.

He also noticed that Elior's recent contributions had begun growing again after several weeks of better humility.

"Not corruption yet," Yehudah said.
"Pattern."

Maryam asked, "What pattern?"

"Giving slightly ahead of being asked, in ways that make refusal harder."

Tirzah, when told, closed her eyes and said, "Yes.
He does that when he fears becoming unnecessary."

That was painfully precise.

Elior corrected.

Badly.

Then better.

Yehudah did not mock him.

This helped too.

Daniel hated that Yehudah helped.

Mara noticed.

"You are punishing him for a story he has not done."

Daniel was adjusting a latch and nearly scraped his hand.

"I know."

"Do you?"

"No."

She sat beside him.

"You heard the name."

"Yes."

"The old one?"

"Yes."

"And now every purse is thirty pieces."

He flinched.

Mara's face changed.

"Is it?"

He said nothing.

She whispered, "Oh."

He had not told her that detail.
Not directly.
The old story's betrayal price had stayed folded with other things too sharp to name.
Thirty silver.
Friend.
Kiss.
Soldiers.
Night.
Garden.

Mara waited.

He said, "In the story, someone close hands him over for money."

"Yehudah?"

"The old form of the name is Judas.
Close enough.
Same root, maybe.
Common enough.
Not proof."

Mara looked toward the courtyard, where Yehudah was arguing with Natan about whether copying materials should be counted as teaching cost or administrative cost.

"Money man with dangerous name," she said.

"Yes."

"That is rude."

"Yes."

"Does he know?"

"No."

"Should he?"

Daniel closed his eyes.

There was the question.

Was hiding this fair? Was telling him cruel? Would the knowledge make him behave differently, perhaps toward the very shape? Would it place an accusation on a man because of a story he had not chosen? Would silence make Daniel treat him unfairly while pretending otherwise?

Mara said, "You are already treating him through it."

"I know."

"Then the room needs the truth."

"Which room?"

"Not public.
Core."

Maryam.
Yosef.
Mara.
Maybe Yehudah? Not yet? Natan? Dangerous.
Ruth? She would say names do not excuse stupidity.
Hanina? She would feed first.

Daniel said, "Maryam and Yosef first."

Mara nodded.

"And me."

"You're here."

"Properly."

"Yes."

She stood.

"Also, if the story says money man betrays you, maybe do not give him the only purse."

"We didn't."

"Good.
Growth."

---

Maryam listened to the thirty silver detail without interrupting.

Yosef's face became wood.

Mara paced like a caged insult.

They sat in the back room of Naama's house after evening work.
The name box was not with them, but Daniel felt its absence like a door in the wrong wall.

He told them the broad shape: a close companion, money, authorities, handing over, kiss, arrest at night.
Not every detail.
Enough.
Too much.

Maryam closed her eyes at "kiss."

Yosef looked at Daniel's hands.

Mara said nothing for so long Daniel checked whether the world had ended.

Then Maryam asked, "Does this man in front of us intend harm?"

"I don't know."

"Has he done harm?"

"Not that."

"Has he helped?"

"Yes."

"Do you trust him?"

"No."

"Because of him or the story?"

Daniel breathed.

"Both.
But I cannot separate them cleanly."

Yosef said, "Then do not pretend to."

Mara stopped pacing.

Yosef continued, "Make structure for the risk you know and the risk you fear."

Daniel nodded.

Maryam said, "No single purse.
No private meetings with authorities through him.
No messages carried by him alone where your safety is concerned.
No accusation without act.
No intimacy created to prove you are not afraid.
No rejection created by fear of story."

That was very good.

Painfully good.

Mara added, "No kisses from money men."

Daniel laughed despite himself.

Maryam almost did.

Yosef said, "No night meetings without witnesses."

That line landed hard.

Daniel nodded.

"Yes."

Mara said, "And if he becomes a friend?"

Silence.

There was the cruelest possibility.

If Yehudah became useful, honest, flawed, funny, irritating, necessary.
If he became someone whose absence would hurt.
If betrayal became not a plot device but the breaking of something real.

Daniel said, "Then more witnesses, not fewer."

Maryam looked at him with grief.

"Do not make friendship impossible to avoid grief."

"I know."

"Do you?"

"No."

Good.

Truth.

They wrote the Judas rules without using the name:

Money-risk:

No single purse.

Accounts inspected by those who carry result.

No private authority meetings through one person.

No safety messages by one carrier alone.

No accusation without act.

No trust performance.

No rejection performance.

No night meetings without witnesses.

No intimacy to disprove fear.

No fear to prevent friendship.

If betrayal comes, grief first, meaning later.

Mara added:

No kisses from money men.

Maryam let it stay.

Yosef added:

A purse carries weight; do not let it choose direction.

Daniel added:

A story can make an innocent man suspicious.
Inspect both man and story.

That last one mattered.

He copied it twice.

---

Yehudah discovered the rules before being told because he was dangerous.

Not the full list.
Not the old story.
But enough.

He came upon Daniel copying the account structure with "money-risk" at the top and stopped before entering.

Good.

He did not pretend not to see.

Better.

Daniel looked up.

Yehudah said, "Is that about me?"

Honest question.

Hard.

Daniel said, "Partly."

Yehudah's face tightened, then smoothed.

No.

Daniel said, "Do not smooth your face."

The smoothness broke.

Good.

Yehudah looked angry now.

Better.

"What risk?"

"Money concentrating power.
Accounts becoming gate.
Strategy replacing people.
Gifts controlling work.
One person carrying messages that should not be alone."

Yehudah listened.

"All true," he said.

"And personal ambition."

He smiled bitterly.

"Also true."

Good.

Daniel waited.

Yehudah asked, "Anything else?"

There it was.

The chance to lie.

The old story stood between them.

Daniel chose carefully.

"There is a story in me that makes men who handle money dangerous before they act."

Yehudah went still.

"Because?"

"Because of my old world."

"Because of the promised-one story?"

Daniel did not answer.

Yehudah understood enough.

His mouth tightened.

"What does the money man do?"

Daniel closed his eyes.

"Hands him over."

Silence.

"To whom?"

"Authorities."

"Why?"

"Texts argue.
Money.
Disillusionment.
Fate.
Evil.
Politics.
Story.
I do not know the man."

Yehudah absorbed this.

"His name?"

Daniel opened his eyes.

"Too close to yours."

Yehudah laughed once, sharply, without humor.

"Ah."

No one spoke.

Yehudah looked toward the courtyard.

"So I entered condemned."

"No."

He looked back.

"Do not insult me with comfort."

Daniel accepted that.

"You entered carrying a shape I fear."

"Better."

"I am trying not to make you carry a deed you have not done."

"Trying."

"Yes."

Yehudah sat, though not invited.

Daniel let him.

Good.

Bad.

Yehudah said, "Do the others know?"

"Maryam, Yosef, Mara."

"Good."

"Good?"

"If you alone carried this, you would treat me worse and call it instinct."

Fair.

Painfully fair.

Yehudah looked at the rules.

"No single purse.
Good.
No private authority meetings through one person.
Good.
No night meetings without witnesses.
Very good." He paused.
"No kisses from money men?"

"Mara."

"Wise."

Daniel laughed once.

Yehudah did not.

"Do you want me to leave?"

Daniel answered slowly.

"No."

"Because I am useful?"

"Partly."

"Because sending me away would be fear?"

"Partly."

"Because you want to prove the story wrong?"

Daniel stopped.

Yehudah saw.

"There."

Yes.

Damn him.

Daniel said, "That too."

"Then do not keep me for that."

"No."

Yehudah leaned back.

"I will not promise never to betray you."

Daniel looked at him.

Yehudah's voice was steady.

"I do not know myself that well.
No one does.
I can promise structures.
Accounts open.
No private authority meetings.
No carrying your safety alone.
No gifts hidden.
If I become angry or disappointed, I will not handle money that day."

That last was unexpectedly good.

Daniel nodded.

"Good."

Yehudah continued, quieter, "And if I begin loving the work because it could become powerful, I will say so."

"Will you know?"

"Maybe not.
That is why others inspect."

Good.

He stood.

"I dislike being a feared story."

"I know."

"No.
You know being feared.
Different."

Daniel bowed his head.

"Yes."

Yehudah left.

He did not slam anything.

This seemed unfairly mature.

Mara, who had absolutely been listening just out of sight, entered.

"That went better than expected."

"How much did you hear?"

"Enough for governance."

"That is not an amount."

"It is now."

---

Barak understood Yehudah faster than Daniel wished.

Of course.

Men who played systems recognized other system-men.

The contact came through Joazar, who looked ashamed while delivering it.
Barak requested a meeting with Yehudah regarding disputed accounts from the warning board, temple complaints, and "possible accommodation."

Accommodation.

A beautiful word for a padded trap.

Yehudah showed the message to Maryam before Daniel.

Good.

Maryam read it.

"No."

Yehudah said, "Agreed."

Then he added, "But we can use the request."

Mara groaned.

"There he is."

Yehudah acknowledged this with a nod.

He proposed a response: no private meeting; Barak could bring specific disputed claims to Naama's room with witnesses, including those allegedly harmed, fair workers, and women carrying the routes.
Accounts would be open in relevant categories but donor names protected where necessary to prevent retaliation or praise-seeking.
Any accommodation must be written plainly and subject to correction.

Natan looked impressed.

Mara looked suspicious.

Maryam said, "Good."

Yosef said, "Also object."

Yehudah nodded.

"Yes.
He must bring the object of dispute."

Daniel said, "He will not."

"Then the refusal is his."

Good.

They sent that.

Barak did not come.

Instead, Lemuel arrived with a complaint about funds used to feed children before offerings.

This was such an obvious trap that even Erel saw it.

The object: a broken tally stick from a food route.

It had been cut incorrectly, perhaps intentionally, to suggest more grain had been received than distributed.
Yehudah identified the mismatch quickly but did not answer alone.
He called Hanina, Tirzah's Jerusalem contact, Naama, and the woman who had baked the bread.
They reconstructed the route.

The grain had not been stolen.

It had been converted partly into soft food for old mouths and partly into fuel because bread without fuel remained theology.

Lemuel looked displeased.

Good.

Yehudah said, "The tally failed because it measured grain only.
It did not measure cooking."

Hanina, later, said she felt vindicated across distance.

They added a cooking labor mark.

Mara hated it aesthetically.

Accepted it morally.

Yehudah's usefulness grew.

So did the danger.

---

Judas—no, Yehudah—laughed for the first time three weeks after entering the work.

Not politely.

Actually.

It was Sima's fault from a distance.

She had sent, through Mara, a new mark proposal for "private, do not lift." It showed a box biting a hand, revised with more teeth and a small angry eye.
Mara unveiled it in Naama's room while everyone argued about whether disputed gifts should be categorized by intention, effect, or ability to refuse.

Yehudah looked at the drawing and laughed.

It surprised him as much as everyone else.

He covered his mouth.

"Sorry."

Mara stared.

"You are capable of joy.
Concerning."

He took the mark.

"This is excellent."

"Of course."

"No, genuinely excellent.
It warns action, not person.
The hand is doing wrong; the box is not evil.
The teeth are memorable."

Mara blinked.

Praise from Yehudah apparently did something different than praise from Daniel.
She recovered by saying, "Do not make it administrative."

"It already is."

She looked wounded.

He laughed again.

Daniel watched.

Something in his chest loosened and tightened at once.

Friendship was entering through competence and laughter.

Damn it.

Maryam saw his face.

Later, she said, "No fear to prevent friendship."

"I know."

"Do you?"

"Less badly."

Good enough for the day.

---

The first actual betrayal was small.

This also mattered.

Yehudah did not sell Daniel to authorities.

He did not kiss him in a garden.

He did not take coin.

He omitted a name.

On an account copy prepared for a room of donors and workers, he listed categories of labor for the food routes: grain purchase, grinding, baking, carrying, distribution, fuel, vessels, recordkeeping.
He named Naama for route coordination, Hanina by reference from home, Tirzah's contact for grain, two men for carrying, and himself for account structure.

He omitted the three women who had done most of the grinding.

Not maliciously, he said.

He did not know their names, he said.

He had meant to add them later, he said.

All plausible.

All insufficient.

The omission was discovered by one of the women, Rinnah, who came to Naama's room with flour on her sleeves and fury in her spine.

"You counted the grain I touched but not my hands."

Silence.

Yehudah went pale.

Good.

Maryam sat.

Mara looked ready to bite.

Daniel did not speak.

Important.

This was not his correction to carry first.

Naama asked Rinnah to speak.

She did.

She spoke of grinding before dawn, of daughters turning millstones, of fuel smoke, of men praising distribution while flour dust lived in women's lungs, of records that made grain visible and labor vanish.

Yehudah listened.

Did not defend after the first failed breath.

Good.

When she finished, he said, "I was wrong."

Rinnah said, "Too small."

He nodded.

"I made your work invisible in the record."

"Yes."

"I counted what men handed over and not what women transformed."

"Yes."

"I used categories that made your labor seem like grain moving by itself."

"Yes."

He looked at Naama.

"I need the names."

Rinnah gave them.

Not gently.

He corrected the copy in front of everyone, then added a new category: transformation labor.

Naama said, "Ugly phrase."

Rinnah said, "Grinding."

So it became grinding, baking, preparing, with names.

Yehudah then asked if the corrected record could be read aloud at the donor room.

Rinnah said, "By me."

Good.

He agreed.

After she left, Yehudah sat very still.

Mara said, "First betrayal."

Daniel flinched.

Yehudah looked at him.

Maryam looked at Mara.

Mara did not apologize.

She said, "Small ones count.
That is how large ones learn paths."

Silence.

Yehudah nodded.

"Yes."

Daniel breathed.

Yes.

Small betrayals count.

This was not condemnation.

It was maintenance.

Yehudah said, "I would like someone else to inspect all records for vanished labor."

Maryam said, "Rinnah."

"Yes."

"And not as favor.
As authority."

He nodded.

Good.

The first betrayal was named, repaired partly, and used to strengthen the structure.

Daniel hated that this made him trust Yehudah more.

Truth was rude.

---

The donor room was awful.

Necessary.

Awful.

It gathered people with coin, grain, access, influence, and the desire to be near work without being too near hunger.
Elior was there, trying to become smaller than his cloak.
Shifra was there because she understood danger.
Joazar came.
Haggai came.
Lemuel came, bored less often now.
Barak did not come but sent ears.
Several women came because Maryam and Naama insisted no donor room could exist without those who knew where gifts landed.

Rinnah read the corrected labor record.

Slowly.

With names.

Men shifted.

Good.

Yehudah stood beside her, not behind.

Better.

When she finished, Elior said, "We did not know."

Rinnah looked at him.

"You knew bread does not grind itself."

He bowed his head.

Excellent.

Yehudah then explained the purse structure: no single purse, categories, inspection, conditions, disputed gifts, hidden hooks, labor records, recipient corrections, no public praise without consent, no gift accepted under a name the work refused, no money for title, no money controlling message.

Haggai said, "You make giving difficult."

Yehudah answered, "Yes."

Mara whispered to Daniel, "I hate that he is good at this."

Daniel whispered back, "Yes."

Then Barak's ear, a man named Seraiah, asked, "Who benefits from making giving difficult?"

Maryam answered before Yehudah could.

"The hungry."

Silence.

She continued, "The giver who is not allowed to buy the story.
The worker whose name is counted.
The child who eats without being displayed.
The widow whose gratitude is not harvested.
The man with coin whose soul is not flattered into rot."

Ruth should have been there.

She would have applauded by insulting someone.

Seraiah said, "Strong words."

Maryam said, "Hunger is stronger."

Yehudah did not improve the answer.

Good.

The donor room ended with fewer gifts than it might have and better ones than it deserved.

This was success.

Also failure.

Good.

---

Daniel and Yehudah spoke afterward in Naama's courtyard under a sky the city had failed to own.

"You looked at me when Mara said first betrayal," Yehudah said.

"Yes."

"Because of the story?"

"Yes."

"And because it was true."

"Yes."

Yehudah nodded.

"I betrayed Rinnah in the record."

"Yes."

"I did not mean to."

"Yes."

"Meaning did not protect her."

"No."

He looked at his hands.

"Do you expect the large betrayal?"

Daniel could lie.

Did not.

"I fear it."

Yehudah smiled faintly.

"Different."

"Yes."

"Do you expect it from me?"

Daniel took too long.

Yehudah nodded once, as if marked.

"Good to know."

"Yehudah—"

"No.
Let it stand.
Better visible."

Daniel stopped.

They sat.

Yehudah said, "I fear something too."

"What?"

"That I will be most dangerous when I am right."

Daniel looked at him.

Yehudah continued, "When money truly needs structure.
When meetings truly need strategy.
When danger truly requires secrecy.
When someone truly must speak to men you dislike.
When I can do useful things others cannot.
That is when I will become easiest to excuse."

Good.

Horrifying.

Daniel said, "Then write that."

Yehudah did.

In the money rules:

Danger increases when competence is real.

Mara later read it and said, "Put that on Natan too."

Natan objected.

No one accepted the objection.

---

The chapter of Judas, if anyone later made one, would be unfair.

Daniel knew this sitting in Jerusalem with the man, the purse, the accounts, the small betrayal repaired, and the large story waiting like a knife under cloth.

A clean betrayer would be easier.

A villain could be refused early.

Yehudah was not clean.

He was vain in specific ways, honest in others, impatient with sentiment, moved by competent mercy, contemptuous of sloppy generosity, vulnerable to scale, capable of laughter, capable of erasure, capable of repair, and carrying a name that made Daniel's old memory flinch.

He was a friend in draft.

That was the danger.

Daniel wrote:

Yehudah:

Brought purse.

Saw money before we admitted money.

Useful.

Ambitious.

Good with ledgers.

Bad with unseen labor unless corrected.

Can accept correction so far.

Danger increases when competence is real.

Do not condemn by story.

Do not excuse by usefulness.

No single purse.

No night meetings without witnesses.

No friendship as proof against fear.

No fear as proof against friendship.

Small betrayals count.

Repair small paths before they become roads.

He paused.

Then added:

If betrayal comes, do not let meaning arrive before grief.

He stared at that.

Avital's sandal had taught them.

No death is meaning before grief.

No betrayal is meaning before wound.

He wrote it too.

Then sat with the page until Mara found him.

She read over his shoulder.

"Good."

"Which part?"

"Friend in draft."

"I didn't write that."

"You should."

He looked at the list.

Then wrote:

Friend in draft.

Mara nodded.

"Drafts can be revised."

"Or abandoned."

"Or ruined by bad scribes."

"Natan?"

"Obviously."

From inside, Natan said, "I heard that."

Mara smiled.

"Good."

The next morning, Yehudah came with the purse.

Repaired.

Used.

Inspected.

He placed it before Maryam.

"Rinnah has agreed to inspect labor records.
Naama will hold one key.
Hanina's contact another.
I hold none alone.
The purse divisions are marked, but coin will be stored in three places when possible.
Disputed gifts separate.
Barak-linked gifts marked with hook.
Elior gifts marked watch for polish.
Anonymous gifts accepted only through two witnesses.
No donor room without food workers.
No account closed without asking who is missing."

Maryam listened.

"Good."

Yehudah looked relieved.

"Not enough," Mara said.

Yehudah nodded.

"No."

Daniel looked at him.

Yehudah met his eyes.

Not condemned.

Not safe.

Present.

For now, that had to be enough.

Yosef took the purse and tested the new drawstring.

It held.

"For now," he said.

The holiest phrase.

Yehudah smiled.

"For now."

Outside, Jerusalem continued: tables, coins, offerings, prayers, schemes, hunger, women with knowledge, men with clean hands, children drawing warning marks, officials watching, Barak sharpening a story, and the sacred machine adjusting to grit.

Inside, the purse held because many hands knew where it could fail.

Daniel did not trust it.

He did not trust Yehudah.

He did not trust himself.

Good.

Trust without inspection had never saved anyone.

They had work.

For now, work would have to hold.
