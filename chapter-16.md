# Chapter 16 — The Wedding Problem

Daniel did not want to go to the wedding.

This was not because he disliked weddings.

He disliked many parts of weddings, but this was different.
He disliked crowds, songs, public joy that could become public shame in one spilled bowl, men who drank past wisdom, women doing ten kinds of labor while men made speeches about blessing, children weaponized by boredom, and old relatives who inspected young people as if evaluating livestock for temperament and future yield.

But he did not dislike weddings in principle.

A wedding was a structure.
Fragile, expensive, load-bearing, full of hidden joints.
Two families tied themselves together in front of witnesses and food, which meant that the witnesses and food were not decorations.
They were part of the structure.
A badly managed wedding could create resentment that lasted longer than the marriage bed.
A generous wedding could build credit for years.
A missing dish, an insulted uncle, a sour jar, a seating error, a song begun at the wrong time—each could become a crack if enough people needed it to.

Maryam liked weddings because they revealed truth.

Yosef endured weddings because refusing invitations created more work than attending them.

Mara liked weddings because people became careless and therefore interesting.

Daniel liked weddings best when he was not inside them.

This wedding was worse because of wine.

Not wine itself.

Wine was ordinary.
Safer than bad water in some contexts, dangerous in quantity, socially important, economically revealing, and generally treated by men as proof that they had become more charming when the evidence suggested the opposite.

The problem was not wine.

The problem was the old story.

A wedding.
A shortage.
A mother.
A son.
Water.
Wine.
Public beginning.
Signs.

The shape glowed at the edge of Daniel’s mind like a coal under ash.

He told himself this wedding could not be that wedding.

First, he was still too young.
Fifteen, perhaps, depending on how one counted and who was counting.
Not a man fully, though his shoulders had begun making arguments in that direction and his voice had settled enough that children no longer laughed when it surprised him.

Second, the wedding was not in the village he associated with the old fragment, though his association was unreliable and geography in memory behaved badly.

Third, no one had asked him to do anything with wine.

Fourth, he had no power to turn water into anything except wet floors and preventable disputes.

Fifth, there were many weddings.
People married constantly because life insisted on continuing despite evidence.

Sixth, he had a plan.

The plan was: attend, carry what was asked, speak boringly, avoid water jars, avoid wine jars, avoid being alone with Maryam near a shortage, and leave with the household intact.

Mara said this was not a plan but a superstition wearing sandals.

She was not invited to comment.

She commented anyway.

They were standing outside while Maryam inspected the wedding bundle.
Bread, cloth, a small gift, and something wrapped so carefully Daniel suspected it was either fragile or socially explosive.

“You cannot avoid water jars at a wedding,” Mara said.

“I can avoid being memorable near them.”

“You already failed that with wells.”

“Wells are not jars.”

“Water is water.”

“That is not technically true.”

She grinned.

“You are frightened.”

“Yes.”

The word came out easily now.

Not comfortably.

Easily.

Mara stopped grinning.

“Will the story be there?”

“No.”

He meant: probably not.

He meant: I cannot know.

He meant: if I say maybe, it will enter the house and sit at dinner.

Mara heard all of that because she was unfair.

She looked toward the doorway where Maryam and Yosef moved through the preparations.

“Then why go?”

“Because not going becomes a story.”

Mara nodded.

That she understood.

Avoidance left footprints.
Sometimes larger than attendance.

“Also,” Daniel said, “Maryam wants to go.”

“Ah.”

This explained everything.

Maryam emerged then and looked at them both.

“Mara, are you coming?”

Mara brightened.

Daniel said, “She is?”

Maryam said, “Her aunt is kin to the bride’s mother.”

Mara looked smug.

Daniel looked at the sky.

The wedding problem had gained a witness with sharp elbows and no respect for sacred dread.

Wonderful.

---

The wedding house was already too full when they arrived.

This was a law of weddings.

No house invited to contain celebration could remain within its intended capacity.
People flowed into courtyard, roof, doorway, lane, adjacent shade, borrowed rooms, and any space not actively occupied by animals or fire.
Children moved in packs.
Women carried food with the coordination of experienced troops.
Men greeted one another too loudly.
Old women sat where they could see everything and be moved by no one.
Musicians tested fragments of melody until the air itself seemed to be tuning.

Daniel entered behind Yosef with a bundle and a defensive expression.

Maryam noticed.

“Face,” she murmured.

He rearranged it into something less like a man approaching a collapsing scaffold.

Mara whispered, “Worse.”

He ignored her.

The bride’s household had done better than Daniel expected.
The courtyard had been swept.
Shade cloth was tied well, though one corner needed checking if wind rose.
Mats were laid with thought.
Food areas were separated from foot traffic, mostly.
Water jars stood along one wall.
Wine jars stood farther inside under supervision.
Good.
Distance between elements.
Clear flow.
Someone competent had planned.

Daniel began relaxing.

This was his first mistake.

Maryam was immediately claimed by women working near the food, which meant she vanished into the structure like a load-bearing post.
Yosef was pulled toward a table whose leg had developed a wobble at the worst possible time.
Daniel was handed a basket and told to carry it to the rear courtyard.

Useful.
Boring.
Excellent.

Mara followed, carrying nothing.

“Why are you following me?”

“To see if the basket becomes prophecy.”

“It contains flatbread.”

“Bread becomes stories.”

“Everything becomes stories if you stand near it.”

She bowed slightly.

They delivered the basket.

Then another.

Then a tray.

Then a bundle of cups.

The work helped.
It always did.
Daniel could trust an object moved from one place to another.
He could check footing, avoid collisions, hold doors, steady a table, adjust a mat corner.
Ordinary actions.
Small load paths.
No symbols unless people insisted.

For an hour, the wedding remained survivable.

Then the table leg broke.

Not the one Yosef had already fixed.

A different table.

Of course.

The table held plates, cups, three bowls, and the dignity of the bride’s uncle, who had just leaned on it while making a point.
The leg snapped near an old knot.
The table dipped.
Cups slid.
One bowl fell.
Mara caught another bowl with reflexes Daniel admired and would never admit to admiring.

The uncle shouted.

The children cheered.

The women near the food turned as one organism.

Daniel grabbed the table edge and lifted enough to stop the remaining dishes from sliding.

“Hold,” he snapped.

A boy nearby froze.

“Not you.
The other side.”

The boy grabbed the opposite edge.

Yosef appeared, because broken furniture called to him across distance.
He inspected the leg.

“Old crack,” he said.

The uncle said, “It was sound.”

Daniel said nothing.

Mara said, “It was waiting.”

Daniel stepped on her foot lightly.

She kicked him back harder.

Yosef needed a temporary support.
Not a full repair.
A wedding repair.
The table did not need to serve generations; it needed to hold through the day without becoming part of family lore.
Unfortunately, it already had.

Daniel looked around.

A spare bench.
Too tall.
A bundle of kindling.
Too unstable.
A storage block near the wall.
Useful if cut.
No time.
A folded mat under a basket.
No.
A short section of wood beside a doorway, perhaps from a dismantled frame.
Good enough if wedged.

He pointed.

Yosef nodded.

Daniel fetched it.

Mara cleared children using the phrase “unless you want the table to eat your feet,” which was effective and not approved.

Yosef and Daniel wedged the support beneath the broken corner, tied it to the leg stump with cord, and shifted heavier dishes away.
The table stood.

Ugly.

Temporary.

Honest.

The bride’s uncle tested it by pressing down.

Yosef caught his wrist.

“Do you test bridges by jumping?”

The uncle withdrew, offended.

Mara whispered, “Some people do.”

Daniel almost laughed.

The table held.

A few people clapped.
This was unnecessary and alarming.

Yosef said, loudly enough, “It will hold if no one leans on it.”

This placed responsibility where it belonged.

Good.

The moment passed.

Mostly.

Daniel returned to carrying things and told himself furniture failure did not count as story.

---

The shortage began as a whisper.

Shortages often did.

Plenty announced itself.
Lack moved through corners.

Daniel noticed first in the flow of cups.

At the start, cups went inward empty and outward full.
Servants and cousins moved easily between the storage area and the guests.
Later, the pattern changed.
More empty cups returned than full ones left.
A young man carrying a jar walked too quickly, then too slowly.
Two women bent over a storage area and spoke with faces angled away from the room.
A man checked one jar, then another, then pretended he had been looking for something else.

Daniel felt the old coal flare.

No.

He turned away.

This was a wedding.
Weddings had shortages.
People drank too much.
Hosts miscalculated.
Jars cracked.
Relatives arrived with extra cousins.
None of this required prophecy.
It required inventory management.

He went to help Yosef with the repaired table.

The table was fine.

Infuriatingly fine.

Mara appeared beside him.

“Something is wrong.”

“No.”

“Yes.”

“You do not know that.”

“Your face does.”

“My face is unreliable.”

“It is loud.”

He looked toward the storage area despite himself.

Maryam was there now.

Of course she was.

She had been drawn to the whisper the way Yosef was drawn to cracked wood.
The bride’s mother stood beside her, pale under the effort of appearing calm.
A younger woman covered jars with cloth.
A boy was sent away, then called back, then sent away again.

Daniel’s stomach dropped.

Mara followed his gaze.

“Wine?”

“Do not say it.”

“Wine.”

He closed his eyes.

“Why not say it?”

“Because saying it makes it more like—”

He stopped.

Mara finished softly, “The story.”

He nodded.

She did not mock.

That was how he knew she was worried too.

Maryam looked up.

Across the crowded courtyard, her eyes found Daniel.

Not calling.

Not yet.

He looked away.

Coward.

He knew the word.
Accepted it.
Continued looking away.

If Maryam called him into this, everything became worse.
If he helped, the shape sharpened.
If he refused, the wedding family might be shamed.
If he overreacted, he might create the very story he feared.
If he underreacted, he left people under a social beam because he was frightened of being seen.

A wedding shortage was not death.

No.

But shame could last.
Debt could grow.
A family could be marked as poor, careless, inhospitable.
A bride could carry whispers into marriage.
A groom’s family could remember.
Small social failures became structural cracks because people liked to pretend kindness had budgets while insult had none.

Maryam began walking toward him.

No.

He met her halfway because letting her cross the entire courtyard would attract attention.

“What?” he said too sharply.

Her face registered the sharpness and set it aside for later.

“They are short.”

“I know.”

“Wine.”

“I know.”

Mara hovered near enough to hear.

Maryam’s voice stayed low.

“Not gone.
Short.”

“That is different.”

“Yes.”

“How much?”

“Enough to shame them before the end if nothing changes.”

Daniel looked toward the jars.

“No.”

Maryam did not ask what he meant.

“I am not asking for wonders,” she said.

The word wonders cut too close.

“I do not have wonders.”

“I know.”

“Do you?”

Maryam’s face changed.

Regret, perhaps.
Or hurt.

Daniel hated himself immediately.

She kept her voice quiet.

“I have a frightened woman, a crowded house, thirsty guests, and time running out.”

He looked away.

“I cannot make wine.”

“I know.”

“Then why are you asking me?”

“Because you see systems when others see jars.”

He looked back.

That was worse than asking for miracle.

It was asking for him.

Mara said, very softly, “You said a roof belongs to everyone under it.”

Daniel glared at her.

She did not flinch.

Maryam waited.

No pressure.

That was the pressure.

Daniel breathed.

“What resources?”

Maryam’s shoulders lowered a fraction.

Not relief.

Action.

“Some wine remains.
More watered than they want people to know.
Water is available.
Herbs.
Fruit syrup, little.
Honey, less.
Empty jars.
Full water jars for washing, not drinking.
Some guests already too drunk to judge quality.
Some guests important enough to care.
Groom’s uncle watching the storage.”

Daniel stared.

“You have inventoried the crisis.”

Maryam said, “Yes.”

Mara looked impressed.

Daniel did too.

He should not have been surprised.

“What do they need?” Maryam asked.

“Not wine.”

Maryam’s eyes sharpened.

“They need the appearance of continued hospitality long enough for the feast to end without a story forming around shortage.”

“Yes.”

This was not a miracle problem.

This was a logistics problem wearing terrifying clothes.

Daniel looked at the flow of guests.

“How long?”

Maryam told him.

He calculated badly, then better.
Cups per group.
Rate of drinking.
Which guests mattered socially.
Which could be slowed by food.
Which could be served watered wine without complaint because they had already passed judgment’s threshold.
Which elders required dignity, not quantity.
Which women working near food had not been given enough and would notice neglect faster than dilution.
Which musicians could be fed to extend a song and delay distribution.
Which children could be given sweetened water and told it was special because they were not idiots but enjoyed official exceptions.

A plan formed.

Not water into wine.

Panic into pacing.

It was ugly.

It was ordinary.

It might work.

Daniel said, “Do not fill large cups.”

Maryam nodded.

“Smaller pours.
More food at the same time.
Salted things away from the heaviest drinkers, not toward them.
Sweet water for children first, publicly, so water service looks intentional.
Move one jar of the better wine where the important guests can see it served from, but stretch it with the weaker.
Do not let them see mixing.
Do not call attention.
Musicians start again before anyone asks why cups are slow.”

Mara whispered, “That is devious.”

“Yes.”

Maryam said, “And the washing jars?”

Daniel flinched.

There it was.

Stone jars.
Water.
Wedding.
Mother.
Son.

Old memory with edges.

He looked toward the jars near the wall.
Large water jars used for washing.
Not wine.
Not drinking.
Ritual, practical, social.
He felt the story lean.

“No,” he said.

Maryam followed his gaze.

Understanding entered her face.

“We do not touch those.”

“Good.”

“Water from the well jars only.”

“Good.”

Mara said, “Why?”

Daniel said, “Because some objects are too available to story.”

Mara accepted this, which showed growth or fear.

Maryam said, “Can you explain this to them?”

“No.”

The answer came instantly.

Maryam nodded.

Of course.

If Daniel explained, the plan became his.
If Maryam explained, it became household management.
If the bride’s mother explained, it became authority.

Maryam said, “Tell me again.”

He did.

She repeated it back more clearly, with fewer technical words and more attention to pride.

Then she returned to the storage area.

Daniel stayed where he was, breathing.

Mara stood beside him.

“You helped.”

“No.”

“Yes.”

“I did not make wine.”

“Good.
That would have been suspicious.”

He almost laughed.

Then he realized he was shaking.

Mara saw.

“Sit.”

“No.”

“Sit or I will tell Maryam.”

He sat on a low wall.

Mara stood in front of him, blocking the view just enough.

“You are not in the story,” she said.

He looked up.

“You do not know that.”

“No.
But right now you are sitting badly on a wall at a wedding while women save foolish drinkers from noticing numbers.
That is too ridiculous to be prophecy.”

He laughed then.

Quietly.

It helped.

---

The plan worked because women did most of it.

This was not modesty.
It was mechanics.

Maryam carried the plan into the storage area.
The bride’s mother absorbed it, resisted two parts, accepted three, altered one, and improved another.
Two aunties implemented distribution changes with terrifying speed.
A younger cousin was reassigned from panicked jar-checking to carrying food, which suited his intelligence better.
The musicians were encouraged to resume by means of flattery and a bowl of something fried.
Children received sweetened water in small cups and immediately became proud of having been given a separate thing.

The better wine remained visible.

The weaker wine moved invisibly.

Food slowed complaint.

Songs filled gaps.

The groom’s uncle was served personally by the bride’s mother from the visible jar and became too honored to inspect further.

The feast continued.

Not perfectly.

Perfect would have been suspicious.

It continued with enough grace that shortage did not become the story.

Daniel watched from the edge.

He did not approach the jars.

He did not speak to the bride’s mother.

He did not let anyone see him calculating pours with his eyes.

Mara did, and kicked his foot when he began doing it too obviously.

Yosef eventually came to sit beside him.

“You look ill,” he said.

“I am attending a wedding.”

“Yes.
But more than usual.”

Daniel looked at him.

Yosef followed his gaze to the storage area.

“Ah.”

Maryam must have told him.

Or Yosef saw because he knew.

“No wonders,” Daniel said.

“No.”

“No water jars.”

“No.”

“No signs.”

Yosef considered the courtyard: people laughing, eating, singing, drinking less than they believed, a family’s shame diverted through timing and competent women.

“No sign people will understand,” he said.

Daniel looked at him sharply.

Yosef kept his eyes on the feast.

“Not every sign is for crowds.”

“I hate that.”

“Yes.”

Daniel leaned forward, elbows on knees.

“I am trying not to become a sign.”

Yosef said, “Then do not confuse that with refusing to be useful.”

“I did not refuse.”

“No.”

A pause.

Yosef added, “Your mother did not ask you for wine.”

“I know.”

“She asked for help.”

“I know.”

“Do you?”

Daniel shut his eyes.

The difference again.
Always the difference.

Knowing in words.
Knowing in the body.
Knowing before fear.
Knowing after.

He opened them.

Maryam moved through the feast as if she had been born inside such structures.
The bride’s mother stood straighter.
The groom’s family laughed.
The bride, unaware or aware and choosing dignity, smiled at someone Daniel could not see.

No miracle.

A social collapse avoided.

A shortage transformed not into abundance, but into enough.

Enough was dangerous.

People mistook enough for blessing all the time.

Perhaps it was.

Daniel was not ready for that.

---

Late in the evening, the bride’s mother came to Maryam.

Daniel saw from across the courtyard.
He could not hear at first.
The woman took Maryam’s hands.
Her face had the exhausted brightness of someone who had stepped over a pit in darkness and only afterward understood the depth.

Maryam tried to deflect.

The bride’s mother insisted.

Maryam shook her head and pointed toward the other women.

Good.

Make others visible.

The bride’s mother nodded, wiping at her eyes.

Then she looked toward Daniel.

No.

Maryam caught her wrist.

Not harshly.

A small motion.

The woman looked back.

Maryam said something.

The woman looked at Daniel again, differently.

Not awe.

Recognition, perhaps.
Or gratitude redirected through caution.

Then she looked away.

Good.

The story had tried to form and Maryam had put a hand on its shoulder.

Daniel breathed.

Mara, beside him, said, “Your mother is frightening.”

“Yes.”

“In a useful way.”

“Yes.”

The bride’s mother did not approach him that night.

This was mercy.

The groom’s uncle did, unfortunately.

He was broad, cheerful, and had received just enough wine to believe himself profound.

“You,” he said.

Daniel looked around, hoping there was another you.

There was not.

“You are Yosef’s boy.”

“Yes.”

“Good table repair.”

“Temporary support.”

“Good support.”

“Temporary.”

The uncle leaned closer.

“The table held.”

“Because no one leaned on it after.”

The uncle laughed.

“I leaned a little.”

Daniel stared.

The uncle laughed harder and clapped his shoulder with enough force to test Daniel’s own foundation.

“You builders.
Always worrying.”

“Yes.”

“Good.
The world needs worriers.”

This sentence, unfortunately, was also good.

The uncle moved on before Daniel could respond.

Mara said, “The world needs worriers.”

“Do not.”

“I am keeping that.”

“Do not.”

She grinned.

---

The shortage did not become public.

Not fully.

That was victory.

It did become known to the people who had prevented it, which meant it became a quieter story, carried in hands rather than mouths.
The bride’s mother would remember.
Maryam would remember.
The women who stretched the service would remember.
The younger cousin reassigned from panic to food would remember that he had been useful rather than shamed.
The children would remember special sweet water and perhaps nothing else.
The groom’s uncle would remember being honored with visible wine and not know he had been managed like a top-heavy jar.

Daniel would remember the washing jars untouched by plan.

He kept looking at them as the feast wound down.

Stone.
Water.
Waiting.

Objects became dangerous when stories stood near them.

Mara noticed.

“Do you want me to spill something in front of them?”

“What?”

“To make them less meaningful.”

“No.”

“I could.”

“I know.”

“That is why I offered.”

He smiled despite himself.

“No.”

Maryam came to them near the end, tired and carrying a bowl.

“Eat.”

“I ate.”

“No.
You watched food.”

Mara accepted the bowl first.

Daniel took bread and something savory he did not identify.

Maryam sat beside him.

For a while they ate without speaking.

Then Daniel said, “You asked.”

“Yes.”

“You knew what it looked like.”

“Yes.”

He waited.

Maryam looked out at the courtyard.

“I also knew a woman was afraid.”

The answer was unfair because it was complete.

Daniel tore the bread.

“I was afraid too.”

“Yes.”

“You still asked.”

“Yes.”

He looked at her.

Maryam’s face was gentle and tired.

“I will not let your fear decide alone.
Not when others stand under it.”

That should have angered him.

It did not.

It made him feel, strangely, less abandoned by his own dread.

He whispered, “Thank you.”

Maryam nodded once.

Mara, chewing, said, “This food is better than prophecy.”

Daniel laughed.

Maryam looked at Mara.

“Most food is.”

---

They left after midnight, though midnight was Daniel’s old word and no one around him would have used it that way.
The night was deep.
The feast still breathed behind them but softly now, like a fire settling into coals.
Some guests slept where they had sat.
Children lay in heaps.
The musicians had gone quiet or unconscious.
Women continued working because celebrations ended for some people before they ended for others and after for most women.

Maryam carried leftover bread wrapped in cloth.

Yosef carried the gift bundle, lighter now.

Mara carried nothing and claimed this was because she had carried the moral burden of the evening.

Daniel carried a small broken cup someone had handed him to repair later.

Of course.

The road home was cool.

For once, no one spoke until the wedding sounds faded.

Then Yosef said, “You did well.”

Daniel looked at him.

“I sat on a wall.”

“Yes.”

“That was the visible part.”

Yosef smiled faintly.

“The visible part is often misleading.”

Maryam walked ahead, listening.

Mara said, “I also did well.”

Yosef said, “You did not spill anything near the water jars.”

“I considered it as an act of mercy.”

“I know.”

Daniel looked at the broken cup in his hands.

The fracture ran along the side, not clean enough for a full repair, but perhaps enough for some use if bound.
Or it could become shard, fill, memory.
Not every broken cup needed to become a cup again.

He thought of wine.

Water.

Hospitality.

Shortage.

A mother asking help.

A son saying no before saying yes.

Not exact.

Not exact.

Not exact.

Still too close.

He said, “This cannot happen again.”

Maryam slowed.

Yosef glanced at him.

Mara snorted.

Daniel looked at her.

“What?”

“You say that after everything.”

“I mean this specific shape.”

“Shapes return.”

“That is not comforting.”

“It was not meant to be.”

Maryam turned.

“Then if it returns, we ask again what is broken.”

Daniel closed his eyes.

Of course.

“And what can be done,” Yosef said.

“And what it can bear,” Mara added.

They all looked at her.

She shrugged.

“I listen.”

Daniel laughed softly.

The road bent toward home.

Behind them, a wedding continued becoming memory.
Ahead, the house waited: door, threshold, beam, chest, tools, the place where the old story could be spoken carefully and then put away.

At the doorway, Maryam paused.

“What will you put on the list?” she asked.

Daniel knew which list.

The dangerous one.
The one with fit and not-fit.
The one that had once been stones in a dry channel and now lived as method more than object.

He looked at the broken cup.

“Not water into wine.”

Mara said, “Panic into pacing.”

Yosef nodded.

“Shortage into enough,” Maryam said.

Daniel swallowed.

That sounded too beautiful.

Too dangerous.

Also true.

He opened the door.

Inside, the house smelled of wood, ash, stored cloth, and the ordinary life that kept rescuing him without asking permission.

He set the broken cup near the work area.

Tomorrow, perhaps, he would see what it could still hold.

Not tonight.

Tonight, the jars remained water.

The wine remained wine.

The story, denied a miracle, had to settle for logistics.

For now, that was mercy.
