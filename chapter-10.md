# Chapter 10 — Ordinary Is Harder Than It Looks

Yeshua decided to become ordinary at nine.

This was ambitious.

Mara said so immediately.

“You cannot decide that.”

“Why?”

“Ordinary people do not decide to be ordinary with that face.”

“What face?”

“The face of someone planning a roof.”

“I am not planning a roof.”

“Exactly.”

The problem was not that he wanted attention. Usually he wanted less. The problem was that avoiding attention had become another way of organizing his life around it. He watched himself being watched. He chose not to speak and then noticed who noticed. He moved away from crowds and then imagined what rumor would do with the absence. He tried to act like a child and discovered that acting like a child was an adult project.

Ordinary, he learned quickly, could not be built directly.

Like comfort.

Or trust.

Or a door that did not squeak after someone said, “Make it not squeak,” without addressing the warped frame.

Maryam listened to his plan while sorting lentils.

“What does ordinary mean?”

He had prepared for this.

“Less seeing.”

Mara snorted.

Maryam looked at him.

“You will stop seeing?”

“No.”

“Then?”

“Say less.”

“About danger?”

“Maybe.”

Maryam continued sorting.

“People may be hurt.”

“I know.”

“So ordinary means letting harm come?”

“No.”

“Then?”

He hated this.

“Not be center.”

Maryam softened.

“Ah.”

Mara stopped mocking.

Yosef, from the bench, said, “A beam cannot become ordinary by pretending not to carry load.”

“I am not a beam,” Daniel said.

“No.”

Yosef looked up.

“But if load rests, pretending does not remove it.”

Maryam added, “Also, not all load is yours.”

Both sentences annoyed him by being true in opposite directions.

Mara said, “Try being useful and boring.”

“That sounds possible.”

“It is not. But try.”

So he did.

---

His first ordinary project was carrying water without comment.

This failed within an hour.

The path from the water source had a loose stone near the bend. He had noticed it before. Everyone had. It shifted underfoot if stepped on from one angle. Adults avoided it from habit. Children forgot. Visitors stumbled. It had not caused serious harm, which meant it had become accepted.

Yeshua decided not to mention it.

Ordinary.

Then Sima, younger and faster than caution, ran past with a small jar and stepped on the stone.

It shifted.

She fell.

The jar cracked.

Her knee scraped.

The world did not end.

That made it worse.

Sima cried. Her mother scolded her for running. Maryam came with water. Daniel stood with his own jar and felt guilt climb him like ivy.

Mara looked at him from across the path.

“You saw.”

He nodded.

“Did not say?”

“I was being ordinary.”

She stared.

Then, with rare restraint, did not insult him until Sima had been cleaned and the jar assessed.

The stone was moved. The path was reset. The jar became dry storage if Sima approved. She did after being allowed to choose what it would hold.

Only then did Mara return.

“Ordinary people move stones.”

“I know.”

“Apparently not.”

“I know now.”

“Ordinary is harder than it looks.”

That became the title of the day because Mara repeated it until everyone hated it.

Maryam, later, said, “Do not make silence your costume.”

Yosef said, “If a stone is loose, moving it is not spectacle.”

Daniel wrote neither sentence down, which was why Mara found charcoal and wrote one on a scrap for him.

Do not make silence your costume.

She added underneath:

Move obvious stones, idiot.

Maryam made her remove the last word.

The spirit remained.

---

His second ordinary project was to stop correcting adults.

This failed more slowly and with greater social cost.

A man named Haggai—not later Jerusalem Haggai, just a local Haggai with poor knot judgment—was tying a load onto a cart. The rope passed over the bundle badly. One knot looked secure only because the load had not moved yet. Yeshua saw it. He said nothing.

Ordinary.

The cart moved.

The bundle shifted.

The rope tightened along the wrong line and snapped a small clay vessel at the edge.

Not catastrophic. Embarrassing. Wasteful.

Haggai cursed the rope.

Mara, who had begun following Daniel’s ordinary attempts because failure entertained her, said, “Rope innocent.”

Haggai glared.

Yeshua closed his eyes.

Maryam was not there. Yosef was. Worse.

Yosef walked over, inspected the rope, the load, the broken vessel, and Yeshua’s face.

“What did you see?”

Daniel considered lying.

This was progress only if measured from complete stupidity.

“Knot wrong.”

“When?”

“Before.”

“Why silence?”

“Trying not correct adults.”

Haggai looked offended.

Yosef looked tired.

Mara looked delighted.

Yosef said, “Correction is not the same as contempt.”

Haggai muttered, “From a child it feels close.”

Maryam would have handled that better.

Yosef did his best.

“Then inspect knot, not pride.”

Good.

Haggai retied the load badly again out of spite. Yosef made him do it a third time. The third held.

Later, Daniel apologized to Haggai for withholding what he saw. Haggai did not know what to do with that and accepted by complaining about the price of rope.

Good enough.

The broken vessel shard went into the chest.

Lesson: silence can be pride wearing humility.

Mara liked that one.

Too much.

---

The third attempt at ordinary involved ignoring a crowd.

This actually worked for half a day.

A family had come because their roof leaked, but they brought three relatives, two neighbors, and a boy who only wanted to see whether Yeshua would say something strange. Maryam identified the boy immediately and gave him a basket to sort. He regretted curiosity.

Yosef inspected the roof.

Yeshua stayed below, carrying materials, not speaking unless asked.

Good.

Mara watched from a wall, suspicious.

The leak was simple enough. A channel clogged by leaves and a small collapse of dried material. Yosef cleared, patched, and explained. Yeshua held tools. Said little. No one gasped. No one asked for a prediction. The spectator boy sorted poorly but productively.

Ordinary.

Then the grandmother asked, “What does the child think?”

The courtyard tensed.

Yeshua looked at Maryam.

Maryam said, “The child is carrying tools.”

The grandmother laughed.

“But he sees.”

Maryam replied, “So do others if they look.”

Good.

Yosef handed the grandmother a small stick and drew the water path for her.

“What do you see?”

The grandmother frowned, then pointed.

“Water sits there.”

“Yes.”

“Then clear before rain.”

“Yes.”

She smiled despite herself.

“Old eyes for me too.”

“No,” Mara called from the wall. “Just eyes.”

The family laughed.

The moment passed.

Yeshua had said nothing.

This was ordinary done correctly: not hiding sight, but distributing it.

On the walk home, Daniel said, “That worked.”

Maryam nodded.

“Because you did not disappear. You carried tools.”

Yosef said, “And she saw.”

Mara said, “And I improved the theology.”

No one granted this.

She accepted credit anyway.

---

The first plan to disappear came after the lean-to collapse.

It did not collapse on anyone.

This was the mercy.

It collapsed on stored wood, two baskets, and a goat tether that probably deserved better. The lean-to belonged to Abner’s cousin, which meant Abner had opinions and no responsibility. It had been built quickly, loaded slowly, and inspected never. The roof sagged over months. Everyone saw. No one owned the seeing.

Daniel saw too.

He had not spoken because the lean-to was not near the house, not holding people, not urgent, and he was practicing boundaries.

Then rain came.

Then wind.

Then a beam slipped. The roof folded. The goat escaped, which complicated the moral tone.

People gathered.

No one was hurt.

Still, the collapse sounded enough like the old beam that Daniel vomited behind a wall.

Mara found him.

She did not joke.

“Did you see?”

“Yes.”

“Why not say?”

“Not mine.”

She sat beside him.

“Was anyone under?”

“No.”

“Could have been?”

“Yes.”

“Then maybe partly yours.”

He wiped his mouth.

“I cannot carry every roof.”

“No.”

“I cannot inspect every lean-to.”

“No.”

“I cannot become the person people wait for.”

“No.”

“I cannot ignore what I see.”

“No.”

“I hate you.”

“Not relevant.”

He laughed weakly.

That evening, he made the plan.

Leave before dawn. Go to a nearby village where fewer people knew him. Work under another name? No, Yeshua was his name here; changing it would be melodramatic and impractical. Work quietly. Avoid markets. Avoid roofs. Impossible. Avoid people. More impossible. Maybe become a laborer under Yosef’s cousin for a season. Send word. Return later when rumors cooled.

He did not tell anyone.

This lasted until Maryam asked why he was packing the wrong cord.

Mothers were unfair.

---

The plan was reviewed by committee.

Maryam, Yosef, Mara, and eventually Hanina because hunger had brought her over and she refused to leave during stupidity.

Daniel presented badly.

“I could go where people do not ask.”

Mara said, “They will ask worse because you are new.”

Yosef said, “What work?”

“Labor.”

“Whose tools?”

“I can learn.”

Maryam asked, “What fear decides this?”

He hated the question because it always arrived with tools.

“I do not want people hurt because they wait for me.”

“Good fear,” Maryam said.

He looked up.

“Good?”

“Fear can tell truth. Then it must sit down before ruling.”

Hanina said, “If you leave, roofs remain foolish.”

“Exactly.”

She pointed an onion at him.

“And people remain foolish. You are not that important.”

Helpful.

Painful.

Mara said, “If you leave because you want the story to lose you, you are still letting story choose road.”

Daniel looked at her.

She shrugged.

“I listen.”

Yosef asked, “Can you teach others to see?”

Daniel paused.

The question had been present all along.

He had avoided it because teaching created followers, followers created crowds, crowds created old story, old story created wood.

Maryam saw the chain on his face.

“Teaching is not catching if they can leave.”

That sentence was not yet fully formed in their household, but its bones were there.

Mara added, “Do not catch people.”

Hanina said, “Teach them to fix their own stools so they stop bringing them during supper.”

Practical.

Yosef said, “If sight distributes, waiting lessens.”

Daniel looked at his packed cord.

The plan to disappear had assumed only two choices: stay and become center, or leave and reduce harm.

There was a third: stay while making himself less necessary.

This was harder and less romantic.

So probably correct.

They made a new plan.

Teach obvious signs.

Move loose stones.

Ask where water goes.

Ask what the object must still do.

Ask who uses it.

Ask whether repair hides danger.

Say maybe.

Call Yosef.

Call Maryam.

Call Mara if the problem includes goats, children, marks, or someone becoming shiny.

Mara approved this jurisdiction.

Daniel unpacked.

Maryam made him eat before anyone praised the decision.

Good.

---

The first teaching circle had five children and one adult pretending not to learn.

Erel was there.

So were Lavi, Sima, a boy named Rafi, and Mara because she claimed to be quality control. The adult was Haggai with the bad knots, sitting near enough to hear while repairing a strap very slowly.

Yosef brought objects from the chest.

Not the most painful ones.

A loose peg. A cracked handle. A wrong roof-line scrap. A short cord. Ruth’s bowl shard. Lavi’s box corner copy.

Yeshua stood before them and wanted to flee.

Maryam said from the doorway, “Food first.”

They ate.

Teaching while hungry made everyone worse.

Then he began.

“What does it still need to do?”

The children answered badly.

Good.

He held up the loose peg.

“What do you see?”

“Wood.”

“Small.”

“Dirty.”

“Yeshua’s face looks scared.”

That was Sima.

Mara applauded.

Daniel breathed.

“Yes. Also peg loose.”

They tested. Pushed from one side, then another. Load changes. Water goes where it can. Smooth can hide crack. A box can keep being box. A bowl for chickens may still be useful. A cord too short for storm is still cord. Do not blame bearer before inspecting fit.

Haggai corrected a knot from the side.

Everyone pretended not to notice except Mara, who noticed loudly.

By the end, no one had become disciple, which was a relief.

They had become slightly more annoying to adults with bad repairs.

This was success.

---

Ordinary became redefined.

Not invisible.

Not silent.

Not unneeded.

Ordinary meant work did not require spectacle to continue. A loose stone moved without announcement. A child taught to test a peg. A roof owner shown water path. A mother allowed to say no when her son was treated as charm. A mistake admitted before it became story. A plan to flee interrupted by onions.

Yeshua remained strange.

This was undeniable.

Daniel remained inside him with old memories and future fragments.

Jesus still waited outside speech, though the shape of the old story pressed closer each year.

But ordinary was not the absence of strangeness.

Ordinary was bread after fear. Tools returned. Mud cleaned. Lessons repeated. Names called for supper. People allowed to leave.

One evening, after the teaching circle, Erel lingered.

“What?” Daniel asked.

Erel looked at the chest.

“Can I come again?”

“Yes.”

“Am I following you?”

The question struck too early.

Daniel was not ready.

Mara, of course, answered from the wall.

“Are you able to leave?”

Erel frowned.

“Yes.”

“Then maybe learning, not following.”

Daniel looked at her.

She shrugged.

“I improve things.”

Erel considered.

“I am learning.”

“Good,” Daniel said.

“From Yeshua?”

“From the objects first.”

Erel nodded.

“And Yosef.”

“Yes.”

“And Maryam.”

“Definitely.”

“And Mara?”

Mara leaned forward.

“Unofficially.”

Erel smiled.

“Unofficially.”

Good.

A word that would become important later had entered safely.

For now.

---

That night, Daniel sat with Maryam near the doorway while the house settled.

“You did not disappear,” she said.

“No.”

“Do you still want to?”

“Yes.”

“Good.”

He looked at her.

“Good?”

“If the wish remains visible, it cannot pretend to be wisdom unnoticed.”

Another impossible sentence.

He leaned against the doorframe.

“Ordinary is hard.”

“Yes.”

“I thought it meant less.”

“Sometimes it means more people carrying less each.”

He looked toward the teaching chest.

“Distribute sight.”

Maryam nodded.

“Distribute sight. Keep returning.”

“Come home.”

“Yes. But not as net.”

He did not yet understand how much that mattered.

He would.

From outside, Mara shouted, “Your goat jurisdiction needs work!”

Daniel stood.

“I do not have goat jurisdiction.”

Maryam smiled.

“You may now.”

He went out.

The goat had tangled its tether around a post because someone had tied a knot badly. Erel was there, already inspecting the line. Lavi held the goat badly but bravely. Sima explained that the goat had made several poor choices. Mara supervised with unbearable satisfaction.

Daniel started to take over.

Stopped.

“What do you see?” he asked Erel.

Erel looked at the knot.

“Wrong path. Pulling makes worse.”

“Good.”

“What can be done?”

“Slack first.”

“Good.”

They fixed it together.

The goat did not thank them.

Ordinary work rarely did.

Maryam called from inside.

“Yeshua, supper.”

He turned.

The name called him home.

Not away from work.

Through it.

For now, ordinary held.
