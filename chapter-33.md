# Chapter 33 — Tables

The first table overturned accidentally.

This disappointed everyone who wanted symbolism and relieved everyone who had to clean.

It happened near the outer court, not in the place Daniel feared most, and not because he had made a decision.
A money changer’s table had one bad leg, two good legs, and a fourth leg improved by optimism.
A pilgrim bumped the corner while arguing exchange rates.
The table tipped.
Coins spilled like metal rain.
A dove cage slid.
The doves objected.
People shouted.

Daniel was ten paces away.

Of course.

He moved before deciding.

So did Yosef.

So did Maryam, because doves in a crowd meant children in panic.
Mara moved toward the cage.
Erel moved toward a child.
Natan moved toward the question and was physically redirected by Ruth, who had arrived in Jerusalem without authorization and considered that sufficient authorization.

The table did not fully fall.

Daniel caught one edge.

Yosef braced the leg.

The money changer shouted, “Careful!”

Mara shouted, “Then build better!”

The table was righted.

The coins were not all recovered.

This became a problem.

A missing coin near a holy place could produce more accusation than an actual injury.
The money changer accused the pilgrim.
The pilgrim accused the rate.
A boy denied stealing before anyone accused him, which was unwise but not proof.
The dove seller threatened to charge for stress to birds.

Maryam said, “Stop.”

The word worked on enough people.

She pointed.

“Children back.
Birds covered.
Coins counted in sight.
Table inspected before blame.”

The money changer said, “Who are you?”

“Someone standing where your table nearly fell.”

Mara whispered, “Best title.”

The table leg was bad.

Not hidden bad.
Bad in the public way that becomes invisible because money passes over it daily and everyone mistakes use for soundness.
One wedge had been added.
Then another.
Then cloth under one foot.
Then a stone.
The leg itself had split near the top.

Daniel said, “This table has been lying longer than the boy has been alive.”

The boy looked offended by usefulness.

The money changer flushed.

“It served.”

“So did the false scale,” Erel said.

Daniel looked at him.

Erel looked both proud and afraid.

Good.

A crowd gathered.

Bad.

Barak appeared as if summoned by public ambiguity.

Worse.

“Yeshua,” he said, using the ordinary name with deliberate weight.
“Will you inspect every table now?”

Daniel looked at the table.

Every old fragment in him lit.

Tables.

Temple.

Doves.

Coins.

Anger.

A whip of cords? Maybe.
He remembered images more than text: overturning, shouting, zeal, cleansing, the house of prayer, den of thieves.
He remembered people admiring righteous anger because it cost them nothing to admire someone else’s danger.

His hand closed around a loose cord.

Mara saw.

“No.”

One word.

Sharp.

He looked at her.

She looked at the cord.

He released it.

Good.

Maryam stepped beside him.

“What is broken first?”

Daniel breathed.

“The table.”

“What else?”

“The rates.
The crowd.
The coin count.
The assumption that a bad table is an accident if profitable.”

“What else?”

He looked at the doves.

“The birds.”

Mara said, “Finally.”

Yosef inspected the leg while the crowd grumbled.
Naama, present for routes, asked pilgrims what they had been charged.
Natan, under strict supervision, wrote numbers and names.
Ruth stood near the boy and dared anyone to accuse without witness.
Erel held the cracked leg and did not speak unless asked.

The table was removed from use.

This caused more outrage than the near collapse.

“It is only one table,” the money changer said.

Azriel, who had become useful around fair weights, said, “Then one table can rest.”

Other tables were inspected.

Not all.
Enough.

Too many were bad.

Some materially.
Some economically.
Some both.

By afternoon, Daniel stood among tables and understood that the old story had offered him a shortcut: anger as structure.
A dramatic act that would tell future readers what side he was on.

He wanted it.

He wanted to flip every table, scatter coins, free doves, shout so loudly no one could pretend not to hear.

He also saw the children who would be trampled, the poor who would lose disputed coins, the sellers who were exploiters and also workers, the officials waiting for a public disorder charge, the women who would carry the mess, and the way anger could become spectacle faster than justice became repair.

He did not overturn a table.

He ordered an inspection.

This was less satisfying.

It may have been braver.

Or cowardly.

He did not know.

By evening, the public correction had a shape:

No exchange without visible rates.

No table without inspected legs.

No cage placed where crowd pressure can crush.

No accusation without counting in sight.

No sacred price hidden behind hurry.

No man handling offerings if his measure lies while empty.

Barak hated it.

Pedaiah, an official who preferred order to embarrassment, tolerated it because the first fallen table had nearly injured people and because several rates proved indefensible under witness.

Some sellers changed nothing the next day.

Some changed enough to avoid inspection.

Some cursed Yeshua’s name.

Some thanked him privately and publicly denied doing so.

That night, Daniel washed coin dust from his hands.

Mara sat nearby.

“You wanted to throw.”

“Yes.”

“Why not?”

“Because the table was not the only thing that would fall.”

She considered.

“Good.”

“Maybe.”

She pointed at him.

“No.
Good.
But do not become proud of restraint either.
Men worship both anger and restraint if you give them faces.”

He groaned.

She smiled.

The table leg went into the chest.

Its label:

Anger may reveal.
It may also trample.

Ruth added:

Inspect before praising either.
