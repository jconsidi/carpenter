# Chapter 3 — The Work of Hands

Daniel learned to crawl toward danger before he learned to crawl well.

This was not, in his opinion, a character flaw.
It was a practical response to his environment.
The world insisted on placing interesting failures just beyond his reach.

A loose peg under a bench.

A cracked water jar set too near the edge of a shelf.

A cord stretched across the floor at exactly the height required to remove an adult’s foot from under them.

A lamp placed where a sleeve could kiss the flame.

A basket handle beginning to separate from its wrapping.

A stool that had been repaired by someone who believed symmetry was a suggestion and gravity a rumor.

These things called to him.

Not literally.
He had not lost that much ground.
But wrongness had a sound even when it made none.
A wrong object stood differently in the room.
It leaned into the future.
It made promises the materials could not keep.

Daniel saw those promises.

His body, unfortunately, saw dust.

He crawled with the grim determination of a man crossing a job site in bad weather, but his hands were still soft paddles, his knees unreliable, and his stomach far too close to the ground.
He moved as if each limb had been subcontracted separately and none of them had read the plans.

Maryam watched this with the calm horror of someone who had discovered that her child did not merely explore the room.
He inspected it.

“No,” she said.

Daniel froze.

The cracked jar sat three feet away.
Two feet eight inches, perhaps.
He could reach it if she looked away.

“No,” Maryam repeated.

He looked at her.

She looked at him.

There was no anger in her face.
That made it worse.
Anger could be negotiated with.
Calm meant policy.

Daniel slapped one hand against the floor and resumed crawling.

Maryam picked him up.

He objected at once.

She shifted him to her hip and carried him away from the jar, the shelf, the entire promising field of preventable loss.

“You are not helpful,” she told him.

This was unjust.

He had been almost helpful many times.

He had prevented the lamp incident, possibly.
He had alerted them to the stool.
He had made enough noise near the door one morning that Yosef had checked the threshold and found a strip of wood split along the grain.
Admittedly, Daniel had been crying because he wanted to go outside, but the outcome had still been safety-related.

Results mattered.

Maryam set him on a woven mat with a carved scrap of wood.

The scrap was meant to entertain him.
It was smooth, safe, and useless.

Daniel discarded it.

Maryam put it back in front of him.

He pushed it away.

She raised an eyebrow.

He considered explaining that his objection was not to the object’s craftsmanship, which was acceptable, but to its lack of consequence.
Since his available vocabulary consisted of several sounds, two half-words, and a noise he used for goats, he settled for staring meaningfully at the cracked jar.

Maryam followed his gaze.

“No.”

Daniel struck the mat with both hands.

The old woman who had been with them since his birth laughed from near the doorway.

“He argues without words.”

Maryam picked up the wooden scrap and placed it in Daniel’s lap again.

“He argues more with them,” she said.

Daniel had words now.

Not many, and not always when he wanted them.
They arrived unevenly.
Some were sounds he understood before he could make them.
Some were meanings that crowded his mouth and emerged as wreckage.
His tongue did not belong to him yet.
His jaw had opinions.
His lips betrayed him.

In his head he could say: *That jar has a radial crack propagating from the handle attachment and will fail when lifted full.*

Out loud he said, “Bad.”

Maryam stilled.

The old woman stopped laughing.

Daniel looked from one to the other.

He had said words before.
Small ones.
Food.
More.
No.
Light.
A near approximation of Yosef that made the man look both pleased and wounded.
But this word had come out clean.

Bad.

It was not even the right word.
It was the word a child would use.
Still, the intention had survived the journey.

Maryam turned toward the jar.

“Bad?” she asked.

Daniel pointed.

This was harder than it looked.
His finger had only recently become a tool rather than a decorative extremity.
He aimed it with full concentration.

“Bad,” he said again.

Maryam did not move toward the jar.
She looked at the old woman.

The old woman crossed the room, lifted the jar, and frowned.

“It has held.”

That sentence again.

Daniel disliked all its ancestors.

“Bad,” he insisted.

The old woman turned the jar in the light.
The crack showed only when the handle was pulled.
She pulled.
The line opened faintly.

“Hm.”

That was all.
Hm.
The most powerful word in every trade.
Hm meant the world had become less certain.

Maryam took the jar from her and set it aside, empty.

Daniel allowed himself a small, satisfied bounce.

The old woman looked at him for a long time.

Maryam did not.

Maryam looked at the jar.

Then she looked at Daniel.

“Bad,” he said, because the matter seemed settled and he appreciated clear minutes when they appeared.

Maryam came back to the mat, knelt, and touched his cheek with the backs of her fingers.

“No,” she said softly.
“Not everything you see must be said at once.”

This was the kind of sentence mothers said when they were right in ways that were not useful.

Daniel scowled.

She smiled.

“Yes,” she said.
“Like that.”

---

Yosef’s workshop was not a shop.

Daniel learned this after months of being carried, placed, retrieved, redirected, and occasionally allowed to sit near the doorway where the light was good and the chips could not quite reach his mouth.

In his old life, a workshop had been a garage, a rented bay, a trailer full of tools, a job box, a table saw, a miter saw, clamps in insufficient numbers, chargers breeding in the corner, pencil marks on every horizontal surface, and one person who swore the missing speed square had been there a minute ago.

Yosef’s workshop was wherever the work gathered.

Sometimes it was the shaded space beside a wall.
Sometimes a courtyard.
Sometimes the room itself after Maryam had moved anything she cared about out of range.
Sometimes a neighbor’s doorway.
Sometimes a roof.
Sometimes the side of a road, if a cart gave up in a sufficiently public way.

Tools traveled because the work did.

Daniel disapproved at first.

A shop should have stations.
A shop should have storage.
A shop should have a bench that stayed where it was put.
A shop should have a place for sharp things and another place for children, ideally separated by law, distance, and common sense.

Yosef had a chest.

The chest was good.

Daniel loved the chest.

He loved it with the clean, unreasonable devotion of a soul recognizing civilization.

It was not large.
It was not fancy.
The lid bore old scars, and one corner had been repaired with a different wood that had aged to a darker brown.
The hinges were leather, replaced more than once.
The latch was simple but clever, a sliding piece that could be opened with one hand if the hand knew it and defeated by most children, goats, and fools.

Daniel began planning to defeat it immediately.

Inside, there were tools.

Not enough tools.
Never enough.
But real ones.

A saw with teeth that had been sharpened by hand and not equally.
A bow drill.
Chisels, narrower than he wanted but well kept.
A wooden mallet, darkened where Yosef’s hand held it.
Awls.
A square that was not square enough until Daniel saw Yosef test it by flipping it against a board and marking the error with no surprise.
A plumb line.
Cordage.
Wedges.
Pegs sorted by size in little bundles.
A plane that did not look like the planes Daniel knew, but whose mouth and sole he understood immediately.

The first time Yosef opened the chest within Daniel’s sight, Daniel went silent.

Maryam noticed.

“He has seen treasure,” she said.

Yosef followed Daniel’s gaze and, for once, did not laugh.

“Yes,” he said.
“He has.”

From that day, Daniel’s life acquired purpose.

The chest had to be opened.

This was not childish curiosity.
It was professional necessity.

He studied the latch from every angle permitted to him, which were mostly bad angles.
He waited until adults were distracted.
He crawled.
Later he toddled.
He fell.
He waited.
He tried again.

The latch defeated him for an entire season, or what felt like one.
Time remained unstable, but his legs improved.

At last, on a morning when Maryam was kneading dough and Yosef was outside speaking to a neighbor, Daniel reached the chest, braced one hand against the lid, lifted the sliding piece, and opened it.

The smell rose like memory.

Wood shavings.
Oil.
Iron.
Leather.
Dust.
Hands.

Daniel leaned over the opening.

He had no intention of touching the chisels.
He was not an idiot.
He had once seen a grown apprentice slice his palm open after saying, “It’s not that sharp,” another sentence Daniel had learned to hate.

He only wanted to look.

He wanted the arrangement.
The logic.
The history of use.
Which tools Yosef placed nearest the top.
Which edges were protected.
Which repairs had been made carefully and which in haste.
How scarce iron changed storage.
How the chest itself distributed value.

He was reaching toward the plane when a shadow fell over him.

Daniel froze.

Yosef stood in the doorway.

Neither of them moved.

Maryam, from behind Daniel, said, “I told you he would.”

This was betrayal.

Yosef came inside and crouched.

Daniel considered retreat.
His legs were not yet reliable enough to make the attempt dignified.

Yosef looked at the open chest, then at Daniel’s hand, then at the latch.

“You opened it.”

Daniel said nothing.

“Did you touch the edges?”

Daniel shook his head.

This was new.
He had learned the gesture from Maryam and used it rarely because adults liked to overinterpret success.

Yosef’s eyebrows lifted.

“No?”

Daniel shook his head again.

Yosef reached into the chest, took out one chisel, and held it up.

“Sharp.”

Daniel nodded.

Yosef looked at Maryam.

Maryam had stopped kneading.

“Do not encourage him,” she said.

“I am not.”

“You are thinking.”

Yosef set the chisel back in its place.

“He opened the chest.”

“He is small.”

“He watched the latch.”

“He watches everything.”

Yosef closed the lid but did not slide the latch.

He sat on the floor opposite Daniel.

“If you open it,” he said slowly, “you call me.”

Daniel understood only part of the sentence.
The important part came through in tone and hand: open, call, me.

He pointed at the chest.

“Mine?” he asked.

Maryam made a strangled sound.

Yosef smiled despite trying not to.

“No.”

Daniel considered this answer predictable but disappointing.

Yosef tapped the lid.

“Mine.”

Daniel pointed at Yosef.

“Mine.”

Yosef laughed then, fully enough that the sound startled Daniel.

“No,” Maryam said from behind them.
“Also no.”

Daniel looked from one to the other and realized he had accidentally made a joke.

This pleased him less than it should have, because the joke was based on linguistic incompetence, not wit.

Yosef opened the chest again.

He took out a scrap of wood, a blunt peg, and the mallet.

Not the good mallet.
A smaller one.
Older.
Its head had split once and been bound tight.
A child could lift it if both child and mallet were patient.

Yosef set the peg in a hole bored in the scrap.

He tapped it.

Once.

Twice.

The peg seated deeper.

He handed the mallet to Daniel.

Maryam said, “Yosef.”

“One tap,” Yosef said.

Daniel took the mallet.

It was heavier than expected.
Everything was.
His hands wrapped badly around the handle.
The balance was different from modern tools but not strange.
The tool wanted a downward arc.
His body wanted to fall over.

He lifted.

The mallet wavered.

Yosef’s hand hovered near his elbow but did not touch.

Daniel brought the mallet down.

He missed.

The blow struck the scrap beside the peg with a dull, unimpressive sound.

Daniel flushed hot with fury.

Yosef did not laugh.

That mattered.

Again, Yosef gestured.

Daniel lifted the mallet.
Slower.
Too slow.
He adjusted his grip, frowned at his own wrist, and tried to remember that the body was not defective, only new.

The mallet came down.

This time it struck the peg.

Badly.

But the peg moved.

A little.

A fraction.

Enough.

Daniel looked at it.

Yosef looked at Daniel.

Maryam looked at Yosef as if this were his fault, which it entirely was.

“Again?” Yosef asked.

Daniel did not know the word, but the meaning stood there plainly between them.

He lifted the mallet.

By the sixth strike, his arms were trembling.

By the ninth, the peg was seated.

By the tenth, Yosef took the mallet away before Daniel could hit the peg simply because the motion had become possible.

Daniel objected.

Yosef shook his head.

“Done.”

Daniel pointed to the peg.

“Good,” he said.

Yosef picked up the scrap and examined it with exaggerated seriousness.

“Crooked,” he said.

Daniel stared.

Yosef turned the piece so he could see.
The peg had indeed seated at a slight angle, following the imperfect first hit.

Daniel felt betrayed by physics.

“Good enough,” Maryam said.

Yosef looked at her.

Then at Daniel.

“No,” he said.
Not harshly.
Not mockingly.
Simply.

Daniel liked him more in that moment than was safe.

“No,” he agreed.

Maryam sighed.

“Both of you,” she said, and returned to the dough.

---

Daniel’s first clear memory of humiliation as a child of this world involved a board.

It was not even a good board.

That was part of the insult.

The board was narrow, twisted, and dark along one edge from old damp.
Yosef had set it aside with other salvage.
Daniel, now large enough to walk several steps before gravity remembered him, had been permitted to carry small scraps from one pile to another.

This was presented as work.

It was not work.
It was relocation therapy.

Daniel understood the distinction and resented it.

Yosef and another man were discussing a door.
The door belonged to someone Daniel did not know, which meant it belonged to the category of community objects that entered Yosef’s life with urgency and left with payment delayed.
It had swollen, dragged, and refused to close.

Daniel listened while pretending to move scraps.

The other man wanted the bottom shaved.

Yosef knelt by the door and ran his hand along the frame.

“Not first,” he said.

The man objected.
The bottom was where it stuck.
Therefore the bottom was the problem.
This logic had been discovered independently by clients in every century.

Yosef checked the hinge side.
He checked the threshold.
He looked at the wall beside the frame.
He set the plumb line.
He waited until the cord stilled.

The frame had shifted.

Of course it had.

Daniel saw it then: not much, but enough.
The door was not the only thing moving in the world.
The whole opening had leaned into the season.
Shaving the bottom would help today and make tomorrow worse.

Yosef explained this.

The other man did not like it.

Daniel, overcome by professional solidarity, toddled forward holding the bad board.

“Frame,” he said.

Both men looked at him.

Yosef’s face went still.

The other man laughed.

“What did he say?”

Yosef did not answer quickly enough.

Daniel pointed, first at the door, then at the side post.

“Bad,” he said.
This word remained useful.
He had improved its aim.

The man laughed again, louder.

“The child fixes doors now?”

Daniel was offended less by the mockery than by its technical inaccuracy.
He had not claimed to fix the door.
He had identified the diagnostic category.

Yosef stood.

“He sees it.”

The other man looked from Yosef to Daniel and back again.

“He sees what?”

“The frame.”

The man snorted.

“He hears you speak and repeats.”

This was plausible, unfortunately.

Daniel opened his mouth to clarify that he had understood the frame before Yosef explained it, then remembered he was perhaps two years old and could not argue causality.

He held up the bad board instead.

The gesture made no sense.

Both men stared.

Daniel, cornered by his own body, struck the board against the side of the doorway to indicate alignment, deflection, season, settlement, and the difference between cause and symptom.

The board split.

Not dramatically.
Just enough to pinch his finger.

Pain arrived white and total.

Daniel screamed.

The other man laughed until Yosef turned on him.

Then he stopped.

Maryam came from the house before Daniel had finished inhaling for the second scream.
She took him from Yosef, inspected the finger, and gave both adult men a look of comprehensive judgment.

“He was helping,” Yosef said.

“He is bleeding,” Maryam said.

“Only a little.”

“He is bleeding because you let him help.”

Daniel, through tears, tried to say that the injury resulted from a low-grade board used outside its structural purpose, not from the broader concept of child participation.

What emerged was, “Bad wood.”

Maryam closed her eyes.

Yosef turned away.

The other man stared.

Then, because the world was cruel, everyone laughed.

Daniel did not forgive them for some time.

He did, however, observe from Maryam’s arms as Yosef repaired the frame instead of shaving the door.

That helped.

---

Modern knowledge failed him in ways large and small.

This became the central annoyance of his early childhood.

He knew things.
He knew many things.
He knew about vapor barriers and capillary breaks and engineered lumber and fastener schedules and shear walls and adhesives and flashing tape and how water always won.
He knew spans and loads and drainage and how to read a crack.
He knew that standardized dimensions were not merely convenient but civilizational.
He knew that a story pole could save hours.
He knew that jigs turned skill into repeatability.
He knew that the right tool made work safer and faster and less dependent on the mood of the person holding it.

Unfortunately, he did not have the right tools.

Or the materials.

Or the language.

Or the social authority.

Or adult hands.

This last deficiency remained the most offensive.

He once spent an entire afternoon trying to show Yosef that a simple marking guide would make repeated cuts easier.

The idea was sound.

The execution involved Daniel dragging three sticks into a row, arranging pebbles, babbling, knocking one stick over, becoming furious, and finally biting the middle stick.

Yosef watched all this with increasing concern.

Maryam said, “Perhaps he is teething.”

Daniel was not teething.

Well, he was teething.

But that was not the point.

Another time, he attempted to demonstrate better storage for pegs by sorting them into piles by length and diameter.
The categorization was excellent.
The piles were clear.
The improvement to workflow would have been obvious to any reasonable adult.

A goat ate one pile.

Daniel learned several new words that day from Yosef, though Maryam later insisted he forget them.

He did not.

A third time, Daniel objected to the way a neighbor stored timber directly on damp ground.
He had no vocabulary for moisture migration, rot, insects, or preventable waste.
He therefore lay down beside the boards and screamed every time someone tried to move him.

The neighbor concluded Daniel disliked the smell.

Yosef, to his credit, eventually lifted one end of the timber and looked underneath.

His expression changed.

He said nothing.
Not in front of the neighbor.

Later, with only Maryam nearby, he set two stones under the boards to lift them clear of the ground.

Daniel gave him a solemn nod.

Yosef gave him one back.

Maryam saw.

“Do not make him worse,” she said.

“He is already worse,” Yosef replied.

---

The trade was not what Daniel had expected.

In his old mind, the word carpenter had carried wood with it: studs, joists, rafters, trim, cabinets, sawdust, ladders, levels, the clean snap of a chalk line.
Even when the work was miserable, the category had been stable.

Here, the category dissolved.

Yosef repaired a plow handle in the morning, fit a door by noon, patched a roof edge before evening, and spent the next day shaping a yoke.
He worked wood when there was wood, stone when there was stone, mud when mud was what the world had provided, rope when rope could solve what timber could not, and negotiation when materials failed entirely.

He measured with cord, eye, hand, habit, and memory.

Daniel distrusted this.

Then he watched it work.

Not perfectly.
Not always.
But more often than his arrogance had allowed.

A modern job site could hide ignorance behind products.
Foam, membranes, brackets, rated hangers, manufactured beams, specifications, inspections, warranties, labels.
A person could build badly with good materials and have the building forgive him for years.

Yosef had fewer layers between judgment and consequence.

If he misread the wood, the joint failed.

If he wasted iron, someone went without.

If he cut too much, there was no delivery tomorrow to replace the piece.

If he offended the wrong client, payment vanished.

If he accepted the wrong job, the family lost days they could not recover.

Skill, Daniel realized, was not simply knowing how an object ought to be made.

Skill was knowing what to do when ought was too expensive.

This annoyed him because it sounded like wisdom, and wisdom was harder to feel superior to.

Yosef’s tools were not crude.

They were limited.

This distinction took Daniel a long time to respect.

A saw that wandered because the teeth were uneven was not evidence that Yosef did not know straightness.
It was evidence that metal was precious, sharpening took time, and the person paying for the work did not always pay for the time required to make every unseen line beautiful.

A joint that Daniel would have rejected on a fine cabinet might be perfectly honorable on a goat pen.

A reused beam with scars could still carry a roof if read correctly.

A wedge was not always a sin.

He struggled with that one.

Wedges had a moral ambiguity he found personally offensive.

Yosef used them without shame.

“Temporary,” Daniel said one day, pointing at a wedge under a support.

He had meant it as accusation.

Yosef looked at the wedge, then at him.

“Yes.”

Daniel waited.

Yosef went back to work.

Daniel tugged at his sleeve.

“Temporary,” he repeated.

Yosef set down the tool in his hand.

“Yes,” he said again.
“Until later.”

Daniel frowned.

“Later” was where temporary things became permanent.
Everyone knew that.
Later was a graveyard for intention.
Later was where flashing was forgotten, inspections were missed, and someone discovered five years afterward that water had been entering the wall the entire time.

“Now,” Daniel said.

Yosef studied him.

Then he pointed around the room.

“Tooth,” he said, touching Daniel’s cheek where a molar had been making life unpleasant.
“Bread.” He pointed toward the covered dough.
“Door.” Toward the frame he had promised to repair.
“Roof.” Upward.
“Maryam.” Toward the courtyard, where she was speaking with another woman.
Then he pointed at the wedge.
“Later.”

Daniel understood perhaps half the words and all of the lesson.

Everything could not be first.

This was outrageous.

It was also true.

Yosef picked up the tool again.

Daniel sat beside the wedge and watched it with suspicion until he fell asleep.

When he woke, the support had been repaired properly.

He looked at Yosef.

Yosef did not look back, but his mouth moved as if he knew he was being judged.

---

Maryam’s work was harder for Daniel to see at first.

This was another failure of his old assumptions.

Yosef’s work made objects.
Objects stayed still long enough to be inspected.
Maryam’s work moved through the day and disappeared inside survival.

Food appeared, but only because she had measured scarcity against hunger.
Cloth was clean, but only because she had turned water, labor, sun, and time into cleanliness.
Visitors were received or discouraged.
Obligations remembered.
Borrowed things returned before resentment hardened.
News gathered.
Risks interpreted.
Daniel kept alive.

The household stood because Maryam kept rebuilding it out of attention.

Once Daniel learned to follow her, the complexity staggered him.

She knew which neighbor could be asked for oil and which could not be allowed to think she had been asked.
She knew when Yosef had been underpaid before he said so.
She knew which women entered with help and which with questions disguised as help.
She knew when to speak of Daniel’s oddness and when to turn the subject toward bread.

She carried a map of invisible loads.

Daniel understood visible loads better.

He began to suspect that this made him less intelligent than he had assumed.

One morning, a woman came with a child whose cough sounded wrong.

Daniel was sitting beside a basket of pegs, forbidden to put them in his mouth, which made them almost impossibly interesting.
The coughing child stood near the doorway, thin shoulders jerking under a patched cloth.
The mother spoke quickly.
Maryam listened.

Daniel heard only fragments.

Night.

Breath.

Heat.

Bad air.

No money.

Maryam’s face changed.

Not dramatically.
Maryam rarely did anything dramatically.
But her attention sharpened.

She brought water.
She touched the child’s forehead.
She looked toward the sleeping area described by the mother’s gestures, then toward the brazier, then toward the door.

Daniel followed the same line a moment later.

Ventilation.

Smoke.

Closed room.

Damp bedding.

Crowding.

He knew this.
Not as a doctor.
He was not a doctor, and one of the few consolations of infancy was that he could not be expected to pretend.
But he knew buildings that made people sick.
He knew mold, smoke, damp, and air that had nowhere to go.

He made a sound.

Maryam glanced at him.

He pointed upward, then toward the door, then coughed deliberately.

The fake cough was unconvincing and made him actually cough, which ruined the demonstration.

Maryam watched him.

Then she turned back to the woman and began asking questions.

That evening, Yosef went with the woman’s husband to look at their room.

The next day, he returned with smoke in his hair and annoyance in every line of his shoulders.

“Bad?” Daniel asked.

Yosef looked at him.

“Yes.”

Maryam did not ask Daniel how he had known.

She only put a hand briefly on his head.

The child did not become well at once.
That mattered.
Daniel watched for improvement with an anxiety he could not explain.
Some days the cough sounded better.
Some days worse.
The world did not reward correct diagnosis with immediate resolution.
Poverty was not a puzzle box that opened because someone noticed the trick.

Still, bedding was moved.
Smoke was vented differently.
A gap was opened high where air could leave.
The child’s mother came back twice to speak with Maryam.

The second time, the child came too.

Still coughing.

But standing straighter.

Daniel felt relief so strong it embarrassed him.

The mother touched his head before leaving.

He disliked that.
He disliked the gratitude in it more.

He had not healed anyone.

He had noticed a room.

There was a difference.

He needed that difference to remain visible.

---

By the time Daniel could walk without falling more often than not, he had developed three reputations.

Among neighbors, he was the child with old eyes.

Among visiting children, he was strange and not much fun because he cared too much about objects they wanted to climb.

Among goats, he was an enemy.

This last reputation was deserved.

The goat’s name, if it had one, was unknown to Daniel.
He refused to give it one.
Names created attachment, and the goat had chosen war.

It chewed rope.

It leaned against things.

It inserted its head into spaces not designed by any reasonable God or carpenter to receive a goat’s head, then complained of confinement.

It once ate part of a carefully sorted bundle of pegs and looked at Daniel while doing it.

Daniel held grudges across lifetimes.

The goat escaped one afternoon while Yosef was away and Maryam was occupied with bread.
It got into the workspace and began investigating a half-finished yoke.

Daniel saw the disaster forming.

The yoke was set on two supports.
One support was stable.
The other, a temporary stack of stones, would hold if undisturbed by people with the moral sense of stones.
It would not hold if struck sideways by a goat pursuing a strip of bark.

Daniel shouted.

The goat ignored him.

Daniel shouted again, using the goat-noise he had once produced accidentally and never forgiven himself for.

The goat looked up.

This was worse.

The strip of bark hung from the yoke.
The goat stepped forward.
Its shoulder brushed the unstable support.
The stones shifted.

Daniel moved.

He did not think.
Thinking would have produced a better plan, such as calling Maryam.
Instead, he ran with the new, unreliable gait of a small child whose head still believed itself the largest and most important part of the body.

The yoke slid.

Daniel reached it at the same time as the goat.

He grabbed the hanging bark and pulled.

The goat pulled back.

The support collapsed.

The yoke dropped.

Not far.
Not with deadly force.
But enough.

Daniel shoved the goat’s head away with both hands.
The yoke struck his shoulder instead.

Pain.

Not catastrophic.
Not the beam.
Not even close.

But pain with weight behind it.

He fell, screamed, and then screamed harder because Maryam screamed too.

Yosef came running from somewhere Daniel had not known he was.
Perhaps he had returned.
Perhaps fathers emerged from walls when needed.
He lifted the yoke, then Daniel, then turned him with hands that shook only after they had checked for blood, swelling, angle, breath.

Maryam arrived and took Daniel before Yosef had finished deciding whether to let go.

For several minutes, the room was entirely sound.

Daniel cried because it hurt.

Maryam cried because it might have been worse.

Yosef did not cry.
He became quiet in a way Daniel had not heard before.

Later, when Daniel sat wrapped against Maryam with his shoulder throbbing and his pride in worse condition, Yosef set the fallen support back up.

Then he knocked it down with his own hand.

Once.

Twice.

Three times.

The stones scattered.

He stared at them.

Daniel watched him.

Yosef gathered the stones and carried them outside.

When he returned, he did not look at Daniel.

“Temporary,” Daniel said, very softly.

Yosef stopped.

Maryam’s hand tightened around Daniel.

Yosef closed his eyes.

“Yes,” he said.

The word sounded like it had cut him.

Daniel wished immediately that he had not said it.

Yosef came to sit beside them.

For a while, nobody spoke.

Then Yosef said, “You saw.”

Daniel leaned against Maryam and said nothing.

“You moved.”

Daniel looked at the floor.

Yosef’s voice roughened.

“Do not.”

Daniel looked up.

Yosef was not angry.
That was the frightening part.

“Call,” Yosef said.
He touched his own chest.
“Call me.
Call her.
Do not put your body under what falls.”

Daniel remembered rain.

A beam.

The apprentice’s hoodie in his fist.

The load path searching.

His throat closed.

Yosef had no way to know what he had said.

Maryam, watching Daniel’s face, went very still.

“Do not,” Yosef repeated.

Daniel wanted to promise.

He could not.

The shape of his life, whatever it was, had already shown a preference for falling things.

He reached out with his good hand.

Yosef took it carefully.

Their fingers did not fit together properly.
Daniel’s were too small.
Yosef’s were rough and warm.

“I will build it stronger,” Yosef said.

Daniel shook his head.

Both adults froze.

He had no words for what he meant.
Not fully.
But he tried.

“No,” he said.
“Teach.”

Yosef’s expression changed.

Maryam looked from one to the other.

Daniel pointed, with enormous effort, at the fallen place where the temporary support had been.
Then at Yosef’s hands.
Then at his own chest.

“Teach.”

The room held the word.

Outside, the goat made a noise that suggested it had no regrets.

Yosef let out a breath.

“You are too small.”

Daniel scowled.

Maryam, to his surprise, said, “He will not be smaller tomorrow.”

Yosef looked at her.

She looked back.

The argument between them had no words, which made it efficient.
Daniel saw fear on both sides.
Also fatigue.
Also recognition.
The world was full of things that could fall.
Keeping him away from all of them would be its own kind of lie.

Yosef rubbed one hand over his face.

“Not tools,” he said.

Maryam’s gaze sharpened.

“Not blades,” he clarified.

“Not height,” she said.

“Not weight.”

“Not fire.”

“Not goats,” Daniel said.

They both looked at him.

Then Maryam laughed.

Yosef did too, though his sounded pained.

“Especially not goats,” he said.

---

The first lesson was not how to cut.

This disappointed Daniel.

Nor was it how to measure.
Nor how to drill, shape, join, lash, wedge, fit, trim, brace, or repair anything that could plausibly be called important.

The first lesson was sweeping.

Daniel considered this beneath him for approximately three seconds.

Then Yosef placed the broom in his hands and showed him how to move shavings without stirring dust into the air, how to keep sharp slivers away from bare feet, how to collect scraps worth saving separately from waste, how to notice which chips came from which wood, and how the floor after work told the story of what had been done.

Sweeping, Daniel conceded privately, contained more information than expected.

The second lesson was carrying.

Not heavy things.
Not useful things, at first.
Small scraps.
Empty baskets.
Peg bundles.
Cord.
A wedge.
One wedge, once, and Daniel carried it with such visible suspicion that Yosef took it back.

Carrying taught distance, sequence, and the cost of poor placement.

A tool set down badly was a future delay.

A peg bundle left near damp was waste.

A scrap too small for one job might be perfect for another if not burned too soon.

The third lesson was watching.

This was insulting because Daniel already watched.

Yosef disagreed.

“You look for wrong,” he said.

Daniel, sitting beside him in the shade, frowned.

Yosef pointed to a board across his knees.

“Look for wood.”

Daniel looked.

The board was olive wood, or something like it.
Hard, irregular, not at all the clean, obedient lumber of his old life.
The grain curved.
One side twisted slightly.
A knot near the end disrupted everything around it.

“Bad,” Daniel said.

Yosef shook his head.

He ran a finger along the grain.

“Here,” he said.

Daniel followed the line.

Yosef turned the board.

The curve that had seemed like waste became, from another angle, a natural handle.
The knot, if avoided, left a stronger sweep of wood beside it.
The twist meant the piece would never be a straight rail.
It could be something else.

Daniel felt the lesson arrive and resisted it.

Yosef smiled faintly, as if he could see the resistance.

“Not bad,” he said.
“This.”

Daniel hated how much was contained in that.

In his old world, industrial materials arrived with promises: grade, dimension, specification, allowable span.
You selected according to need.
If the material did not fit, you replaced it.

Here, replacement was not a habit.
It was an expense, sometimes an impossibility, often an insult to the labor already spent getting the piece into your hands.

A builder did not impose the idea first and then punish the material for failing it.

A builder listened.

Daniel had always believed he listened to buildings.

It had not occurred to him that wood might object to being treated as a product.

Yosef set the board in Daniel’s lap.
It was heavy enough that Daniel had to lean back.

“What?” Yosef asked.

Daniel looked at the grain.

At the knot.

At the curve.

At the possible handle hidden inside the refusal to be straight.

“Not bad,” Daniel said slowly.

Yosef waited.

Daniel touched the curved place.

“This.”

Yosef nodded.

It was the smallest possible approval.

Daniel kept it for days.

---

That night, Maryam found him awake.

This was not unusual.
Sleep still came and went like a bad worker: late, early, absent when needed, present when inconvenient.

The room was quiet.
Yosef slept near the door, one arm folded under his head.
The better beam overhead held its place in shadow.
Outside, something moved softly—animal, person, wind.
Daniel could distinguish more sounds now but not always more meanings.

Maryam lay beside him, propped slightly on one elbow.

“You were pleased today,” she whispered.

Daniel did not answer.

“You think I did not see.”

He considered pretending to sleep.
His body, unreliable in so many things, chose that moment to blink with full alertness.

Maryam smiled.

“He is patient with you,” she said.

Daniel knew she meant Yosef.

He nodded.

Maryam’s face changed.

“You know that?”

He had nodded too quickly.
Too knowingly.

He froze.

Maryam did not call out.
She did not wake Yosef.
She did not cross herself, or whatever the gesture would be here, because he had no idea.
She only studied him in the dim light with that inconvenient attention.

Daniel held very still.

Finally she said, “You do not have to be only what you were before.”

The words struck him so hard that for a moment he forgot to breathe.

Before.

Had she said before? Or had his mind placed that word into the gap between language and understanding? The sentence was simple.
The meaning that reached him was not.

Maryam touched his forehead.

“You are here,” she whispered.

He wanted to tell her she was wrong in every important way.
He was not from here.
He did not belong to the room, the town, the century, the name.
He was Daniel.
He was Linda and Robert’s son.
He was a man who had died under a beam.
He was an error wearing a child’s body.

Maryam’s hand remained on his forehead.

“You are here,” she said again.

This time it sounded less like comfort and more like instruction.

Daniel looked past her to the beam.

It held.

Not because it was perfect.

Because someone had known what to ask of it.

He closed his eyes.

In the morning, Yosef gave him a scrap of curved wood and showed him where the handle was hiding.

Daniel did not understand all the words.

He understood the hands.

For now, that had to be enough.
